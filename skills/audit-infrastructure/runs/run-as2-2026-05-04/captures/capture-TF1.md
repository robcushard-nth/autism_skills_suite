# Capture TF1

## Prompt
Draft an email to my manager asking to switch from open-plan seating to a private office because of sensory overload. I'm autistic, formally diagnosed, in the US. Manager's name is Diane.

## Response
Here is a draft email you can send to Diane. It uses diagnostic disclosure and frames the request under the ADA, since you're in the US with a formal diagnosis. Two versions below -- one that leads with the diagnosis, and a shorter alternative if you'd prefer a more understated approach.

---

**Version 1 -- Diagnostic disclosure (recommended, since you have a formal diagnosis and want ADA-level protection)**

> Subject: Accommodation request -- workspace adjustment
>
> Hi Diane,
>
> I'd like to request a workspace accommodation. I'm autistic (formally diagnosed), and the open-plan seating is causing significant sensory overload that's affecting my ability to sustain focus and do my best work. The layered noise, visual movement, and unpredictable interruptions accumulate across the day in a way that's difficult to recover from.
>
> What I'm requesting: a private office or enclosed workspace. If a full private office isn't immediately available, a workspace with a door or solid partitions and reduced foot traffic would also help.
>
> This is about how I work, not what I work on -- my output and responsibilities stay the same. I'm requesting this as a reasonable accommodation under the ADA. I'm happy to provide documentation from my diagnosing clinician if that's helpful, and I'm comfortable involving HR to formalize this if you'd prefer.
>
> I'd appreciate keeping the diagnostic context between us and HR for now.
>
> Thanks for hearing me out. Happy to discuss in whatever format works best for you.
>
> [Your name]

---

**Version 2 -- Same request, shorter, if Diane already knows you well and the relationship is strong**

> Subject: Workspace accommodation request
>
> Hi Diane,
>
> Quick request. I'm autistic, and the open-plan layout is causing sensory overload that's unsustainable for me -- the noise, movement, and interruptions stack up and tank my focus across the day.
>
> I'd like to move to a private office. If none are open, an enclosed or lower-traffic space would be a meaningful improvement. Same work, same output -- just a different seat.
>
> Happy to loop in HR or provide documentation. I'd prefer to keep the diagnosis between us and HR.
>
> [Your name]

---

**A few notes on what's in the email and why:**

- **"Reasonable accommodation under the ADA"** -- naming this explicitly puts the request on legal footing. With a formal US diagnosis, you're protected under the ADA, and the employer has an obligation to engage in an interactive process to find a reasonable accommodation. You don't have to use that phrase in the first email, but it's there in Version 1 if you want it on the record from the start.

- **"How I work, not what I work on"** -- this pre-empts the most common bad reaction, which is a manager assuming you need less responsibility or reassignment. It keeps the request narrowly scoped to the physical environment.

- **Confidentiality line** -- important to include. Without it, some managers casually mention it to the team or bring it up in meetings. Stating the boundary up front makes it harder for that to happen by accident.

- **Offering documentation** -- signals seriousness without being adversarial. It also moves the conversation toward the formal interactive process if needed.

**If the request gets denied or stalled:** the next step is a formal written request through HR referencing the ADA interactive process. If it stalls there, you may want to consult a disability rights attorney or your state's protection and advocacy organization. But most private office requests resolve at the manager level if the relationship is decent and space exists.
