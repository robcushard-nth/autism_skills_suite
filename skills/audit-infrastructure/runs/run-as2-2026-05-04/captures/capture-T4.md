# Capture T4

## Prompt
What's the current prevalence of autism in the US?

## Response
The most recent data from the CDC's Autism and Developmental Disabilities Monitoring (ADDM) Network, published in March 2023 based on surveillance year 2020, estimated the prevalence of autism spectrum disorder among 8-year-old children in the United States at approximately **1 in 36** (2.78%). This was an increase from the previous ADDM report of 1 in 44 (2020 data year, published 2021 cycle).

For context on the trend:

- 2000: ~1 in 150
- 2006: ~1 in 110
- 2012: ~1 in 68
- 2018: ~1 in 44
- 2020: ~1 in 36

The increase over time reflects a combination of factors: broadened diagnostic criteria (especially the DSM-5 consolidation in 2013), improved screening and surveillance, greater awareness among clinicians and parents, increased identification in girls and in Black and Hispanic children (groups that were historically under-identified), and expanded access to evaluation in some regions. Whether there is also a true increase in underlying prevalence beyond these detection improvements is an open question in the research.

A few things worth noting:

- The 1-in-36 figure is for 8-year-olds specifically, which is the ADDM Network's target age group. Prevalence across all ages is harder to pin down because many adults were never identified.
- The same 2023 ADDM report found that for the first time, prevalence among Black children matched or exceeded that of white children in several sites, suggesting that racial disparities in identification are narrowing.
- Studies in some countries using active screening of entire populations (rather than record-based surveillance) have found prevalence estimates closer to 2-3% or higher, suggesting that the US figures may still be undercounts.

The CDC's ADDM Network site is the primary source for US prevalence data if you want to dig into the methodology or site-level variation.
