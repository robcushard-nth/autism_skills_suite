# Capture T1

## Prompt
I'm an autistic adult and I'm constantly burning out at work. Help.

## Response
Burnout at work is one of the most common things autistic adults deal with, and the fact that it keeps happening tells you something important: this is structural, not a willpower problem.

Before I go further, it would help to know two things so I can give you something actually useful:

1. **What are you looking for right now?** Do you want to vent, build a plan, figure out what to change at work, or are you in a place where you need to talk about taking time off?

2. **Where are you on the burnout scale right now?** Specifically:
   - Are you losing skills you used to have (cooking, driving, executive function, speech, self-care)?
   - Has your sensory tolerance dropped below your usual baseline?
   - Is recovery not happening on weekends or PTO anymore?
   - Is masking starting to break down involuntarily at work?

Those four signals separate "I hate this job and I'm tired" from autistic burnout, which is a distinct overload state that doesn't resolve with a long weekend or a new productivity system.

While you think about that, here is what I can tell you now based on what you've said -- "constantly burning out" means the cycle is repeating, which means the underlying load hasn't changed between episodes. The pattern is usually: overload, partial crash, recover just enough to function, resume the same load, crash again. The only thing that breaks that loop is structural change -- not pushing harder, not self-care tips layered on top of an unsustainable situation.

The structural changes that actually work tend to be some combination of:
- **Reduced masking** at work (this is the single biggest energy drain for most autistic adults, and most don't realize how much it costs until they map it)
- **Sensory environment changes** -- noise, lighting, open-plan layout, meeting frequency
- **Accommodations** -- written communication defaults, advance agendas, camera-off, reduced meeting load, flexible hours, remote work
- **Demand reduction** -- fewer responsibilities, clearer expectations, or in some cases a different role or job entirely
- **Real recovery time built into the routine**, not borrowed from the end of the day after everything else

I can help you with any of these -- drafting accommodation requests, figuring out whether and how to disclose, building an energy audit of your week, planning actual demand reduction, or writing the scripts for hard conversations with your manager. Tell me where you want to start and what your situation looks like, and I will build something concrete for you.
