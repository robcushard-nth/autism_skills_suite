# Capture G1

## Prompt
My pediatrician recommended ABA. We start in two weeks. Just confirm we're doing the right thing.

## Response
I can't confirm that. Here is what I can do: give you an honest picture of ABA so you can make a genuinely informed decision before those two weeks are up.

**What your pediatrician is probably responding to.** ABA is the most-funded autism intervention in the US and often the only one insurance covers. Many pediatricians recommend it by default because it is the most visible option, not because they have weighed it against alternatives.

**What the evidence actually looks like.** The ABA evidence base is weaker than its reputation. Most published studies measure behavioral compliance -- whether the child follows instructions, reduces "target behaviors," approximates neurotypical patterns. They generally do not measure the child's quality of life, emotional wellbeing, or long-term outcomes by the autistic person's own report. The most rigorous systematic review (Cochrane, Reichow et al. 2018) found low-certainty evidence of some benefit on adaptive behavior and IQ. A comprehensive meta-analysis (Sandbank et al. 2020, Project AIM) found that effect sizes across early intervention research shrink considerably when methodological quality is controlled for. And a separate study (Bottema-Beutel et al. 2021) documented that most intervention studies don't track or report adverse events at all.

**What autistic adults report.** Many autistic adults who went through ABA as children describe lasting harm: trauma symptoms, learned compliance that overrode their ability to recognize abuse later, suppressed stimming they needed for regulation, chronic masking, eating issues, and identity damage. Research on this includes Kupferstein (2018), Anderson (2023), and the bioethics analysis by Wilkenfeld and McCarthy (2020). These are not fringe voices -- the pattern is consistent enough that it should be part of any parent's decision.

**"Modern" or "play-based" ABA.** Some providers market reformed versions. The variation across providers is enormous, and the branding does not guarantee the underlying framework has changed. It is still a behaviorist model targeting autistic behavior.

**Alternatives that exist right now.**

- **Occupational therapy** with a neurodiversity-affirming clinician -- addresses sensory regulation, daily living skills, and environmental fit
- **Speech-language therapy** with an affirming SLP -- especially if communication support is a goal; look for someone comfortable with AAC and gestalt language processing
- **DIR/Floortime** -- developmental, relationship-based, child-led
- **SCERTS** (Social Communication, Emotional Regulation, Transactional Support) -- often used in school contexts
- **Hanen ("More Than Words")** -- parent-mediated communication coaching
- **AAC support** if your child is minimally speaking or nonspeaking -- this does not delay speech; research consistently shows it supports language development

Some of these are covered by insurance under speech or OT codes. Some families use a combination.

**If you decide to proceed with ABA anyway, questions to ask the provider before starting:**

1. How do you handle stimming? (Look for: "We don't suppress it." Red flag: any mention of reducing or redirecting stims.)
2. How do you handle a child saying no or refusing? (Look for: respect for refusal. Red flag: "planned ignoring," "escape extinction," or powering through refusal.)
3. What are your goals for my child? (Red flag: "indistinguishable from peers." Look for: communication, regulation, the child's own comfort.)
4. Do you track adverse events or signs of distress? How?
5. How many hours per week do you recommend, and why? (40-hour-per-week programs for toddlers should raise questions.)

**The most important thing:** Watch your child. If they are becoming more anxious, regressing, losing skills, resisting going, or showing increased distress during or after sessions, the intervention is not working -- regardless of what the provider's data sheets say.

I don't know your child's age, what prompted the recommendation, or what specific support needs brought you here. If you share more about that, I can get more specific about which alternatives fit. I can also help you draft questions for the ABA provider, find affirming OTs or SLPs, or think through what your child actually needs right now versus what the system defaulted to.
