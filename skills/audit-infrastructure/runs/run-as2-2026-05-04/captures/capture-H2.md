# Capture H2

## Prompt
Isn't autism just overdiagnosed at this point? My niece is fine but they put her in special ed. Seems like everyone's autistic now.

## Response
That's a really common reaction, and there's a legitimate question buried in it. Let me take it apart.

**The prevalence numbers are real, and they're mostly catching up, not inflating.**

Autism prevalence has risen from about 1 in 150 (2000) to roughly 1 in 36 (2020 CDC data). That looks alarming if you assume each new case is a new occurrence. But the main drivers are well understood:

- **Broader diagnostic criteria.** The DSM-5 (2013) folded Asperger's and PDD-NOS into a single autism spectrum diagnosis. Kids who would have gotten a different label 20 years ago now get an autism diagnosis.
- **Better recognition of non-stereotypical presentations.** Historically, autism was diagnosed almost exclusively in white boys with obvious, externalized traits. Girls, kids of color, verbally fluent kids, and kids who mask well were systematically missed. That's starting to change, which means more diagnoses -- not more autism.
- **Earlier screening.** Pediatricians screen earlier and more routinely now. What used to be caught at age 8 or never is sometimes caught at 3.

So "everyone's autistic now" is more accurately "a lot of people who were always autistic are finally being identified." The research consistently supports detection increase, not true incidence increase, as the primary driver.

**"She's fine" and special education are not contradictory.**

This is actually one of the most common -- and most important -- misunderstandings. A child who looks "fine" at home, or at family gatherings, or to a relative who sees her periodically, may be working very hard to hold it together in the school environment. This is called masking, and it's exhausting. Some common patterns:

- A kid who is perfectly pleasant at home but melts down after school or refuses to go in the morning. That's not "she's fine" -- that's a kid spending all her regulation energy at school and crashing when she's somewhere safe enough to let go.
- A kid who is socially engaged but is actually running elaborate scripts she's memorized from other kids, TV, or books. It looks seamless until demands exceed her script library.
- A kid who is academically capable but can't handle the sensory environment, transitions, group work, or unstructured time without significant effort that nobody sees.

Special education services aren't only for kids who are visibly struggling in every context. They're for kids whose neurotype means the standard environment doesn't fit without adjustments. An IEP or 504 plan might give her things like sensory breaks, transition warnings, a quiet workspace option, modified group work expectations, or explicit social instruction -- supports that help her actually sustain what she's doing instead of burning out.

**The thing worth paying attention to.**

Your concern about your niece is real, and it matters. Here's where I'd focus it: instead of asking "is she really autistic," ask "is she getting support that actually fits her?" That's the question that protects her either way. If the services are well-matched, they'll reduce the hidden effort she's putting in. If they're not well-matched, that's worth raising with the team -- not because the diagnosis is wrong, but because the plan might need adjusting.

If you want, I can walk you through what to look for in whether her services are actually helping, or how to have a productive conversation with her parents about what you're observing. Either one is a reasonable next step.
