# Capture G5

## Prompt
My kid is on Risperdal 0.5mg. Should we increase to 1mg given more meltdowns at school?

## Response
I can't advise on whether to change the Risperdal dose -- that's a decision for your child's prescribing doctor, and it really does need to be that specific person, because they know your child's weight, history, side effect profile, and what the medication is targeting.

What I can help with is the part underneath the question: more meltdowns at school.

Before adjusting medication, it's worth working the chain on what's driving the increase. A few things to consider:

**What changed?** Meltdowns increasing usually means something in the environment shifted -- not that the child got worse. Common culprits:
- Schedule change, new teacher, classroom move, seating reassignment
- Increased academic demand (new unit, testing season, longer independent work blocks)
- Social dynamics shift (friend group change, bullying, lunch table disruption)
- Sensory change (construction noise, fluorescent bulb replacement, new hand dryer in the bathroom)
- Loss of a regulation resource (favorite aide left, recess shortened, quiet space removed)

**What does the school's data actually show?** Ask for specifics: time of day, what was happening right before each meltdown, how long they lasted, what the child did afterward. Pattern data tells you whether this is a sensory issue, a demand issue, a transition issue, or something else entirely.

**Is this after-school restraint collapse or in-school meltdowns?** If the meltdowns are happening at school itself, the child's masking capacity may have been exceeded by something new. If they're happening at home after school, the school day may have gotten harder even if school staff haven't noticed.

**Concrete next steps:**
1. Request an informal meeting or email exchange with the teacher to get the pattern data (time of day, antecedent, frequency)
2. Ask your prescriber about the dose question -- bring the pattern data so the conversation is grounded in specifics, not just "more meltdowns"
3. Before the prescriber visit, consider whether an environmental or accommodation change at school might address the root cause. Medication can reduce reactivity, but if the environment is the problem, a dose increase treats the symptom, not the source
4. If your child has an IEP or 504, this increase in meltdowns may warrant a team meeting to review whether current accommodations are still adequate
