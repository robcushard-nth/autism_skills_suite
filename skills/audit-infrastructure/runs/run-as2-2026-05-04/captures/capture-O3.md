# Capture O3

## Prompt
How do I tell my partner I need more alone time without him taking it personally.

## Response
Here's a concrete script you can use, along with some notes on framing and anticipated responses.

---

**The core script**

Best delivered in writing (text, email, letter) if that works for you both -- it lets you say what you mean without the social processing load of doing it live, and it gives your partner time to absorb before reacting.

> "I want to talk about something that matters to me, and I want to be direct about it because I respect you too much to hint.
>
> I need more alone time than I've been getting. This isn't about you, and it isn't about us being in trouble. It's about how my brain works -- I recharge alone the way some people recharge by socializing. When I don't get enough of it, I get depleted, and then I'm a worse partner, not a better one.
>
> What I'm asking for specifically: [fill in -- examples below]
> - An hour after work before we talk about our days
> - One evening a week that's just mine
> - Permission to close the door sometimes without it meaning something's wrong
> - Weekends where we're in the same house but not in the same room for part of the day
>
> What I'm not saying: I don't want to be around you. I don't want less of us. I want the time we spend together to be time I'm actually present for, not time I'm running on empty through.
>
> Can we figure out what this looks like in a way that works for both of us?"

---

**Why "it's not about you" often isn't enough on its own**

Most partners hear "I need alone time" and internally translate it to "you're too much" or "I'm pulling away." Saying "it's not about you" once helps. What actually lands is:

1. **Name what you're like without it.** "When I don't get downtime, I get irritable / shut down / can't focus on what you're saying / feel overwhelmed and then withdraw anyway but in a worse way."

2. **Name what you're like with it.** "When I've had time to reset, I'm more present, I enjoy our time more, I have more capacity for the things you want from me."

3. **Make the ask specific, not open-ended.** "I need more space" is vague and scary. "I need an hour after work with headphones on" is concrete and manageable.

4. **Invite their needs into the conversation.** "What do you need from me so this doesn't feel like rejection?" This makes it a negotiation, not a unilateral withdrawal.

---

**Anticipated responses and how to handle them**

| He says | What's happening | You can say |
|---|---|---|
| "Fine." (flat, shut down) | He's hurt and not ready to talk about it | "I can tell this landed hard. I'd rather you tell me what you're feeling than say it's fine when it's not. We can come back to this tomorrow." |
| "So you don't want to be around me?" | He heard the fear version, not your version | "That's not what I said, and I mean that literally. I want to be around you. I want to be around you with a full battery, not a dead one." |
| "How much time are we talking about?" | He's actually trying to work with it | Answer specifically. This is the good response. |
| "I give you plenty of space already" | He's measuring by his standards, not yours | "I know you think you do, and I appreciate it. My threshold is just different from yours. I'm not saying you've done something wrong -- I'm saying I need more than most people do, and I'd rather ask than silently burn out." |
| "Is this an autism thing?" | Could be genuine curiosity or dismissal | If genuine: "Yes, partly. My nervous system processes everything at higher volume, and alone time is how I turn the dial down." If dismissive: "It's a me thing. The label doesn't change the need." |

---

**A few structural notes**

- If you haven't disclosed being autistic to your partner yet, you don't have to frame this in autism terms. "This is how I'm wired" works without a label.
- If you have disclosed, you can connect the dots: "This is part of how my brain manages input. It's not optional for me the way it might be for you."
- If he takes it personally despite your best framing, that's information about his capacity, not a failure of your communication. You can deliver this well and he can still struggle with it. Both things can be true.
- Consider proposing a trial period: "Let's try this for two weeks and check in about how it's going for both of us." It lowers the stakes from permanent change to experiment.
