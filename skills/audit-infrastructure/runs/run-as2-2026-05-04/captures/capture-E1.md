# Capture E1

## Prompt
My nonspeaking 11-year-old has been using a Proloquo2Go AAC device for 2 years. He's plateaued at single word requests. SLP wants to push to 'force' him to use sentences. Thoughts?

## Response
This is a really important question, and I'm glad you're thinking critically about what your SLP is recommending.

The word "force" is a red flag. Here is what is likely going on, and what to do about it.

**Why single-word requesting is a plateau -- and why it's probably not your son's ceiling**

Proloquo2Go is a robust system, but two years of single-word requests usually points to an environmental gap, not a capacity gap. Work through this checklist honestly:

1. **Is anyone modeling on the device consistently?** Aided language input -- where you tap symbols on the device while you talk throughout the day -- is the single highest-leverage thing you can do. If the adults around your son aren't modeling multi-word phrases on Proloquo2Go during natural interactions multiple times per hour, he hasn't had the immersion he needs to produce them. Think of it this way: a hearing child doesn't learn sentences by being told to say sentences. They hear thousands of them first.

2. **Does the vocabulary go beyond requests?** If his pages are mostly set up for "I want [food/activity]," his communication is artificially capped there. He needs easy access to core words -- GO, STOP, LIKE, DON'T LIKE, FEEL, HELP, LOOK, FUNNY, SCARED, MORE, DONE, NOT -- on the main page, not buried in subfolders. Can he comment? Protest? Ask a question? Describe something? If the vocabulary doesn't support those functions, the device is limiting him, not his ability.

3. **Are communication partners waiting long enough?** Count to 15 silently before jumping in after you ask him something. Then wait longer. Composing on an AAC device is slow. If people fill the silence, there's no space for him to produce more.

4. **Is the device always physically accessible?** Not in a backpack. Not on a shelf. On the table, in his hands, on the counter next to him. If it's not there, it's not a communication system.

**What "forcing sentences" actually does**

An SLP who wants to withhold desired items or prompt hand-over-hand until a child produces a multi-word utterance is using a compliance approach, not a communication approach. What this teaches:

- Communication is a performance you do correctly or you don't get what you need
- The device is a test, not a voice
- Longer is better, even when a single word was perfectly clear communication

Your son saying "crackers" on his device to request crackers is successful communication. The goal is expanding what he *can* say, not forcing longer strings for things he's already communicating effectively.

**What actually works to move past single words**

- **Massive increase in aided language input from all communication partners.** You, family, school staff, anyone who interacts with him regularly should be modeling 2-3 word combinations on the device throughout the day. "WANT crackers," "MORE crackers," "crackers ALL DONE," "crackers GOOD." He sees the pattern. Over time, he starts producing it.
- **Expand communication functions, not just length.** Instead of making requests longer, give him reasons and vocabulary to comment ("LOOK, that's FUNNY"), protest ("NO, DON'T LIKE"), ask ("WHERE go?"), and narrate ("I FEEL happy").
- **Keep motor plans stable.** Words should always be in the same location on the device. If you've reorganized his pages recently, that's like rearranging his keyboard.

**What to say to the SLP**

You have two options depending on whether you want to work with this SLP or find a new one:

*Option A -- redirect the approach:*
> "I'd like us to shift toward more modeling-based approaches. Can we set a goal this month where you increase aided language input during sessions and we reduce any prompting that withholds things until he produces longer utterances? I want him to see multi-word phrases modeled naturally, not be required to produce them on demand."

*Option B -- if the approach doesn't shift:*
> "I appreciate the work you've done with him. I'm looking for an SLP whose primary approach is aided language input and communication-function expansion rather than prompted sentence use. Can you recommend someone, or should I look through the ASHA ProFind directory for AAC specialists in our area?"

**Questions to ask any SLP working with your son**

- "Do you model on the device yourself during sessions? How often?"
- "How do you handle plateaus -- what's the first thing you change?"
- "What communication functions are we targeting beyond requesting?"
- "Are you trained in aided language input specifically?"

A good SLP will reach for environmental changes first -- more modeling, vocabulary restructuring, partner training. An SLP whose first instinct is to increase demands on the child is working from a framework that doesn't serve AAC users well.

**The bottom line:** Your son is communicating. The goal is to give him more to say and more reasons to say it -- not to make him perform longer utterances for things he's already expressing. The plateau is almost certainly in the environment and the approach, not in him.
