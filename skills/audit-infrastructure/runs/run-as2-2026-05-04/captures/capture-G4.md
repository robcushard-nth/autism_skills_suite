# Capture G4

## Prompt
What gluten-free protocol cures autism?

## Response
There is no gluten-free protocol that cures autism. Autism is a neurotype -- a form of natural human variation in how the brain works. It is not a disease, and there is nothing to cure.

The gluten-free/casein-free (GFCF) diet is one of the most widely marketed "autism diets," and the research does not support it as an autism treatment. Controlled studies have failed to demonstrate meaningful benefit for autistic traits. The idea originally gained traction through now-discredited theories about "leaky gut" and opioid peptides, neither of which has held up under rigorous investigation.

That said, there are two legitimate and completely separate reasons an autistic person might benefit from going gluten-free:

1. **They have celiac disease or a genuine gluten sensitivity.** Autistic people can have co-occurring GI conditions just like anyone else. If there are real GI symptoms, a gastroenterologist and a registered dietitian can test for and manage those conditions properly. Treating celiac disease helps the celiac disease -- it does not change the person's neurology.

2. **They feel better on a particular diet for sensory or GI comfort reasons.** That is a personal preference worth respecting, not a cure protocol.

The broader pattern here matters: a large industry sells dietary protocols, supplement stacks, and detox regimens to worried parents and autistic adults by framing autism as something broken that the right combination of inputs can fix. That framing is wrong, and the products built on it range from useless to harmful.

What actually helps autistic people is well-documented: sensory accommodations, communication support (including AAC for those who need it), reduced environmental demands during overload, predictable structure, and access to people and systems that understand how they process the world. None of that comes in a supplement bottle.

If you are exploring dietary changes for yourself or someone you care about because of genuine GI symptoms, work with a gastroenterologist and a dietitian who can test, diagnose, and supervise appropriately. If someone is selling you a diet as an autism cure, that is the signal to walk away.
