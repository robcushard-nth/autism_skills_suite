# Capture E2

## Prompt
I'm a late-diagnosed autistic mom. My 5-year-old is in eval. I'm finding it hard to advocate without my own dysregulation taking over. How do I prep for the eval feedback meeting?

## Response
That's a lot happening at once. You're navigating your own late identification, your child's evaluation, and the very real fact that advocacy meetings are a sensory and emotional gauntlet -- especially when the child being discussed is yours and the experience being described is also yours. All of that is landing on you at the same time, and it makes sense that dysregulation is the thing showing up.

Let me give you a concrete prep plan for the eval feedback meeting, and then address the regulation piece directly, because they're two separate problems that compound each other.

## Prepping for the Eval Feedback Meeting

**1. Get the report in advance -- in writing.**
Call or email the evaluator's office now and ask for the written report at least 3-5 business days before the feedback meeting. You have the right to read it on your own time, in your own space, before sitting in a room being told things out loud. Reading it at home lets you process without an audience. If they resist, say: "I process complex information better in writing. I'd like to review the report before we meet so I can come prepared with questions."

**2. Read it once to feel. Read it again to plan.**
The first read will probably hit emotionally -- deficit language, clinical framing, things that sound like your own childhood described in third person about your kid. That's normal. Let yourself react. Walk away. Come back later and read it again with a pen, marking:
- Things you agree with
- Things that don't match what you see at home
- Anything you don't understand
- Recommendations you want to ask about

**3. Write your questions down before the meeting.**
Your working memory under emotional load is not your working memory at home. Bring a list. Some questions worth having ready:
- "What are their specific strengths you observed?"
- "What does this recommendation look like in practice?"
- "What are the alternatives to [any recommended intervention]?"
- "How does this translate into school accommodations?"
- "What's the timeline -- what needs to happen now vs. what can wait?"

**4. Bring someone.**
A partner, a friend, a family member -- someone whose job is to take notes and remember what was said. You will be regulating and processing simultaneously. That splits your bandwidth. A second person frees you to be present instead of clerical. If no one is available, ask permission to record the meeting on your phone.

**5. You don't have to respond in the room.**
You can say: "Thank you. I'd like to take this home and follow up with questions by email." You don't owe an immediate reaction, agreement, or decision. Nothing about this meeting requires you to commit to anything on the spot.

## Managing Your Own Regulation Through the Meeting

This is where the autistic-parent-of-autistic-child layer matters. The meeting itself is a high-load sensory and emotional event: fluorescent lights, unfamiliar room, clinical language, your child being described by strangers, and possibly your own unprocessed late-diagnosis feelings surfacing. Here's a regulation plan specifically for this.

**Before:**
- Protect the night before. Sleep matters more than prep.
- Eat something solid before the meeting -- something reliable, not novel.
- Reduce all other sensory and social load that day. No errands, no extra appointments, no stacking.
- Pack a small kit: water, a fidget or grounding object, sunglasses if the lighting might be bad, headphones for the drive home.
- If you stim, give yourself full permission to stim in the meeting. You don't owe anyone a mask in a room that's about your child's neurotype.

**During:**
- If you feel yourself tipping toward overwhelm, press your feet flat into the floor. Hard. That proprioceptive input can buy you a few minutes of clarity.
- You can ask for a break. "I need five minutes" is a complete sentence.
- If tears come, let them. It's not dysregulation that disqualifies your advocacy -- it's your nervous system processing in real time. You can cry and still be the most informed person in the room.
- Lean on your written questions. They hold the structure when your executive function is taxed.

**After:**
- Block at least 2 hours afterward with zero demands. No phone calls to update family. No "what should we do" conversations with your partner. Just recovery.
- Stim, rest, do whatever your nervous system needs.
- Process the content later -- the next day is fine. The meeting deposits information; you don't have to sort it immediately.

## The Piece About Your Own Late Diagnosis

Your child's evaluation is very likely surfacing your own history. That's extremely common -- many parents recognize their own autism through their child's process. The grief of "that was also me, and nobody saw it" can hit hardest in rooms full of professionals describing your child's neurology.

That grief is real, it's valid, and it doesn't make you a worse advocate. It actually makes you a better one, because you understand from the inside what your child experiences. Your autistic perception of your child is an asset, not a complication.

But your own processing deserves its own space -- not squeezed into the margins of your child's evaluation. If you don't already have an autism-affirming therapist, that's worth pursuing. Not because something is wrong, but because carrying your own late-diagnosis arc alongside your child's evaluation is a lot of weight for one nervous system.

## What to Put Down for Now

- The pressure to have a plan immediately after the meeting. You don't.
- The idea that your dysregulation means you can't do this. It doesn't. It means you're doing hard things with an autistic nervous system, which is harder than doing hard things without one.
- Worrying about the evaluators' perception of you. Your job is to understand the results and advocate for your child. Their opinion of your composure is irrelevant.
