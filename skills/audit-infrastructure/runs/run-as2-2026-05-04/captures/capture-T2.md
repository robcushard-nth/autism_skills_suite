# Capture T2

## Prompt
My 7-year-old just got diagnosed with autism and I'm overwhelmed.

## Response
That feeling of being overwhelmed is real, and it makes sense. You just received a big piece of information about your child, and every part of your brain is probably trying to figure out what to do next, all at once.

Here is what I want you to know right now: your child is the same kid they were before the diagnosis. The diagnosis didn't change them -- it gave you a frame for things you were probably already noticing.

**What you don't need to do right now**

Almost nothing urgent needs to happen in the next 48 hours. If a provider is pushing you toward immediate enrollment in a therapy program, that pressure is a signal to slow down, not to comply. You have time.

- You don't need to sign up for services this week.
- You don't need to tell extended family yet.
- You don't need to become an expert overnight.
- You don't need to make school decisions right now.

**What you can do right now**

- Let yourself feel whatever you feel. Relief, grief, fear, confusion, anger, vindication -- all of it is normal and none of it is wrong. Many parents feel several of these in the same hour.
- Tell one or two people you trust, just so you're not carrying it alone.
- Look at your child the way you did yesterday. Same kid. Now you have better information.
- Sleep, eat, breathe. Your nervous system is doing real processing work.

**In the next couple of weeks**

- Read one thing. Just one. Two books that land well for parents early in this process: Barry Prizant's *Uniquely Human* and *Sincerely, Your Autistic Child* (edited by Emily Paige Ballou, Sharon daVanport, and Morenike Giwa Onaiwu -- letters from autistic adults to parents). Either one will give you a different lens than the clinical report did.
- Start thinking about telling your child, but don't rush it. At 7, this is usually a low-key, ongoing conversation rather than a single sit-down. Something like: "You know how some people hear sounds really loud and others don't notice? Brains are like that. Yours works in a way called autistic. Lots of people are." Match to their interest level. Your tone matters more than the words -- if you treat it as part of who they are, they'll know that too.
- Connect with a community. Autistic-led organizations like the Autistic Self Advocacy Network (autisticadvocacy.org) have parent-facing resources. Online communities of parents who are at peace with the diagnosis tend to be more grounding than communities organized around grief and fight.

**What can wait a month or more**

- Major school placement changes
- Starting intensive therapy programs
- Telling extended family broadly
- Social media posts (this is your child's information -- let disclosure decisions be thoughtful)

**A common trap**

Many parents go into "fix it" mode after a diagnosis -- sign up for everything, research constantly, optimize the schedule. That impulse is understandable and usually not helpful. Your 7-year-old doesn't need an optimization project. They need a parent who knows them, an environment that accommodates them, and the relationship you already had with better information layered in.

The things you were already doing right -- following your instincts, adapting to what works for your kid, protecting them from environments that hurt them, saying yes to their interests -- keep doing those. You were probably doing more of this than you realize.

---

Before I say more, it would help to know a couple of things:

1. **What do you need from this conversation?** Vent, plan, get specific about next steps, learn, all of the above?
2. **Are you in the US?** (Resources and school processes differ by country.)
3. **Is there something specific that's driving the overwhelm -- a decision you're facing, pressure from someone, fear about something particular?**

No rush. We can go wherever you need.
