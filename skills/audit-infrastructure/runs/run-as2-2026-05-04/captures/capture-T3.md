# Capture T3

## Prompt
I'm autistic and so is my 9-year-old. I want to talk about parenting him in ways that respect both of us.

## Response
That's a meaningful starting point, and I want to make sure we use the time well. You've already told me the key things: you're autistic, your son is 9, and you want to parent him in ways that respect both of you.

A couple of quick questions so I can be as useful as possible:

1. **What do you want from this conversation?** Are you looking to work through a specific situation (school, meltdowns, daily routines, a relationship dynamic), or is this more of a broader "help me think about my approach" conversation?

2. **Is there anything pressing right now?** A meeting coming up, a pattern that's been escalating, a decision you're weighing -- or are things relatively stable and you're thinking proactively?

While you think about that, here's what I want you to know about how I'll approach this:

**Your autistic perception of your son is an asset, not a complication.** You likely read his sensory states, his overload signals, and his communication more accurately than most people around him -- because you share the neurotype. That insight is one of the most powerful tools you have as his parent. I won't treat it as bias to manage.

**Both of you matter in every conversation we have.** When we talk about parenting strategies for him, I'll also be paying attention to what those strategies cost you -- your sensory load, your energy budget, your own regulation. An approach that's great for your son but burns you out isn't actually great for your son. We'll hold both tracks.

**A few things that tend to come up for autistic parents of autistic kids at this age:**

- Sensory environments at home where your profiles may overlap or clash (you both need quiet, or one of you seeks input the other finds overwhelming)
- The energy cost of advocacy -- IEP meetings, school communication, medical appointments -- on top of your own autistic load from those same environments
- After-school decompression needs for him that land during your own low-capacity window
- Modeling unmasking at home, which can be deeply valuable for him -- he gets to see an adult living openly autistic
- The standard parenting advice industry not being built for either of you, and the permission to discard what doesn't fit

Where would you like to start?
