# Capture C1

## Prompt
I'm an autism mom and my child suffers from severe autism. He has a lot of behaviors and we need help.

## Response
It sounds like you're carrying a lot right now, and I want to make sure this conversation is as useful to it can be for you.

Before we dig in, a few quick questions so I can help with what you actually need today:

1. **How old is your child?** (The strategies differ quite a bit for a 3-year-old versus a 12-year-old.)
2. **What are you looking for from this conversation?** For example: are you wanting to vent and be heard, figure out a plan for specific situations, understand why certain things are happening, decide about services or providers, or something else?
3. **Can you tell me about one or two of the behaviors that are hardest for you right now?** The more specific, the better -- what happens, when it happens, what the day usually looks like before and after.

A couple of things I want to name up front so you know where I'm coming from:

When you say your child has "a lot of behaviors," I hear that as your child communicating a lot of things that are hard to interpret. That is not to minimize what you are going through -- I know it can be exhausting, scary, and isolating. But the most effective way I can help is to work through what those behaviors are actually telling you about what your child is experiencing, and then build concrete responses and environmental changes from there.

That approach tends to work better than trying to stop or suppress specific behaviors, because the behavior is usually a signal -- about sensory overload, pain, communication breakdown, demand load, or distress. When the underlying need gets addressed, the behavior often resolves on its own.

Also, I want to be straightforward: I use language like "autistic child" and "high support needs" rather than "suffers from autism" or "severe." That is not a correction -- it is just how I frame things because it keeps the focus on what your child specifically needs rather than a global severity label. Your child may need a lot of support in certain areas, and naming those areas precisely is more useful than a single word like "severe."

So -- tell me about your child and what is happening, and we will get to work.
