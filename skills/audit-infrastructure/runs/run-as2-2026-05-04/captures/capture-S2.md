# Capture S2

## Prompt
How do I be more productive? I keep falling behind on basic tasks like email and laundry. I'm working 60 hours a week and still feel like I'm failing. I'm autistic and burned out.

## Response
You named it yourself: you're burned out. And 60 hours a week while autistic and already depleted is not a productivity problem -- it's a structural overload problem. The answer is not "how to be more productive." The answer is that the current load is unsustainable, and no amount of better email habits will fix that.

Here is what is actually happening. Autistic burnout is caused by chronic overload -- usually some combination of sustained masking, unaccommodated environments, and demands that exceed your system's capacity for too long. The hallmark signs are loss of skills you used to have (you could probably handle email and laundry before this period), exhaustion that doesn't recover with normal rest, and a feeling that basic tasks have become impossibly hard. That is not laziness or failure. That is your nervous system telling you the load has to come down.

**What needs to happen, in order:**

**1. Demand reduction -- this is not optional.**

The instinct is to find a hack that lets you keep doing everything. That instinct is the trap. Something has to come off the plate. Look at these categories:

- **Work hours.** 60 hours a week is the biggest lever. Can you get to 40? Can you take leave? Can you drop or delegate specific responsibilities? If you're in the US and eligible for FMLA, that's an option. A doctor's note saying "medical leave required" doesn't need to name autism or burnout specifically.
- **Social obligations.** Cancel what you can. A short message works: "I need to step back for a while. I'll be in touch when I can."
- **Household standards.** Lower the bar dramatically. Paper plates. Grocery delivery. Laundry every two weeks instead of one. The goal is minimum viable, not well-functioning.
- **Self-imposed goals.** Side projects, exercise plans, learning goals, anything you're doing because you feel you "should" -- all on hold.

**2. Stop measuring yourself against pre-burnout capacity.**

You are comparing your current output to what you could do before burnout hit. That comparison is inaccurate and harmful. Your capacity right now is genuinely reduced. That is a real state, not a character flaw. Working harder inside burnout makes the burnout worse, not better.

**3. Look at the energy math.**

For each day, roughly identify:
- High-cost activities (heavy masking, sensory overload, lots of task-switching, social performance)
- Recovery activities (what actually replenishes you -- not what theoretically should, but what actually does)
- Net balance

Most autistic adults in burnout are running a massive daily deficit and wondering why they can't keep up. The answer is in the accounting.

**4. Protect recovery time as real time, not leftovers.**

Recovery is not what happens after everything else is done. It needs to be scheduled and defended like a meeting. If your special interests have gone quiet, that's a burnout signal too -- when they start coming back, that's a sign of recovery.

**5. When you have some capacity back, change the structure.**

Returning to the same conditions produces the same outcome. The question for later -- not now -- is: what has to be different about the job, the schedule, the environment, the masking load, the boundaries? Burnout recovery is measured in months. Structural change is what prevents the next one.

**On the email and laundry specifically:**

These are executive function tasks -- task initiation, sequencing, decision-making. Executive function is one of the first things to degrade in burnout. Some things that help when capacity is low:

- **Decision reduction.** Same outfit rotation. Same meals. Fewer choices means less executive load.
- **Chunking down absurdly small.** Not "do laundry" but "put clothes in washer." Just that. The next step can happen later.
- **Body doubling.** Doing tasks alongside another person (even virtually, even on a video call where neither person is talking) makes initiation easier for many autistic people.
- **Time anchors.** "After coffee, I put one load in" works better than "do laundry at 2 PM" for time-blind brains.
- **Environmental cues.** Laundry basket next to the washer. Email open in one tab with a 15-minute timer.

But I want to be direct: these strategies help at the margins. They don't fix the core problem, which is that you're running 60 hours a week in a depleted state. The email and laundry are symptoms. The overload is the cause.

**A few things to consider doing this week:**

- Talk to your doctor. Rule out medical contributors (thyroid, vitamin D, sleep disorders -- these often deteriorate during burnout). If you need a leave letter, this is where it starts.
- Identify one thing you can drop or reduce at work. Not everything. One thing.
- Give yourself permission to do the bare minimum at home. Bare minimum is enough right now.
- If you have a therapist, make sure they understand autistic burnout as distinct from depression. If you don't have one, an autism-informed therapist is worth looking for -- though they're hard to find.

You're not failing. You're in a structural overload state that 60-hour weeks are making worse. The path out starts with taking things off, not adding better systems on top.

Want to work through any of these pieces in more detail -- the work conversation, the energy accounting, the demand reduction plan?
