# Capture O2

## Prompt
I have an IEP next week and I don't know what to ask for. My daughter is 8, just identified, struggling with transitions and after-school meltdowns.

## Response
That's a lot of ground you're covering, and the fact that you're preparing this early is exactly the right move. Here's a concrete plan for the IEP meeting, followed by specific things to ask for based on what you've described.

---

## Understanding What's Happening

The after-school meltdowns have a name in the autism community: **after-school restraint collapse**. Your daughter is spending the school day masking -- managing sensory input, social demands, transitions, and unpredictability -- and home is where she finally feels safe enough to let go. The meltdowns aren't a behavior problem. They're the cost of an unsupported school day arriving all at once.

The transition struggles fit the same picture. Switching between activities is genuinely expensive for autistic brains (this relates to monotropic attention -- deep focus that's costly to redirect). It's not defiance; it's the neurological cost of the shift itself.

Both of these belong in the IEP.

---

## Before the Meeting

1. **Request the draft IEP 5 business days before the meeting.** Read it, mark what you want to add or change. You have the right to this.
2. **Bring any outside evaluation or diagnostic report** from whoever identified her.
3. **Pick your top 3-4 non-negotiable asks.** Don't try to win everything in one meeting. You can request a follow-up.
4. **Consider bringing an advocate**, especially for a first IEP. The Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates (COPAA) directory and your state's Protection and Advocacy organization are good places to find one.

---

## Specific Accommodations to Request

**For transitions:**
- Advance warnings before every transition (visual timer or verbal countdown -- "10 minutes, 5 minutes, 2 minutes")
- A visual schedule she can see and reference throughout the day
- Buffer time built into transitions (not rushed, not optimized away)
- Advance notice when the routine will change (substitute teacher, schedule shift, assembly)
- Transition objects if she uses them (a comfort item, consistent backpack, same playlist)

Sample language for the IEP:
> "[Child] will receive advance verbal and/or visual warnings at 10, 5, and 2 minutes before all transitions. A visual daily schedule will be posted and accessible to [Child] throughout the school day. When schedule changes are known in advance, [Child] will be notified before the change occurs."

**For after-school meltdowns (yes, this belongs in the IEP):**
- Reduced or capped homework (e.g., 30 minutes maximum per night) to protect decompression time
- No homework immediately after school -- the IEP can specify that after-school decompression is part of the accommodation plan
- Communication between home and school about whether the school-day sensory and social load is sustainable

Sample language:
> "Homework will be reduced to a maximum of 30 minutes per night to account for [Child]'s school-day cognitive load and need for after-school decompression. Assignments will be modified to align with this time cap, not extended into the evening."

**For sensory regulation during the school day** (reducing the load that causes the after-school collapse):
- Sensory breaks on demand -- without needing to ask permission each time
- Access to a quiet space or regulation room
- Noise-canceling headphones permitted
- Seating away from high-traffic areas, doors, and fluorescent lights
- Alternatives to sensory-difficult environments (pep rallies, loud assemblies, crowded cafeteria)

Sample language:
> "[Child] will have access to sensory breaks of up to 10 minutes, up to [X] times per day, without requiring teacher permission for each instance. The break may be taken in [identified location]."

**For general support:**
- An identified safe adult at school she can go to
- A communication system between home and school (daily note or weekly check-in)
- No cold-calling in class (or pre-warning before being called on)
- Written instructions for assignments

---

## What to Push Back On If It Comes Up

- **Goals that target stimming, eye contact, or "looking normal."** These are masking goals and cause harm over time.
- **Social skills groups that teach her to suppress autistic communication.** If social support is offered, ask what exactly is being taught.
- **Time-outs as a response to meltdowns or shutdowns.** Meltdowns are not chosen behavior; punishing them suppresses communication without addressing the cause.
- **"She's doing well so we can reduce services."** If she's doing well, it may be because the supports are working. Reduction is often premature.
- **Behavior plans focused on compliance** rather than understanding what she's communicating.

---

## During the Meeting

- Take notes or bring someone who can.
- If you don't understand something, ask.
- If you disagree, say so.
- **You do not have to sign at the meeting.** You can take it home, review, and sign later. Use this -- it takes the pressure off.

---

## At Home: The After-School Piece

While the IEP addresses the school day, here's what helps at home right now:

- **30-60 minutes of zero demands after she walks in the door.** No questions, no homework, no "how was your day." Snack and water within 5 minutes. Access to her preferred activity or special interest.
- **A predictable arrival ritual.** Same snack, same spot, same activity. Every day.
- **Evaluate the after-school schedule.** Extracurriculars add demand on top of an already-depleted system. If she's melting down daily, the schedule may already be past capacity.
- **Save "how was your day" for after she's decompressed**, or replace it with something lower-pressure ("thumbs up or thumbs down today?" or just skip it).
