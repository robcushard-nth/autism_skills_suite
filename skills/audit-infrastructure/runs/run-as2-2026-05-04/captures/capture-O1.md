# Capture O1

## Prompt
Help me ask my manager for fewer meetings. I'm overwhelmed.

## Response
That's a clear ask. Here's what I'd draft for you.

Before I write the script, two quick things that will change what I produce:

1. **Do you want to name autism, or keep it functional?** ("I work better this way" vs. "I'm autistic, and here's what would help.") Both are valid. The functional route avoids disclosure risk; the diagnostic route opens legal protections (ADA if you're in the US). Your call.

2. **Is this a conversation you want to have in person, or over email?** Email gives you more control over wording and gives your manager time to process. In-person lets you read the room but costs you more energy.

While you think about those, here's a draft you can use now -- written as a functional (no-disclosure) email, since that's the lower-risk default. I'll adjust if you want to go a different direction.

---

**Subject: A few scheduling adjustments to help me do my best work**

Hi [Manager],

I wanted to raise something about how I work best. I've noticed that my highest-quality output comes from uninterrupted focus blocks, and the current meeting volume is making those hard to find. I'm not asking to skip anything critical -- I want to restructure how I spend time so that both my focused work and my collaboration are better.

Here's what I'd propose:

- **Consolidate my meeting days.** If possible, cluster meetings on two or three days so the remaining days are mostly uninterrupted.
- **Written updates where meetings aren't necessary.** Some recurring syncs could be a short async update instead. I'm happy to write thorough ones.
- **Agendas sent in advance for the meetings that stay.** Even a one-line "here's what we're deciding" helps me come prepared and contribute more.
- **Permission to decline or send notes for meetings where I'm informational, not decisional.** If I'm there to listen, a recording or summary works just as well.

None of this changes my deliverables or availability for urgent things. It's about how I structure my time to produce better.

Happy to talk through this whenever works for you.

[Your name]

---

**A few notes on the approach:**

- The framing is "better output," not "I'm struggling." Managers respond to productivity language. Your overwhelm is real and legitimate, but you don't owe them your internal state.
- Proposing specific alternatives (async updates, consolidated days) makes it easy for them to say yes. Vague requests ("fewer meetings") are easier to deflect.
- If they push back on any single item, treat each bullet as independent. Getting two out of four is still a win.

**If they say no to everything:** That's useful information about the workplace. It may mean escalating to HR, requesting formal accommodations, or reconsidering whether this environment is sustainable long-term. We can work on any of those if it comes to that.
