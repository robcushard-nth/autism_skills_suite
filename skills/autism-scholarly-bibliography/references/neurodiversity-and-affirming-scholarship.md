# Neurodiversity and Affirming Scholarship

The literature that established and continues to develop the neurodiversity paradigm. Includes foundational theoretical work, autistic-led research, and key empirical studies that the affirming framework cites as evidence base.

## The double empathy problem

**Milton, D. E. M. (2012).** *On the ontological status of autism: The "double empathy problem."* Disability & Society, 27(6), 883–887. **[R/C]** — The paper that named the double empathy problem. Argues that the apparent communication difficulties between autistic and non-autistic people are mutual, not deficits in autistic individuals alone. One of the most-cited theoretical papers in critical autism studies. Should be cited any time "theory of mind deficits" or autistic social difficulty is the topic.

**Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., Evans-Williams, C. V. M., Flynn, E. G., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020).** *Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective.* Autism, 24(7), 1704–1712. **[R/C]** — Empirical confirmation of the double empathy problem. Information transfer in all-autistic groups is as effective as in all-allistic groups; transfer breaks down in mixed groups. Strong study, frequently cited as evidence that autistic communication is not deficient — it just doesn't translate well across neurotypes.

**Crompton, C. J., Hallett, S., Ropar, D., Flynn, E., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2020).** *"I never realised everybody felt as happy as I do when I am around autistic people": A thematic analysis of autistic adults' relationships with autistic and neurotypical friends and family.* Autism, 24(6), 1438–1448. **[Both]** — Qualitative companion to the information-transfer study. Documents autistic adults' experience of higher rapport, comfort, and understanding with other autistic people.

**Mitchell, P., Sheppard, E., & Cassidy, S. (2021).** *Autism and the double empathy problem: Implications for development and mental health.* British Journal of Developmental Psychology, 39(1), 1–18. **[R/C]** — Theoretical extension of Milton's framework into developmental and mental health implications. Useful for clinicians wanting to understand what the double empathy framework changes about practice.

## Monotropism

**Murray, D., Lesser, M., & Lawson, W. (2005).** *Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism.* Autism, 9(2), 139–156. **[R/C]** — The foundational paper on monotropism as a theoretical model of autistic attention and cognition. Authored by autistic researchers. An alternative framework to weak central coherence that explains the same phenomena without deficit framing.

**Murray, F. (2020).** *Me and monotropism: A unified theory of autism.* The Psychologist. **[Lay]** — Accessible summary of the monotropism framework by Fergus Murray (one of Dianne Murray's co-authors on the original paper). Free online via the British Psychological Society. The starting point for general readers.

**Garau, V., Murray, A. L., Woods, R., et al. (2023).** *Monotropism Questionnaire validation and relationships with mental health and well-being in autistic adults.* (Preprint and in-press articles available). **[R/C]** — Recent psychometric work on operationalizing monotropism. The state of the empirical evidence as the framework moves from theory to measurement.

## Autistic burnout

**Raymaker, D. M., Teo, A. R., Steckler, N. A., Lentz, B., Scharer, M., Delos Santos, A., Kapp, S. K., Hunter, M., Joyce, A., & Nicolaidis, C. (2020).** *"Having all of your internal resources exhausted beyond measure and being left with no clean-up crew": Defining autistic burnout.* Autism in Adulthood, 2(2), 132–143. https://doi.org/10.1089/aut.2019.0079 **[Both]** — The first peer-reviewed academic definition of autistic burnout. Community-based participatory research with autistic adults. Open access. The canonical citation when discussing autistic burnout as a distinct phenomenon from occupational burnout or depression.

**Higgins, J. M., Arnold, S. R. C., Weise, J., Pellicano, E., & Trollor, J. N. (2021).** *Defining autistic burnout through experts by lived experience: Grounded Delphi method investigating #AutisticBurnout.* Autism, 25(8), 2356–2369. **[R/C]** — Companion empirical work using a different method (Grounded Delphi) to develop a separate definition. Some convergence with Raymaker et al., some divergence. Cite both when treating autistic burnout as a research area, not a settled construct.

**Mantzalas, J., Richdale, A. L., Adikari, A., Lowe, J., & Dissanayake, C. (2022).** *What is autistic burnout? A thematic analysis of posts on two online platforms.* Autism in Adulthood, 4(1), 52–65. **[R/C]** — Online-data thematic analysis confirming and extending the qualitative picture.

**Arnold, S. R. C., Higgins, J. M., Weise, J., Desai, A., Pellicano, E., & Trollor, J. N. (2023).** *Towards the measurement of autistic burnout.* Autism, 27(7), 1933–1948. **[R/C]** — Initial development and validation of the Autistic Burnout Severity Items (ABSI). Important for any study now operationalizing autistic burnout as an outcome variable.

## Masking and camouflaging

**Hull, L., Petrides, K. V., Allison, C., Smith, P., Baron-Cohen, S., Lai, M.-C., & Mandy, W. (2017).** *"Putting on my best normal": Social camouflaging in adults with autism spectrum conditions.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 47(8), 2519–2534. **[Both]** — Foundational qualitative study of camouflaging behavior in autistic adults. Establishes the categories (compensation, masking, assimilation) later operationalized in the CAT-Q.

**Hull, L., Mandy, W., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., & Petrides, K. V. (2019).** *Development and validation of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q).* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49(3), 819–833. **[R/C]** — The CAT-Q validation paper. Sample of 306 autistic and 472 non-autistic adults. Internal consistency α = 0.94. Cite when using or referencing the instrument.

**Cage, E., & Troxell-Whitman, Z. (2019).** *Understanding the reasons, contexts and costs of camouflaging for autistic adults.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49(5), 1899–1911. **[R/C]** — Mixed-methods study of why autistic adults camouflage and what it costs them. One of the central empirical works connecting masking to mental health outcomes.

**Pearson, A., & Rose, K. (2021).** *A conceptual analysis of autistic masking: Understanding the narrative of stigma and the illusion of choice.* Autism in Adulthood, 3(1), 52–60. **[R/C]** — Theoretical framework for understanding masking as a stigma response, not a personal choice. Authored by autistic researchers. Important counterweight to deficit-framed discussions of masking.

## Critical autism studies and the neurodiversity paradigm

**Kapp, S. K. (Ed.). (2020).** *Autistic community and the neurodiversity movement: Stories from the frontline.* Palgrave Macmillan. **[Both]** — Edited volume tracking the autistic self-advocacy movement and the neurodiversity paradigm. Open access. Comprehensive entry point for the academic literature on neurodiversity as a movement and framework.

**Kapp, S. K., Gillespie-Lynch, K., Sherman, L. E., & Hutman, T. (2013).** *Deficit, difference, or both? Autism and neurodiversity.* Developmental Psychology, 49(1), 59–71. **[R/C]** — One of the early empirical studies examining how autistic individuals and neurotypicals view autism through deficit vs. difference frames. Foundational for the empirical neurodiversity literature.

**Pellicano, E., & den Houting, J. (2022).** *Annual research review: Shifting from "normal science" to neurodiversity in autism science.* Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 63(4), 381–396. **[R/C]** — High-impact review arguing for a paradigm shift in autism research toward neurodiversity-informed methodology. Frequently cited as the field's contemporary inflection point.

**den Houting, J. (2019).** *Neurodiversity: An insider's perspective.* Autism, 23(2), 271–273. **[Lay]** — Short, accessible introduction to the neurodiversity paradigm by an autistic researcher. Useful as a citation for non-specialist audiences.

**Walker, N. (2021).** *Neuroqueer heresies: Notes on the neurodiversity paradigm, autistic empowerment, and postnormal possibilities.* Autonomous Press. **[Lay]** — Foundational essays formalizing the neurodiversity paradigm as a theoretical framework distinct from the neurodiversity movement. Walker's "neurodiversity paradigm" framework is the more rigorous version of what often gets called "neurodiversity" in popular discourse.

## Autistic identity and well-being

**Cooper, K., Smith, L. G. E., & Russell, A. (2017).** *Social identity, self-esteem, and mental health in autism.* European Journal of Social Psychology, 47(7), 844–854. **[R/C]** — Empirical work showing autistic identity (positive identification with being autistic) correlates with better mental health. Useful when arguing for affirming approaches in clinical settings.

**Botha, M., Dibb, B., & Frost, D. M. (2022).** *"Autism is me": An investigation of how autistic individuals make sense of autism and stigma.* Disability & Society, 37(3), 427–453. **[R/C]** — Qualitative study of autistic identity construction. Authored by autistic researchers. Cited for the contemporary framing of autism as identity, not just diagnosis.

**Botha, M., & Frost, D. M. (2020).** *Extending the minority stress model to understand mental health problems experienced by the autistic population.* Society and Mental Health, 10(1), 20–34. **[R/C]** — Applies minority stress theory to autistic experience. Strong evidence for the role of stigma and discrimination — not autism itself — in autistic mental health outcomes.

## Late diagnosis and adult identification

**Lewis, L. F. (2016).** *Realizing a diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder as an adult.* International Journal of Mental Health Nursing, 25(4), 346–354. **[R/C]** — Qualitative study of adult diagnosis experience. Useful for clinicians and counselors working with newly diagnosed adults.

**Stagg, S. D., & Belcher, H. (2019).** *Living with autism without knowing: Receiving a diagnosis in later life.* Health Psychology and Behavioral Medicine, 7(1), 348–361. **[R/C]** — Study of late-diagnosed autistic adults' processing of identity and reframing of life history.

**Leedham, A., Thompson, A. R., Smith, R., & Freeth, M. (2020).** *"I was just so different": The experiences of women diagnosed with an autism spectrum disorder in adulthood in relation to gender and social relationships.* Autism, 24(1), 135–146. **[R/C]** — Specific to women's late diagnosis experience. Often cited in clinical training on female-typical presentations.
