# Co-occurring Conditions and Mental Health Research

The literature on what typically travels with autism — anxiety, depression, suicidality, trauma, eating differences, GI conditions, hypermobility, and the major comorbidity research.

## The major comorbidity reviews

**Lai, M.-C., Kassee, C., Besney, R., Bonato, S., Hull, L., Mandy, W., Szatmari, P., & Ameis, S. H. (2019).** *Prevalence of co-occurring mental health diagnoses in the autism population: A systematic review and meta-analysis.* The Lancet Psychiatry, 6(10), 819–829. **[R/C]** — The canonical citation. Roughly 70%+ of autistic individuals have at least one co-occurring mental health condition. Specific rates: anxiety ~20%, depression ~11%, ADHD ~28%, OCD ~9%, schizophrenia spectrum ~4%, bipolar ~5%. Useful as a single overall comorbidity citation.

**Hossain, M. M., Khan, N., Sultana, A., Ma, P., McKyer, E. L. J., Ahmed, H. U., & Purohit, N. (2020).** *Prevalence of comorbid psychiatric disorders among people with autism spectrum disorder: An umbrella review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses.* Psychiatry Research, 287, 112922. **[R/C]** — Umbrella review for the most comprehensive picture of the comorbidity landscape.

**Mannion, A., & Leader, G. (2013).** *Comorbidity in autism spectrum disorder: A literature review.* Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders, 7(12), 1595–1616. **[R/C]** — Earlier comprehensive review, useful when historical context is needed for trajectory of the comorbidity literature.

## Anxiety

**van Steensel, F. J., Bögels, S. M., & Perrin, S. (2011).** *Anxiety disorders in children and adolescents with autistic spectrum disorders: A meta-analysis.* Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review, 14(3), 302–317. **[R/C]** — Foundational meta-analysis of anxiety in autistic children and adolescents. Roughly 40% meet criteria for at least one anxiety disorder.

**White, S. W., Oswald, D., Ollendick, T., & Scahill, L. (2009).** *Anxiety in children and adolescents with autism spectrum disorders.* Clinical Psychology Review, 29(3), 216–229. **[R/C]** — Earlier review, clinically focused, cited frequently in treatment literature.

**Wood, J. J., & Gadow, K. D. (2010).** *Exploring the nature and function of anxiety in youth with autism spectrum disorders.* Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice, 17(4), 281–292. **[R/C]** — Theoretical paper on the unique features of autism-related anxiety vs. typical anxiety presentations. Important for clinicians who try to apply standard CBT protocols.

## Depression

**Hudson, C. C., Hall, L., & Harkness, K. L. (2019).** *Prevalence of depressive disorders in individuals with autism spectrum disorder: A meta-analysis.* Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology, 47(1), 165–175. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis of depression in autism. Lifetime prevalence around 14%; current prevalence around 11%. Higher rates in adults than children.

**Cassidy, S., Bradley, P., Robinson, J., Allison, C., McHugh, M., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2014).** *Suicidal ideation and suicide plans or attempts in adults with Asperger's syndrome attending a specialist diagnostic clinic: A clinical cohort study.* The Lancet Psychiatry, 1(2), 142–147. **[R/C]** — Foundational study showing 66% of newly-diagnosed adults reported lifetime suicidal ideation; 35% reported planning or attempting suicide. Established autism as a major suicide risk population.

## Suicidality

**Cassidy, S., Bradley, L., Shaw, R., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2018).** *Risk markers for suicidality in autistic adults.* Molecular Autism, 9(1), 42. **[R/C]** — Specific risk factors in autistic adults. Camouflaging, autistic burnout, and unmet support needs as predictors.

**Hirvikoski, T., Mittendorfer-Rutz, E., Boman, M., Larsson, H., Lichtenstein, P., & Bölte, S. (2016).** *Premature mortality in autism spectrum disorder.* British Journal of Psychiatry, 208(3), 232–238. **[R/C]** — Swedish population study documenting elevated mortality in autistic individuals, with suicide as a major contributor. Frequently cited in policy and advocacy contexts.

**Cassidy, S. A., Gould, K., Townsend, E., Pelton, M., Robertson, A. E., & Rodgers, J. (2020).** *Is camouflaging autistic traits associated with suicidal thoughts and behaviours? Expanding the interpersonal psychological theory of suicide in an undergraduate student sample.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 50(10), 3638–3648. **[R/C]** — Establishes the masking-suicidality link empirically. Important for the clinical literature on why masking matters beyond comfort.

**South, M., Costa, A. P., & McMorris, C. (2021).** *Death by suicide among people with autism: Beyond zebrafish.* JAMA Network Open, 4(1), e2034018. **[R/C]** — Editorial calling for autism-specific suicide research and intervention. Useful framing citation.

## Trauma and PTSD

**Rumball, F., Happé, F., & Grey, N. (2020).** *Experience of trauma and PTSD symptoms in autistic adults: Risk of PTSD development following DSM-5 and non-DSM-5 traumatic life events.* Autism Research, 13(12), 2122–2132. **[R/C]** — Important for documenting that autistic adults experience PTSD from events that don't meet DSM-5 trauma criteria — bullying, social exclusion, sensory overwhelm, restraint experiences. Reframes what counts as traumatic for autistic populations.

**Kerns, C. M., Newschaffer, C. J., & Berkowitz, S. J. (2015).** *Traumatic childhood events and autism spectrum disorder.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 45(11), 3475–3486. **[R/C]** — Documents elevated trauma exposure in autistic children. Strong citation for the trauma-informed autism literature.

**Haruvi-Lamdan, N., Horesh, D., Zohar, S., Kraus, M., & Golan, O. (2020).** *Autism spectrum disorder and post-traumatic stress disorder: An unexplored co-occurrence of conditions.* Autism, 24(4), 884–898. **[R/C]** — Argues for routine PTSD screening in autistic populations. Reviews differential diagnosis between autistic traits and PTSD symptoms.

## Eating differences and ARFID

**Bourne, L., Bryant-Waugh, R., Cook, J., & Mandy, W. (2020).** *Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder: A systematic scoping review of the current literature.* Psychiatry Research, 288, 112961. **[R/C]** — Scoping review of ARFID. Establishes the autism-ARFID link as a major area of clinical concern.

**Inoue, T., Otani, R., Iguchi, T., Ishii, R., Uchida, S., Okada, A., et al. (2021).** *Prevalence of autism spectrum disorder and autistic traits in children with anorexia nervosa and avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder.* BioPsychoSocial Medicine, 15(1), 9. **[R/C]** — Documents the strong overlap between ARFID and autism in clinical samples.

**Westwood, H., & Tchanturia, K. (2017).** *Autism spectrum disorder in anorexia nervosa: An updated literature review.* Current Psychiatry Reports, 19(7), 41. **[R/C]** — Anorexia-autism comorbidity. Important for clinicians treating eating disorders, as autistic anorexia presentations may differ and may not respond to standard protocols.

## Sleep

**Hodge, D., Carollo, T. M., Lewin, M., Hoffman, C. D., & Sweeney, D. P. (2014).** *Sleep patterns in children with and without autism spectrum disorders: Developmental comparisons.* Research in Developmental Disabilities, 35(7), 1631–1638. **[R/C]** — Sleep difference patterns across autistic children. Useful for clinical and parenting contexts.

**Morgan, B., Nageye, F., Masi, G., & Cortese, S. (2020).** *Sleep in adults with autism spectrum disorder: A systematic review and meta-analysis of subjective and objective studies.* Sleep Medicine, 65, 113–120. **[R/C]** — Adult-focused sleep research. Documents the persistence of sleep differences into adulthood.

## GI and physical health

**McElhanon, B. O., McCracken, C., Karpen, S., & Sharp, W. G. (2014).** *Gastrointestinal symptoms in autism spectrum disorder: A meta-analysis.* Pediatrics, 133(5), 872–883. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis documenting elevated GI symptoms in autistic populations. Useful for primary care and gastroenterology referral conversations.

**Croen, L. A., Zerbo, O., Qian, Y., Massolo, M. L., Rich, S., Sidney, S., & Kripke, C. (2015).** *The health status of adults on the autism spectrum.* Autism, 19(7), 814–823. **[R/C]** — Large Kaiser Permanente study of adult autistic health. Documents elevated rates of multiple chronic conditions across body systems.

## Hypermobility and EDS

**Csecs, J. L. L., Iodice, V., Rae, C. L., Brooke, A., Simmons, R., Quadt, L., et al. (2022).** *Joint hypermobility links neurodivergence to dysautonomia and pain.* Frontiers in Psychiatry, 12, 786916. **[R/C]** — Documents elevated joint hypermobility in autistic and ADHD adults, with links to dysautonomia and chronic pain. Foundational for the autism-EDS-POTS triad literature.

**Cederlöf, M., Larsson, H., Lichtenstein, P., Almqvist, C., Serlachius, E., & Ludvigsson, J. F. (2016).** *Nationwide population-based cohort study of psychiatric disorders in individuals with Ehlers-Danlos syndrome or hypermobility syndrome and their siblings.* BMC Psychiatry, 16(1), 207. **[R/C]** — Swedish cohort showing elevated autism and other neurodevelopmental conditions in EDS populations.

## PMDD and hormonal sensitivity

**Steward, R., Crane, L., Mairi Roy, E., Remington, A., & Pellicano, E. (2018).** *"Life is much more difficult to manage during periods": Autistic experiences of menstruation.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 48(12), 4287–4292. **[R/C]** — Qualitative study of autistic experiences of menstruation, including sensory amplification and emotional dysregulation. The clinical research is still thin; this is the entry point.

**Obaydi, H., & Puri, B. K. (2008).** *Prevalence of premenstrual syndrome in autism: A prospective observer-rated study.* Journal of International Medical Research, 36(2), 268–272. **[R/C]** — Older study documenting elevated PMS prevalence in autistic women. Newer empirical work on PMDD-autism specifically is still emerging; recommend the user search Autism in Adulthood for current articles.

## Catatonia (often missed)

**Wing, L., & Shah, A. (2000).** *Catatonia in autistic spectrum disorders.* British Journal of Psychiatry, 176(4), 357–362. **[R/C]** — Foundational paper documenting that autism-spectrum catatonia exists, often missed because it presents differently from primary catatonia. Roughly 12–17% of adolescent and adult autistic individuals develop catatonic features. Important for clinicians who might mistake the presentation for shutdown, depression, or behavioral regression.

**Dhossche, D. M., & Wachtel, L. E. (2010).** *Catatonia is hidden in plain sight among different pediatric disorders: A review article.* Pediatric Neurology, 43(5), 307–315. **[R/C]** — Review of pediatric catatonia, including in autism. Useful for clinicians who haven't been trained to recognize it in this population.
