# Foundational and Historical Literature

The texts that built the field — including the ones the field has substantially revised, rejected, or had to confront. Cite the classics with their context, not as if they were neutral science.

## The original case studies

**Kanner, L. (1943).** *Autistic disturbances of affective contact.* The Nervous Child, 2, 217–250. **[R/C]** — The first English-language description of autism as a distinct condition, based on 11 cases. Establishes early diagnostic criteria. Required reading for the historical record. Limitations: small sample, all from a select clinical population, framing later used to support "refrigerator mother" theory Kanner himself eventually rejected.

**Asperger, H. (1944).** *Die "Autistischen Psychopathen" im Kindesalter.* Archiv für Psychiatrie und Nervenkrankheiten, 117, 76–136. **[R/C]** — Asperger's case studies of autistic children, written in Vienna during Nazi occupation. The basis for the diagnostic category that bore his name from DSM-IV (1994) until DSM-5 folded it into ASD (2013). Cite alongside the historical reckoning below — Asperger's institutional cooperation with Nazi child-killing programs is now documented and changes how the work is read.

**Czech, H. (2018).** *Hans Asperger, National Socialism, and "race hygiene" in Nazi-era Vienna.* Molecular Autism, 9(1), 29. **[Both]** — The archival study that established Asperger's complicity with Nazi euthanasia programs. Required context for any current citation of Asperger's original work. Open access; widely accessible.

**Sheffer, E. (2018).** *Asperger's children: The origins of autism in Nazi Vienna.* W. W. Norton. **[Both]** — Book-length historical treatment of the same material. More accessible than the Czech paper for general readers. Establishes why much of the autistic community has moved away from "Aspergers" as identity terminology.

## The discredited classics

**Bettelheim, B. (1967).** *The empty fortress: Infantile autism and the birth of the self.* Free Press. **[R/C historical only]** — The most influential statement of "refrigerator mother" theory, blaming autism on cold maternal attachment. The framing was wrong, the clinical practice it justified was harmful, and Bettelheim's professional credentials were later shown to have been fabricated. Cite as historical document showing how the field went wrong, not as evidence.

**Pollak, R. (1997).** *The creation of Dr. B: A biography of Bruno Bettelheim.* Simon & Schuster. **[Lay]** — The investigation that documented the credential fraud and the harmful clinical practices at Bettelheim's Orthogenic School. Useful as the corrective companion to *The Empty Fortress*.

**Lovaas, O. I. (1987).** *Behavioral treatment and normal educational and intellectual functioning in young autistic children.* Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 55(1), 3–9. **[R/C]** — The study that established Applied Behavior Analysis as the dominant autism intervention. Claims 47% of intensively treated children became "indistinguishable from peers." Limitations widely critiqued: small sample, methodological issues, ethical problems with the original protocol (which included aversive procedures), the "indistinguishable from peers" outcome itself now widely understood as a masking-as-success metric. Cite with the contemporary critique attached.

**Lovaas, O. I., Schaeffer, B., & Simmons, J. Q. (1965).** *Building social behavior in autistic children by use of electric shock.* Journal of Experimental Studies in Personality, 1, 99–109. **[R/C historical only]** — Worth knowing this exists in the literature when citing the early ABA evidence base. Aversive procedures including electric shock were part of the original Lovaas protocol. Most contemporary ABA practice has moved away from aversives, but the methodological lineage is part of the honest history.

## The vaccine causation literature — retracted, but cite for the record

**Wakefield, A. J., et al. (1998).** *Ileal-lymphoid-nodular hyperplasia, non-specific colitis, and pervasive developmental disorder in children.* The Lancet, 351, 637–641. **[R/C historical only]** — The retracted study that drove the vaccine-causation panic. **Retracted by The Lancet in 2010.** Wakefield lost his medical license. Cite only when explicitly addressing the history of misinformation; do not cite as evidence of any claim.

**Godlee, F., Smith, J., & Marcovitch, H. (2011).** *Wakefield's article linking MMR vaccine and autism was fraudulent.* BMJ, 342, c7452. **[Both]** — The British Medical Journal editorial accompanying Brian Deer's investigative reporting establishing the Wakefield study as fraud, not just bad science. The corrective citation.

**Taylor, L. E., Swerdfeger, A. L., & Eslick, G. D. (2014).** *Vaccines are not associated with autism: An evidence-based meta-analysis of case-control and cohort studies.* Vaccine, 32(29), 3623–3629. **[R/C]** — One of several large meta-analyses establishing no causal link. Useful when the user needs a single citation to support the consensus position.

## The DSM history

**American Psychiatric Association.** *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders.* DSM-III (1980), DSM-III-R (1987), DSM-IV (1994), DSM-IV-TR (2000), DSM-5 (2013), DSM-5-TR (2022). **[R/C]** — The diagnostic category has changed substantially across editions. DSM-III introduced "Infantile Autism" as a discrete diagnosis. DSM-IV introduced Asperger's Disorder, PDD-NOS, and the rest of the PDD category. DSM-5 collapsed these into "Autism Spectrum Disorder" with severity levels. DSM-5-TR refined diagnostic specifiers. Cite the specific edition relevant to the historical period being discussed.

**Volkmar, F. R., & McPartland, J. C. (2014).** *From Kanner to DSM-5: Autism as an evolving diagnostic concept.* Annual Review of Clinical Psychology, 10, 193–212. **[R/C]** — A clinical-historical review of how the diagnostic concept has evolved. Useful entry point for the diagnostic history.

**Silberman, S. (2015).** *NeuroTribes: The legacy of autism and the future of neurodiversity.* Avery. **[Both]** — Long-form journalistic history covering Kanner, Asperger, the rise of ABA, the parent-blaming era, the vaccine panic, and the emergence of the neurodiversity movement. The most-cited general-audience history of the field. Strengths: comprehensive narrative, accessible. Limitations: journalistic synthesis rather than primary scholarship; the field has moved further on some points since publication.

**Donvan, J., & Zucker, C. (2016).** *In a different key: The story of autism.* Crown. **[Lay]** — Companion / counterpoint to Silberman. More focus on parents and clinicians, less on autistic self-advocacy. Useful read alongside *NeuroTribes* for a fuller picture, including the perspectives Silberman undercovered.

## The early autistic-authored autobiographical literature

**Williams, D. (1992).** *Nobody nowhere: The extraordinary autobiography of an autistic.* Times Books. **[Lay]** — One of the earliest autistic-authored memoirs in English. Donna Williams's account of childhood autism, sensory experience, and identity. Historically important.

**Grandin, T. (1995).** *Thinking in pictures: My life with autism.* Doubleday. **[Lay]** — The most cited autistic-authored work of the 1990s. Visual thinking framework, sensory regulation (squeeze machine), career trajectory. See knowledge-core notes on Grandin for current framing of her positions.

**Sinclair, J. (1993).** *Don't mourn for us.* Our Voice, 1(3). **[Lay]** — Short essay that became a foundational text of the autistic self-advocacy movement. Available free online via the Autism Network International archive. Cite as an early statement of the position later formalized as the neurodiversity paradigm.

## Foundational autistic self-advocacy

**Singer, J. (1999).** *"Why can't you be normal for once in your life?" From a "problem with no name" to the emergence of a new category of difference.* In M. Corker & S. French (Eds.), *Disability discourse* (pp. 59–67). Open University Press. **[R/C]** — Judy Singer's chapter introducing the term "neurodiversity." The original formal use, often miscited.

**Bascom, J. (Ed.). (2012).** *Loud hands: Autistic people, speaking.* Autistic Self Advocacy Network. **[Both]** — Anthology of autistic-authored writing edited by Julia Bascom. Cited in academic work but accessible to general readers. Foundational primary source for autistic-led perspectives on intervention, identity, and rights.

## Cite-with-context

A few works that appear in autism literature and are useful to know but require careful framing:

**Baron-Cohen, S., Leslie, A. M., & Frith, U. (1985).** *Does the autistic child have a "theory of mind"?* Cognition, 21(1), 37–46. **[R/C]** — The original "theory of mind" paper. Cite alongside the contemporary critique: theory-of-mind deficit framing has been challenged by the double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) and by autistic-led research suggesting the deficit is mutual rather than one-directional. Foundational but contested.

**Frith, U. (1989).** *Autism: Explaining the enigma.* Blackwell. **[Both]** — Influential statement of weak central coherence theory. Cite in the historical record of cognitive theories of autism. Subsequent monotropism literature (Murray, Lesser, & Lawson, 2005) reframes much of what weak central coherence described in deficit terms as different attention allocation.
