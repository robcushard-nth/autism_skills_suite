# Intervention Research

The evidence base for autism interventions, organized to support honest assessment rather than advocacy for any single approach. Includes the ABA literature, the harm research, and the alternative-intervention literature.

## ABA — the evidence the field cites

**Lovaas, O. I. (1987).** *Behavioral treatment and normal educational and intellectual functioning in young autistic children.* Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology, 55(1), 3–9. **[R/C]** — The foundational study. Cite with the contemporary methodological critique attached. The 47% "indistinguishable from peers" claim is widely contested.

**McEachin, J. J., Smith, T., & Lovaas, O. I. (1993).** *Long-term outcome for children with autism who received early intensive behavioral treatment.* American Journal of Mental Retardation, 97(4), 359–372. **[R/C]** — Lovaas follow-up study often cited as evidence of ABA's lasting effects. Same methodological limitations as the original.

**Eldevik, S., Hastings, R. P., Hughes, J. C., Jahr, E., Eikeseth, S., & Cross, S. (2009).** *Meta-analysis of early intensive behavioral intervention for children with autism.* Journal of Clinical Child & Adolescent Psychology, 38(3), 439–450. **[R/C]** — A meta-analysis cited as evidence for EIBI (early intensive behavioral intervention). Effect sizes for IQ and adaptive behavior in the moderate range. Limitations: outcomes measured are the ones the protocol targets, not autistic well-being.

**Reichow, B., Hume, K., Barton, E. E., & Boyd, B. A. (2018).** *Early intensive behavioral intervention (EIBI) for young children with autism spectrum disorders (ASD).* Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, 5, CD009260. **[R/C]** — Cochrane review of EIBI. Concludes some evidence of benefit on adaptive behavior, IQ, and language at low certainty. Cite as the most rigorous current systematic review of the EIBI evidence base.

## ABA — the methodological and ethical critique

**Sandoval-Norton, A. H., Shkedy, G., & Shkedy, D. (2019).** *How much compliance is too much compliance: Is long-term ABA therapy abuse?* Cogent Psychology, 6(1), 1641258. **[R/C]** — Critical analysis arguing that long-term ABA produces compliance at the expense of autonomy and well-being. Cited in current debate.

**Kupferstein, H. (2018).** *Evidence of increased PTSD symptoms in autistics exposed to applied behavior analysis.* Advances in Autism, 4(1), 19–29. **[R/C]** — Survey research suggesting elevated PTSD symptoms in autistic adults who received ABA as children. Cite with awareness that the study has been critiqued methodologically (self-selected sample, retrospective design); also cite alongside subsequent work.

**Leaf, J. B., Ross, R. K., Cihon, J. H., & Weiss, M. J. (2018).** *Evaluating Kupferstein's claims of the relationship of behavioral intervention to PTSS for individuals with autism.* Advances in Autism, 4(3), 122–129. **[R/C]** — ABA practitioners' methodological critique of Kupferstein. Cite alongside Kupferstein for the academic exchange.

**Anderson, L. K. (2023).** *Autistic experiences of applied behavior analysis.* Autism, 27(3), 737–750. **[R/C]** — More recent qualitative study of autistic adults' retrospective experiences of ABA. Convergence with Kupferstein on negative outcomes; methodologically stronger.

**Dawson, M., & Fletcher-Watson, S. (2022).** *When autism researchers disregard harms: A commentary.* Autism, 26(2), 564–566. **[R/C]** — Commentary on the field's tendency to dismiss autistic adults' harm reports. Useful citation for the meta-level argument about whose evidence counts.

**Wilkenfeld, D. A., & McCarthy, A. M. (2020).** *Ethical concerns with applied behavior analysis for autism spectrum "disorder."* Kennedy Institute of Ethics Journal, 30(1), 31–69. **[R/C]** — Bioethics analysis of ABA from autonomy and dignity perspectives. Cite when the question is ethical rather than empirical.

**Sandbank, M., Bottema-Beutel, K., Crowley, S., Cassidy, M., Dunham, K., Feldman, J. I., et al. (2020).** *Project AIM: Autism intervention meta-analysis for studies of young children.* Psychological Bulletin, 146(1), 1–29. **[R/C]** — Comprehensive meta-analysis of early intervention research. Important findings: most published intervention research has serious methodological problems (small samples, no blinding, conflict of interest, outcome reporting bias). Effect sizes shrink considerably when methodological quality is controlled.

**Bottema-Beutel, K., Crowley, S., Sandbank, M., & Woynaroski, T. G. (2021).** *Adverse event reporting in intervention research for young autistic children.* Autism, 25(2), 322–335. **[R/C]** — Documents the field's poor record on adverse event reporting. Most intervention studies don't track or report harm. Foundational citation for the harm-reporting argument.

## Alternative and adjacent interventions

### DIR/Floortime

**Greenspan, S. I., & Wieder, S. (2006).** *Engaging autism: Using the Floortime approach to help children relate, communicate, and think.* Da Capo Press. **[Both]** — Foundational text on the developmental, individual-differences, relationship-based (DIR/Floortime) model. Less peer-reviewed evidence than ABA but more aligned with affirming approaches.

**Pajareya, K., & Nopmaneejumruslers, K. (2011).** *A pilot randomized controlled trial of DIR/Floortime parent training intervention for pre-school children with autistic spectrum disorders.* Autism, 15(5), 563–577. **[R/C]** — Small RCT of DIR/Floortime. Cite for the empirical evidence base alongside the limitations (small sample, single site).

**Solomon, R., Necheles, J., Ferch, C., & Bruckman, D. (2007).** *Pilot study of a parent training program for young children with autism: The PLAY Project Home Consultation program.* Autism, 11(3), 205–224. **[R/C]** — Evaluation of a DIR-based parent training program. Useful entry into the developmental-relational intervention literature.

### Early Start Denver Model (ESDM)

**Dawson, G., Rogers, S., Munson, J., Smith, M., Winter, J., Greenson, J., Donaldson, A., & Varley, J. (2010).** *Randomized, controlled trial of an intervention for toddlers with autism: The Early Start Denver Model.* Pediatrics, 125(1), e17–e23. **[R/C]** — RCT of ESDM, an early intervention combining developmental and behavioral approaches. Often cited as evidence for early intervention.

**Rogers, S. J., Estes, A., Lord, C., Vismara, L., Winter, J., Fitzpatrick, A., et al. (2012).** *Effects of a brief Early Start Denver Model (ESDM)-based parent intervention on toddlers at risk for autism spectrum disorders.* Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, 51(10), 1052–1065. **[R/C]** — Parent-implemented ESDM. Useful for discussions of parent-mediated intervention.

### SCERTS

**Prizant, B. M., Wetherby, A. M., Rubin, E., Laurent, A. C., & Rydell, P. J. (2006).** *The SCERTS Model: A comprehensive educational approach for children with autism spectrum disorders.* Brookes. **[R/C]** — Foundational text for the SCERTS (Social Communication, Emotional Regulation, Transactional Support) model. Often recommended as a more affirming alternative to ABA in school contexts.

### Hanen / parent-mediated communication intervention

**Carter, A. S., Messinger, D. S., Stone, W. L., Celimli, S., Nahmias, A. S., & Yoder, P. (2011).** *A randomized controlled trial of Hanen's "More Than Words" in toddlers with early autism symptoms.* Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 52(7), 741–752. **[R/C]** — Evaluation of the Hanen parent-mediated communication intervention. Useful for the parent-coaching alternative literature.

### AAC and communication

**Ganz, J. B., Davis, J. L., Lund, E. M., Goodwyn, F. D., & Simpson, R. L. (2012).** *Meta-analysis of PECS with individuals with ASD: Investigation of targeted versus non-targeted outcomes, participant characteristics, and implementation phase.* Research in Developmental Disabilities, 33(2), 406–418. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis of Picture Exchange Communication System. Useful for the PECS evidence base.

**Iacono, T., Trembath, D., & Erickson, S. (2016).** *The role of augmentative and alternative communication for children with autism: Current status and future trends.* Neuropsychiatric Disease and Treatment, 12, 2349–2361. **[R/C]** — Review of AAC use with autistic children. Important for any discussion of communication intervention beyond speech therapy.

**Romski, M., & Sevcik, R. A. (2005).** *Augmentative communication and early intervention: Myths and realities.* Infants & Young Children, 18(3), 174–185. **[R/C]** — Refutes the persistent myth that AAC delays speech development. Cite when parents or clinicians resist AAC out of concern it will replace speech.

### Sensory integration and OT

**Schaaf, R. C., Benevides, T., Mailloux, Z., Faller, P., Hunt, J., van Hooydonk, E., et al. (2014).** *An intervention for sensory difficulties in children with autism: A randomized trial.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 44(7), 1493–1506. **[R/C]** — RCT of Ayres Sensory Integration® intervention. Mixed effects literature; cite alongside the systematic reviews.

**Watling, R., & Hauer, S. (2015).** *Effectiveness of Ayres Sensory Integration® and sensory-based interventions for people with autism spectrum disorder: A systematic review.* American Journal of Occupational Therapy, 69(5), 6905180030. **[R/C]** — Systematic review of sensory integration interventions. Conclusions: some evidence for ASI as a discrete approach, less for sensory-based interventions broadly.

## The "indistinguishable from peers" debate

**Schreibman, L., Dawson, G., Stahmer, A. C., Landa, R., Rogers, S. J., McGee, G. G., et al. (2015).** *Naturalistic developmental behavioral interventions: Empirically validated treatments for autism spectrum disorder.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 45(8), 2411–2428. **[R/C]** — Major review establishing NDBIs (naturalistic developmental behavioral interventions) as a category. The middle ground between traditional ABA and developmental approaches.

**Kapp, S. K. (2018).** *Social support, well-being, and quality of life among individuals on the autism spectrum.* Pediatrics, 141(Supplement 4), S362–S368. **[R/C]** — Argues for outcome metrics centered on autistic well-being rather than approximation to neurotypical behavior. Foundational citation for the "indistinguishable from peers" critique.

**Ne'eman, A. (2021).** *When disability is defined by behavior, outcome measures should not promote "passing."* AMA Journal of Ethics, 23(7), E569–E575. **[R/C]** — Bioethics argument against masking-as-success outcome metrics. Authored by Ari Ne'eman, ASAN co-founder.

## Pseudoscience — cite only for refutation literature

The MMS, chelation, hyperbaric, and stem-cell-tourism literature is not credible science and should not be cited as evidence. When the user needs the refutation literature:

**Davis, T. N., O'Reilly, M., Kang, S., Lang, R., Rispoli, M., Sigafoos, J., et al. (2013).** *Chelation treatment for autism spectrum disorders: A systematic review.* Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders, 7(1), 49–55. **[R/C]** — Systematic review concluding no credible evidence for chelation as autism treatment, with documented harms.

**FDA. (2019, 2020, 2023).** *Warnings on Miracle Mineral Solution / chlorine dioxide products.* US Food and Drug Administration. **[Both]** — Cite the FDA warnings directly when the topic is MMS/CD.

**Schechter, R., & Grether, J. K. (2008).** *Continuing increases in autism reported to California's developmental services system: Mercury in retrograde.* Archives of General Psychiatry, 65(1), 19–24. **[R/C]** — One of the studies showing autism rates continued rising after thimerosal removal — refuting the mercury-in-vaccines hypothesis.

**Hviid, A., Hansen, J. V., Frisch, M., & Melbye, M. (2019).** *Measles, mumps, rubella vaccination and autism: A nationwide cohort study.* Annals of Internal Medicine, 170(8), 513–520. **[R/C]** — Large Danish cohort study (over 650,000 children) confirming no link between MMR and autism. Useful single citation for the vaccine consensus.
