# Sensory and Cognitive Research

The literature on autistic perception, attention, executive function, interoception, and the cognitive theories that have shaped (and been contested in) the field.

## Sensory processing

**Marco, E. J., Hinkley, L. B. N., Hill, S. S., & Nagarajan, S. S. (2011).** *Sensory processing in autism: A review of neurophysiologic findings.* Pediatric Research, 69(5 Pt 2), 48R–54R. **[R/C]** — Review of neurophysiological evidence for sensory processing differences in autism. Foundational citation when establishing that sensory differences have measurable neural correlates.

**Robertson, C. E., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2017).** *Sensory perception in autism.* Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 18(11), 671–684. **[R/C]** — High-impact review of autistic sensory perception across modalities. Strong synthesis of behavioral and neural evidence.

**Tomchek, S. D., & Dunn, W. (2007).** *Sensory processing in children with and without autism: A comparative study using the Short Sensory Profile.* American Journal of Occupational Therapy, 61(2), 190–200. **[R/C]** — Foundational comparative study using the Sensory Profile, one of the most-used clinical instruments. Documents the prevalence of sensory differences across the autistic pediatric population.

**Ben-Sasson, A., Hen, L., Fluss, R., Cermak, S. A., Engel-Yeger, B., & Gal, E. (2009).** *A meta-analysis of sensory modulation symptoms in individuals with autism spectrum disorders.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 39(1), 1–11. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis of sensory modulation differences. Useful for prevalence estimates and effect sizes.

**MacLennan, K., O'Brien, S., & Tavassoli, T. (2022).** *In our own words: The complex sensory experiences of autistic adults.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 52(7), 3061–3075. **[R/C]** — Qualitative study of autistic adults' descriptions of their own sensory experience. Important corrective to literature that observes sensory behavior without asking what it feels like.

## Interoception

**Garfinkel, S. N., Tiley, C., O'Keeffe, S., Harrison, N. A., Seth, A. K., & Critchley, H. D. (2016).** *Discrepancies between dimensions of interoception in autism: Implications for emotion and anxiety.* Biological Psychology, 114, 117–126. **[R/C]** — Foundational empirical study on interoceptive differences in autism. Documents that autistic adults often have lower interoceptive accuracy alongside higher interoceptive anxiety — a specific mismatch with implications for emotion regulation and alexithymia.

**DuBois, D., Ameis, S. H., Lai, M.-C., Casanova, M. F., & Desarkar, P. (2016).** *Interoception in autism spectrum disorder: A review.* International Journal of Developmental Neuroscience, 52, 104–111. **[R/C]** — Review of the interoception literature in autism. Useful entry point for clinicians considering interoception in assessment and intervention.

**Mul, C.-L., Stagg, S. D., Herbelin, B., & Aspell, J. E. (2018).** *The feeling of me feeling for you: Interoception, alexithymia and empathy in autism.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 48(9), 2953–2967. **[R/C]** — Connects interoception, alexithymia, and empathy — the empathy "deficit" in autism is largely accounted for by alexithymia, which is often interoceptive in basis.

## Alexithymia

**Bird, G., & Cook, R. (2013).** *Mixed emotions: The contribution of alexithymia to the emotional symptoms of autism.* Translational Psychiatry, 3(7), e285. **[R/C]** — Foundational paper on the alexithymia-not-autism hypothesis: many of the empathy and emotion-recognition findings attributed to autism are actually attributable to co-occurring alexithymia. Frequently cited.

**Kinnaird, E., Stewart, C., & Tchanturia, K. (2019).** *Investigating alexithymia in autism: A systematic review and meta-analysis.* European Psychiatry, 55, 80–89. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis estimating roughly 50% of autistic individuals meet criteria for alexithymia, compared to roughly 5% of the general population.

## Monotropism and attention

**Murray, D., Lesser, M., & Lawson, W. (2005).** *Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism.* Autism, 9(2), 139–156. **[R/C]** — The foundational paper on monotropism. Authored by autistic researchers. The alternative framework to weak central coherence.

**Lawson, W. (2010).** *The passionate mind: How people with autism learn.* Jessica Kingsley Publishers. **[Lay]** — Wenn Lawson's book-length treatment of monotropism applied to learning. Accessible.

**Murray, F. (2018).** *Me and monotropism.* The Psychologist, 31, 44–49. **[Lay]** — Free online via the BPS. Best accessible introduction.

## Executive function

**Hill, E. L. (2004).** *Executive dysfunction in autism.* Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 8(1), 26–32. **[R/C]** — Influential review of executive function research in autism. Foundational, but cite alongside more recent work that has refined the picture.

**Demetriou, E. A., Lampit, A., Quintana, D. S., Naismith, S. L., Song, Y. J. C., Pye, J. E., et al. (2018).** *Autism spectrum disorders: A meta-analysis of executive function.* Molecular Psychiatry, 23(5), 1198–1204. **[R/C]** — Meta-analysis of executive function across autistic populations. Confirms moderate effect sizes across most EF domains.

**White, S. J., Burgess, P. W., & Hill, E. L. (2009).** *Impairments on "open-ended" executive function tests in autism.* Autism Research, 2(3), 138–147. **[R/C]** — Documents the asymmetry between performance on structured EF tasks (often intact) and open-ended real-life EF (often impaired). Useful for clinicians who hear "but the standardized tests are normal."

## ADHD comorbidity and AuDHD

**Lai, M.-C., Kassee, C., Besney, R., Bonato, S., Hull, L., Mandy, W., Szatmari, P., & Ameis, S. H. (2019).** *Prevalence of co-occurring mental health diagnoses in the autism population: A systematic review and meta-analysis.* The Lancet Psychiatry, 6(10), 819–829. **[R/C]** — Major meta-analysis. Establishes that more than 70% of autistic individuals have at least one co-occurring mental health condition. ADHD comorbidity estimated around 28% across studies. Frequently cited as the canonical comorbidity citation.

**Antshel, K. M., & Russo, N. (2019).** *Autism spectrum disorders and ADHD: Overlapping phenomenology, diagnostic issues, and treatment considerations.* Current Psychiatry Reports, 21(5), 34. **[R/C]** — Clinical review of AuDHD presentation, differential diagnosis, and treatment.

## Predictive coding and uncertainty models

**Pellicano, E., & Burr, D. (2012).** *When the world becomes "too real": A Bayesian explanation of autistic perception.* Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 16(10), 504–510. **[R/C]** — Foundational paper proposing that autistic perception involves attenuated priors — the world feels more "real" because predictive processing is differently weighted. Influential theoretical framework.

**Van de Cruys, S., Evers, K., Van der Hallen, R., Van Eylen, L., Boets, B., de-Wit, L., & Wagemans, J. (2014).** *Precise minds in uncertain worlds: Predictive coding in autism.* Psychological Review, 121(4), 649–675. **[R/C]** — Major theoretical paper extending predictive coding accounts to autism. Explains repetitive behaviors, sensory differences, and social difficulties through a single computational lens. Heavily cited in cognitive neuroscience literature.

**Lawson, R. P., Rees, G., & Friston, K. J. (2014).** *An aberrant precision account of autism.* Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 8, 302. **[R/C]** — Variant of the predictive-coding account focused on precision weighting. For readers wanting the technical version.

## Theory of mind and its critique

**Baron-Cohen, S., Leslie, A. M., & Frith, U. (1985).** *Does the autistic child have a "theory of mind"?* Cognition, 21(1), 37–46. **[R/C]** — The original ToM paper. Cite alongside the critique below.

**Gernsbacher, M. A., & Yergeau, M. (2019).** *Empirical failures of the claim that autistic people lack a theory of mind.* Archives of Scientific Psychology, 7(1), 102–118. **[Both]** — Comprehensive review of how poorly the original ToM claim has held up empirically. Co-authored by an autistic scholar (Yergeau). Required citation when discussing the ToM literature today.

**Heyes, C. (2014).** *False belief in infancy: A fresh look.* Developmental Science, 17(5), 647–659. **[R/C]** — Methodological critique of the broader theory-of-mind paradigm.

**Crompton, C. J., Ropar, D., et al. (2020).** *Autistic peer-to-peer information transfer is highly effective.* (See neurodiversity-and-affirming-scholarship.md for full citation.) **[R/C]** — Empirical demonstration that autistic-to-autistic communication is intact. Reframes the deficit as relational, not individual.

## Weak central coherence — historical, contested

**Frith, U., & Happé, F. (1994).** *Autism: Beyond "theory of mind."* Cognition, 50(1–3), 115–132. **[R/C]** — Frith and Happé's weak central coherence theory. Influential historically; cite alongside monotropism literature for the contemporary reframing of the same phenomena.

**Happé, F., & Frith, U. (2006).** *The weak coherence account: Detail-focused cognitive style in autism spectrum disorders.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 36(1), 5–25. **[R/C]** — Updated formulation of WCC, with the framing shifted from deficit to "cognitive style." Useful for tracking how the field has revised.

## Autistic strengths and pattern thinking

**Mottron, L., Dawson, M., Soulières, I., Hubert, B., & Burack, J. (2006).** *Enhanced perceptual functioning in autism: An update, and eight principles of autistic perception.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 36(1), 27–43. **[R/C]** — Mottron's enhanced perceptual functioning model. Often cited as evidence-based counterpart to deficit-focused theories.

**Dawson, M., Soulières, I., Gernsbacher, M. A., & Mottron, L. (2007).** *The level and nature of autistic intelligence.* Psychological Science, 18(8), 657–662. **[R/C]** — Documents that autistic intelligence is often underestimated when measured with standard verbal-loaded instruments. Co-authored by autistic researcher Michelle Dawson.

**Soulières, I., Dawson, M., Samson, F., Barbeau, E. B., Sahyoun, C. P., Strangman, G. E., et al. (2009).** *Enhanced visual processing contributes to matrix reasoning in autism.* Human Brain Mapping, 30(12), 4082–4107. **[R/C]** — Neuroimaging evidence for enhanced visual processing as the basis for autistic strengths in pattern reasoning. Useful for the cognitive-strengths literature.
