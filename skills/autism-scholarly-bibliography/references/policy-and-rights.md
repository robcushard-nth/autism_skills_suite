# Policy and Rights Literature

The legal, policy, and rights-based scholarship on autism — special education law, disability rights, employment research, restraint and seclusion, deinstitutionalization history, and the self-advocacy movement.

## Disability rights foundations (cite for autism in context)

**Shapiro, J. P. (1993).** *No pity: People with disabilities forging a new civil rights movement.* Times Books. **[Both]** — Foundational journalistic history of the US disability rights movement. Provides essential context for understanding autism advocacy as part of a broader rights movement, not a stand-alone phenomenon.

**Charlton, J. I. (1998).** *Nothing about us without us: Disability oppression and empowerment.* University of California Press. **[Both]** — The foundational text of the "nothing about us without us" framework central to disability rights and increasingly to autism research. Cite when establishing the participatory ethics framework.

**Linton, S. (1998).** *Claiming disability: Knowledge and identity.* New York University Press. **[R/C]** — Foundational work in disability studies establishing identity-first language and the social model of disability.

## Special education law (US)

**Yell, M. L. (2019).** *The law and special education* (5th ed.). Pearson. **[Both]** — The standard textbook on special education law in the US. Covers IDEA, Section 504, and ADA as they apply to schools. Useful entry point for non-attorneys.

**Turnbull, H. R., Stowe, M. J., & Huerta, N. E. (2007).** *Free appropriate public education: The law and children with disabilities* (7th ed.). Love Publishing. **[Both]** — Older but foundational text on FAPE under IDEA. Useful when historical context is needed.

**Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District, 137 S. Ct. 988 (2017).** **[R/C]** — Supreme Court case raising the standard of educational benefit required under IDEA from "merely more than de minimis" to a standard of "appropriately ambitious progress." Major case for special education law and frequently cited in IEP advocacy.

**Pasachoff, E. M. (2011).** *Special education, poverty, and the limits of private enforcement.* Notre Dame Law Review, 86(4), 1413–1492. **[R/C]** — Legal scholarship on the failures of private enforcement in special education. Useful for systemic critique.

## Restraint, seclusion, and discipline

**US Government Accountability Office. (2009).** *Seclusions and restraints: Selected cases of death and abuse at public and private schools and treatment centers.* GAO-09-719T. **[R/C]** — The GAO investigation that documented widespread restraint and seclusion abuse in US schools. Cite when discussing policy failures and the case for reform.

**Westling, D. L., Trader, B. R., Smith, C. A., & Marshall, D. S. (2010).** *Use of restraints, seclusion, and aversive procedures on students with disabilities.* Research and Practice for Persons with Severe Disabilities, 35(3-4), 116–127. **[R/C]** — Survey research documenting the prevalence of these practices and their disproportionate use on disabled students.

**Vera Institute of Justice. (Various reports).** *On restraint, seclusion, and the school-to-prison pipeline as it intersects with disability.* **[R/C]** — Vera publications useful for the intersection of disability and the carceral system in education contexts.

**Ne'eman, A. (2010).** *The future (and the past) of autism advocacy, or why the ASA's magazine, The Advocate, wouldn't publish my piece.* Disability Studies Quarterly, 30(1). **[R/C]** — Open-access disability studies article on contested terrain in autism advocacy. Useful for the history of conflict between parent-led and autistic-led advocacy organizations.

## Employment and workplace research

**Roux, A. M., Shattuck, P. T., Cooper, B. P., Anderson, K. A., Wagner, M., & Narendorf, S. C. (2013).** *Postsecondary employment experiences among young adults with an autism spectrum disorder.* Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, 52(9), 931–939. **[R/C]** — Foundational data on autistic young adults' employment outcomes. Documents the substantial gap between autistic and other disabled young adults.

**Shattuck, P. T., Narendorf, S. C., Cooper, B., Sterzing, P. R., Wagner, M., & Taylor, J. L. (2012).** *Postsecondary education and employment among youth with an autism spectrum disorder.* Pediatrics, 129(6), 1042–1049. **[R/C]** — Often-cited data on postsecondary outcomes. Useful for transition-planning literature.

**Scott, M., Jacob, A., Hendrie, D., Parsons, R., Girdler, S., Falkmer, T., & Falkmer, M. (2017).** *Employers' perception of the costs and the benefits of hiring individuals with autism spectrum disorder in open employment in Australia.* PLoS ONE, 12(5), e0177607. **[R/C]** — Documents that the costs of hiring autistic employees are typically far less than employers anticipate. Useful for workplace advocacy.

**Hayward, S. M., McVilly, K. R., & Stokes, M. A. (2018).** *Challenges for females with high functioning autism in the workplace: A systematic review.* Disability and Rehabilitation, 40(3), 249–258. **[R/C]** — Specific to women's workplace experience. Note dated language ("high functioning") in the title; content is clinically useful.

**Bury, S. M., Hedley, D., Uljarević, M., & Gal, E. (2020).** *The autism advantage at work: A critical and systematic review of current evidence.* Research in Developmental Disabilities, 105, 103750. **[R/C]** — Critical review of the "autism advantage" framing in employment literature. Important counterweight to corporate diversity-marketing narratives.

## ADA and employment law

**Burgess, S., & Cimera, R. E. (2014).** *Employment outcomes of transition-aged adults with autism spectrum disorders: A state of the states report.* American Journal on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities, 119(1), 64–83. **[R/C]** — State-by-state outcomes for vocational rehabilitation services for autistic adults.

**Murphy, M. P. (2024).** *Disability disclosure and ADA accommodations: A current review of the legal landscape.* (Search current law review databases — HeinOnline, LexisNexis — for current articles on this evolving area.) **[R/C]** — The ADA case law continues to evolve. Recommend the user search current law reviews for the most recent treatments rather than rely on a single citation.

## Self-advocacy movement history

**Sinclair, J. (2010).** *Being autistic together.* Disability Studies Quarterly, 30(1). **[Both]** — Jim Sinclair's reflection on the founding of Autism Network International and the early autistic-only spaces. Open access. Foundational primary source for self-advocacy history.

**Bagatell, N. (2010).** *From cure to community: Transforming notions of autism.* Ethos, 38(1), 33–55. **[R/C]** — Anthropological account of the shift in autistic community framing. Useful for cultural and historical analysis.

**Robertson, S. M., & Ne'eman, A. D. (2008).** *Autistic acceptance, the college campus, and technology: Growth of neurodiversity in society and academia.* Disability Studies Quarterly, 28(4). **[R/C]** — Open-access piece by ASAN co-founders on the early growth of the neurodiversity framework in academic and student contexts.

## Policy and service systems

**Anderson, K. A., Sosnowy, C., Kuo, A. A., & Shattuck, P. T. (2018).** *Transition of individuals with autism to adulthood: A review of qualitative studies.* Pediatrics, 141(Supplement 4), S318–S327. **[R/C]** — Synthesis of qualitative literature on transition to adulthood. Useful for transition policy and service-design literature.

**Friedman, C., Rizzolo, M. C., & Spassiani, N. A. (2019).** *Self-advocacy organizations addressing the rights of people with autism in the United States: A scoping review.* Disability Studies Quarterly, 39(2). **[R/C]** — Scoping review of US self-advocacy organizations. Useful for policy and movement-history research.

## International policy frameworks

**United Nations. (2006).** *Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.* **[Both]** — The CRPD is the central international human rights instrument for disability rights. Article 24 (education), Article 27 (work), and the General Comments are particularly relevant for autism advocacy.

**Krahn, G. L., Walker, D. K., & Correa-De-Araujo, R. (2015).** *Persons with disabilities as an unrecognized health disparity population.* American Journal of Public Health, 105(Supplement 2), S198–S206. **[R/C]** — Foundational citation for treating disability — including autism — as a health disparity population in policy and public health frameworks.

## Deinstitutionalization history (essential context)

**Trent, J. W. (1994).** *Inventing the feeble mind: A history of mental retardation in the United States.* University of California Press. **[R/C]** — Foundational history of the institutional system that autistic people (especially those with high support needs) were placed into. Essential context for understanding the rights movement that followed.

**Carey, A. C. (2009).** *On the margins of citizenship: Intellectual disability and civil rights in twentieth-century America.* Temple University Press. **[R/C]** — Companion historical work focused on civil rights gains. Useful for understanding why "supported decision-making" exists as a legal alternative to guardianship.
