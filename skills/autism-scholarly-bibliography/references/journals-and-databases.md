# Journals, Databases, and Where the Literature Lives

A practical guide to finding autism research — the journals where the literature lives, the databases that index it, the research groups producing it, and search strategies for getting at the current evidence.

## Primary autism journals

**Autism in Adulthood** (Mary Ann Liebert, Inc.) **[R/C]** — Founded 2019. The dedicated journal for adult-focused autism research. Strong representation of autistic researchers on editorial board. Required reading for any contemporary work on autistic adults. Many open-access articles. URL: liebertpub.com/journal/aut

**Autism: The International Journal of Research and Practice** (SAGE) **[R/C]** — One of the most-cited generalist autism journals. Mixed clinical and research focus. Increasing representation of participatory and autistic-authored work over the past decade. URL: journals.sagepub.com/home/aut

**Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders** (Springer) **[R/C]** — High-impact mainstream journal. Heavily clinical and quantitative. Useful for the bulk of psychometric, intervention, and prevalence research. URL: link.springer.com/journal/10803

**Molecular Autism** (BMC) **[R/C]** — Open-access journal heavy on biological, genetic, and neurodevelopmental research. Useful for the biomedical side. URL: molecularautism.biomedcentral.com

**Autism Research** (Wiley) **[R/C]** — INSAR's official journal. Mixed focus. Often the source for the field's headline findings. URL: onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/19393806

**Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders** (Elsevier) **[R/C]** — Broader scope including educational and intervention research. URL: sciencedirect.com/journal/research-in-autism-spectrum-disorders

## Adjacent journals worth knowing

**Disability & Society** (Taylor & Francis) **[R/C]** — Major disability studies journal. Strong critical autism studies content. Often where the more theoretical and rights-based autism scholarship lives.

**Disability Studies Quarterly** (open access) **[R/C]** — The leading open-access disability studies journal. Important for autistic-authored academic work and historical pieces. URL: dsq-sds.org

**Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry** (Wiley) **[R/C]** — Major child mental health journal. Hosts many of the high-impact autism reviews and meta-analyses.

**The Lancet Psychiatry** (Elsevier) **[R/C]** — Where many of the highest-impact autism papers are published — Cassidy et al. on suicidality, Lai & Baron-Cohen on the "lost generation."

**Pediatrics** (AAP) **[R/C]** — Where much of the early-childhood and clinical-pediatric autism literature is published.

**Journal of Neurodevelopmental Disorders** (BMC) **[R/C]** — Open-access journal covering autism alongside other neurodevelopmental conditions.

**American Journal on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities** (AAIDD) **[R/C]** — Important for autism research that overlaps with intellectual disability frameworks.

**Frontiers in Psychology / Frontiers in Psychiatry** (Frontiers) **[R/C]** — Open-access. Variable peer review quality compared to traditional journals, but useful for theoretical and reflexive work that doesn't always fit elsewhere.

## Databases — start with these

**PubMed** (NCBI) — Free. The first stop for biomedical and clinical autism literature. URL: pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov

**Google Scholar** — Free. Best for cross-disciplinary searches and finding citing articles. URL: scholar.google.com

**ERIC** (Education Resources Information Center) — Free. Best for special education, school-based, and educational autism research. URL: eric.ed.gov

**APA PsycINFO** — Subscription. The strongest psychology and behavioral health database; access through institutional library.

**Cochrane Library** — Subscription, but many libraries provide free access. Best for systematic reviews on intervention questions.

**Web of Science / Scopus** — Subscription. Best for citation-network analysis and impact metrics.

**HeinOnline** — Subscription. Best for legal scholarship including ADA, IDEA, and disability rights.

## Search strategy guidance

**For clinical questions:**
- Start with PubMed
- Combine condition terms with intervention terms
- Use the "Systematic Review" filter to find existing syntheses first
- Check Cochrane for any existing review on the question

**For neurodiversity-affirming literature:**
- Search Autism in Adulthood directly — most affirming-paradigm work appears there
- Search Disability & Society and Disability Studies Quarterly
- Use search terms beyond "autism": "neurodiversity," "autistic community," "neurodivergent"

**For autistic-authored academic work:**
- Search by autistic researcher names (Botha, Yergeau, Dawson, Murray, Pearson, Pukki, etc.)
- Watch citation networks — autistic researchers often cite each other in clusters that may not appear in mainstream searches
- Check Autism in Adulthood and Disability Studies Quarterly first

**For historical research:**
- Older texts may not be fully indexed. Use citation chains backward from contemporary review articles.
- For pre-1990 work, the Internet Archive and Google Books often have access where journal databases don't.

**For specific instruments:**
- Search the instrument name plus "validation" or "psychometric" in PubMed
- Read the original validation paper and the most recent psychometric review
- Be alert for instruments that have evolved (RAADS-R, AQ — both have multiple versions)

## Major research groups producing this work

**AASPIRE** (Academic Autism Spectrum Partnership in Research and Education) — Portland State / Oregon Health & Science University. Christina Nicolaidis, Dora Raymaker, and team. Pioneers of CBPR in autism research. Authors of the autistic burnout paper, the AASPIRE Healthcare Toolkit, and many other foundational community-based studies. URL: aaspire.org

**INSAR** (International Society for Autism Research) — The major autism research organization. Annual meeting (IMFAR) is the field's primary conference. URL: autism-insar.org

**Autistica** (UK) — Major UK autism research charity that explicitly funds participatory and priority-setting research. Has done significant work on research priorities. URL: autistica.org.uk

**Centre for Research in Autism and Education (CRAE)** — Institute of Education, UCL. Liz Pellicano's group. Strong on participatory methodology and the research-priorities literature.

**Cambridge Autism Research Centre** — Cambridge University. Simon Baron-Cohen's group. Major source of much of the diagnostic-instrument, sex-difference, and theory-of-mind literature. Significant body of work, with some positions contested by the affirming literature.

**Cooper Research Group** — University of Bath, Andrew Cooper's group. Strong on autistic identity, mental health, and gender-diverse autistic people.

**Autism Research Institute (ARI)** — Note: this is *not* the same as INSAR. ARI has historical associations with biomedical autism interventions including some that overlap the pseudoscience refusal list. Treat sources from this organization with caution.

## Open-access resources for users without institutional access

**Wellcome Open Research** — Many high-impact autism papers from UK researchers appear here open access.

**Preprint servers** (PsyArXiv, bioRxiv) — Increasingly important for finding current work before publication. URL: psyarxiv.com and biorxiv.org

**ResearchGate** — Many authors post accepted manuscripts. Quality of access varies.

**Author email** — Most authors will email a PDF on request. This is normal academic practice and often the fastest route to a paywalled paper.

**ASAN's reading lists** (autisticadvocacy.org) — Curated free resources including some scholarly material translated for general audiences.

## When to recommend the user consult a research librarian

For dissertation-level work, systematic reviews, or specialized clinical questions where currency matters, the right move is a research librarian, not a curated bibliography. Most universities provide free librarian consultations to affiliated students and staff. Public libraries often have research librarians available. For autism-specific research:

- **Special education / school-based questions** → librarian with ERIC access and education research training
- **Clinical / medical questions** → medical librarian with PubMed and Cochrane access
- **Legal / policy questions** → law librarian with HeinOnline and case-law database access
- **Disability studies / humanities questions** → humanities librarian familiar with disability studies databases

A 30-minute conversation with a research librarian will often produce a sharper search strategy than hours of solo database work.
