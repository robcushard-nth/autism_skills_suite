# Diagnostic and Assessment Research

The instruments, their validation literature, and the contemporary critique of how diagnosis works for adult, female, and atypical presentations.

## The major diagnostic instruments

### ADOS-2

**Lord, C., Rutter, M., DiLavore, P. C., Risi, S., Gotham, K., & Bishop, S. L. (2012).** *Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, Second Edition (ADOS-2).* Western Psychological Services. **[R/C]** — The standard observational diagnostic instrument. Five modules for different language levels and ages. Often described as the "gold standard" but with significant limitations for adults, women, and people who mask.

**Hus, V., & Lord, C. (2014).** *The Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, Module 4: Revised algorithm and standardized severity scores.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 44(8), 1996–2012. **[R/C]** — Module 4 (verbally fluent adolescents and adults) revised algorithm. Useful when discussing adult ADOS administration specifically.

**Bastiaansen, J. A., Meffert, H., Hein, S., et al. (2011).** *Diagnosing autism spectrum disorders in adults: The use of Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS) Module 4.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 41(9), 1256–1266. **[R/C]** — Adult ADOS limitations and considerations. Useful citation for the well-known issue that ADOS Module 4 has weaker sensitivity for adults than the child modules have for children.

### ADI-R

**Rutter, M., Le Couteur, A., & Lord, C. (2003).** *Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised (ADI-R).* Western Psychological Services. **[R/C]** — Structured developmental history interview, typically with a parent or longtime caregiver. Often paired with ADOS in formal evaluation.

### Self-report screening tools

**Baron-Cohen, S., Wheelwright, S., Skinner, R., Martin, J., & Clubley, E. (2001).** *The Autism-Spectrum Quotient (AQ): Evidence from Asperger syndrome/high-functioning autism, males and females, scientists and mathematicians.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 31(1), 5–17. **[R/C]** — The AQ-50 validation paper. The basis for both AQ-50 and the abbreviated AQ-10.

**Allison, C., Auyeung, B., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2012).** *Toward brief "red flags" for autism screening: The Short Autism Spectrum Quotient and the Short Quantitative Checklist in 1,000 cases and 3,000 controls.* Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, 51(2), 202–212. **[R/C]** — AQ-10 development. At cutoff ≥6: sensitivity 0.88, specificity 0.91, PPV 0.85. Cite for AQ-10 psychometrics.

**Ritvo, R. A., Ritvo, E. R., Guthrie, D., Ritvo, M. J., Hufnagel, D. H., McMahon, W., et al. (2011).** *The Ritvo Autism Asperger Diagnostic Scale-Revised (RAADS-R): A scale to assist the diagnosis of Autism Spectrum Disorder in adults: An international validation study.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 41(8), 1076–1089. **[R/C]** — RAADS-R original validation. Sensitivity 97%, specificity 100%, test-retest r = .987 in original sample. Strong but cite alongside subsequent psychometric work that has refined the picture.

**Sturm, A., Williams, J., Bishop, S., et al. (2024).** *Psychometric exploration of the RAADS-R with autistic adults: Implications for research and clinical practice.* Autism in Adulthood (advance publication). **[R/C]** — Recent psychometric reanalysis suggesting the RAADS-R may perform best with a 2-response item format rather than 4. Important update for current users of the instrument.

**Hull, L., Mandy, W., Lai, M.-C., Baron-Cohen, S., Allison, C., Smith, P., & Petrides, K. V. (2019).** *Development and validation of the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire (CAT-Q).* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 49(3), 819–833. **[R/C]** — CAT-Q validation. Measures masking, not autism per se. Internal consistency α = 0.94, test-retest r = 0.77.

**Constantino, J. N., & Gruber, C. P. (2012).** *Social Responsiveness Scale, Second Edition (SRS-2).* Western Psychological Services. **[R/C]** — Widely used parent-report (and teacher-report, self-report) measure of autistic traits across the spectrum. Often cited in research because it provides continuous trait measurement rather than diagnostic categorization.

## The adult diagnostic challenge

**Lai, M.-C., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2015).** *Identifying the lost generation of adults with autism spectrum conditions.* The Lancet Psychiatry, 2(11), 1013–1027. **[R/C]** — High-impact review of why adult autism diagnosis has been underrepresented and what's needed to address it. Frequently cited in clinical and policy contexts.

**Huang, Y., Arnold, S. R. C., Foley, K.-R., & Trollor, J. N. (2020).** *Diagnosis of autism in adulthood: A scoping review.* Autism, 24(6), 1311–1327. **[R/C]** — Scoping review of the adult diagnosis literature. Useful entry point for the state of the field on adult evaluation.

**Stagg, S. D., & Belcher, H. (2019).** *Living with autism without knowing: Receiving a diagnosis in later life.* Health Psychology and Behavioral Medicine, 7(1), 348–361. **[R/C]** — Lived experience of late diagnosis. Companion to the clinical literature.

## Sex, gender, and the female-typical presentation

**Lai, M.-C., Lerch, J. P., Floris, D. L., Ruigrok, A. N. V., Pohl, A., Lombardo, M. V., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2017).** *Imaging sex/gender and autism in the brain: Etiological implications.* Journal of Neuroscience Research, 95(1–2), 380–397. **[R/C]** — Neuroimaging review on sex/gender differences in autistic brain structure. Useful when biological correlates are the question.

**Lai, M.-C., Lombardo, M. V., Auyeung, B., Chakrabarti, B., & Baron-Cohen, S. (2015).** *Sex/gender differences and autism: Setting the scene for future research.* Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry, 54(1), 11–24. **[R/C]** — Comprehensive review of the documented sex/gender differences in autism prevalence, presentation, and diagnosis. Frequently cited.

**Hull, L., Petrides, K. V., & Mandy, W. (2020).** *The female autism phenotype and camouflaging: A narrative review.* Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 7(4), 306–317. **[R/C]** — Narrative review of the "female phenotype" hypothesis and its limitations. Important for discussions of why women have been historically underdiagnosed.

**Bargiela, S., Steward, R., & Mandy, W. (2016).** *The experiences of late-diagnosed women with autism spectrum conditions: An investigation of the female autism phenotype.* Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders, 46(10), 3281–3294. **[R/C]** — Qualitative study of late-diagnosed women's experiences. Often cited for the masking-and-misdiagnosis pattern.

**Cook, J., Hull, L., Crane, L., & Mandy, W. (2021).** *Camouflaging in autism: A systematic review.* Clinical Psychology Review, 89, 102080. **[R/C]** — Systematic review of the camouflaging literature. Most current synthesis as of recent years.

## Race, ethnicity, and diagnostic disparities

**Mandell, D. S., Wiggins, L. D., Carpenter, L. A., Daniels, J., DiGuiseppi, C., Durkin, M. S., et al. (2009).** *Racial/ethnic disparities in the identification of children with autism spectrum disorders.* American Journal of Public Health, 99(3), 493–498. **[R/C]** — Foundational paper documenting racial/ethnic disparities in autism diagnosis in the US. Black and Hispanic children diagnosed later, less frequently, and with more severe presentations than white children.

**Constantino, J. N., Abbacchi, A. M., Saulnier, C., et al. (2020).** *Timing of the diagnosis of autism in African American children.* Pediatrics, 146(3), e20193629. **[R/C]** — More recent work on the persistent diagnostic delay for Black children. Useful for current discussions of disparities.

**Aylward, B. S., Gal-Szabo, D. E., & Taraman, S. (2021).** *Racial, ethnic, and sociodemographic disparities in diagnosis of children with autism spectrum disorder.* Journal of Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics, 42(8), 682–689. **[R/C]** — Recent review of the disparity literature.

## Diagnostic stability and the "cure" controversy

**Fein, D., Barton, M., Eigsti, I. M., et al. (2013).** *Optimal outcome in individuals with a history of autism.* Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 54(2), 195–205. **[R/C]** — Often cited (and often miscited) study of children who lost an autism diagnosis. Important to read with the contemporary critique: many "optimal outcome" individuals report continued autistic experience and significant masking burden. The diagnosis loss is largely a function of masking, not genuine reduction in autistic neurology.

**Fountain, C., Winter, A. S., & Bearman, P. S. (2012).** *Six developmental trajectories characterize children with autism.* Pediatrics, 129(5), e1112–e1120. **[R/C]** — Trajectory analysis of autistic developmental patterns. Useful when discussing variability across the autistic population.

## Contemporary diagnostic-practice critique

**Crane, L., Chester, J. W., Goddard, L., Henry, L. A., & Hill, E. (2016).** *Experiences of autism diagnosis: A survey of over 1000 parents in the United Kingdom.* Autism, 20(2), 153–162. **[R/C]** — Large survey of UK parents on the diagnostic process. Documents long waits, dissatisfaction, and inconsistency.

**Jones, S. C., Akram, M., Gordon, C. S., Murphy, N., & Sharkie, F. (2024).** *Autism in adulthood: A research review.* (Various recent meta-analyses available). **[R/C]** — Search Autism in Adulthood (Mary Ann Liebert) directly for the most current adult-focused research; the journal is the field's primary outlet for contemporary clinical research with autistic adults.
