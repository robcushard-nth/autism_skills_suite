# Sensory Regulation Plans

Practical templates for building personalized regulation toolkits. Read `autism-knowledge-core/references/sensory-and-regulation.md` for the framework before producing plans.

## Building a sensory profile

Walk the user through each of the eight sensory systems. Don't make it a clinical intake — make it a curious mapping conversation. Two questions per channel:

**Visual** — What kinds of light, motion, or visual environments fail you? What kinds restore you?
**Auditory** — What sounds break you? What sounds ground you?
**Tactile** — What textures, fabrics, temperatures, or contact are intolerable? What's comforting?
**Olfactory** — What smells are too much? Are there any that calm you?
**Gustatory** — What food textures, temperatures, or flavors do you avoid? What do you reach for?
**Vestibular** — How are you with movement, heights, motion sickness? Does spinning, rocking, or swinging help?
**Proprioceptive** — Do you seek deep pressure (weighted blankets, tight hugs, compression)? Or does it feel like too much?
**Interoceptive** — How well do you notice hunger, thirst, fatigue, need to use the bathroom, emotional shifts? What signals do you usually miss?

Output is a one-page profile the user can use as reference and share with people who need to know.

## Pre-event regulation plan template

For a known high-load event (family gathering, conference, wedding, performance, travel day):

**Before:**
- Sleep loaded the night before — minimum X hours
- No other sensory load that day if possible (no shopping, no errands, no other social)
- Eat a meal you know works (low novel sensory input, full nutrition)
- Pack the kit: [list what user needs — earplugs, sunglasses, fidget, water, snack, comfort item]

**During:**
- Identify the exit before going in (where is the bathroom, where is the parking, who knows you might leave)
- Set an internal time limit, even if not enforced
- Pre-plan one "I need to step out for a sec" line
- Permission in advance to leave at any point

**After:**
- Block the next [X hours / day] as recovery — no plans, no demands
- Stim freely
- Solo or low-demand company
- Sensory comfort menu: [what specifically helps this person recover — bath, weighted blanket, video game, walk, special interest deep dive]

## Environmental modification — home

Quick wins users can implement at home:
- Lighting: replace overhead fluorescents with lamps; consider warm-temperature bulbs; blackout curtains for sleep
- Sound: rugs and curtains absorb sound; white noise or pink noise as background; quiet appliances when replacing
- Tactile: consistent fabric choices for bedding, clothing in rotation; remove clothing tags or buy tagless
- Olfactory: scent-free cleaning products; control where food smells live; ventilation
- Visual: reduce visual clutter in rest spaces; consider a "low-stim corner" with controlled lighting and minimal pattern

## Environmental modification — work

What's negotiable with most employers:
- Move desk away from high-traffic, kitchen, printer
- Headphone policy for focus work
- Lamp instead of overhead lighting
- Camera-off default for internal calls
- Written meeting agendas
- Asynchronous communication for complex topics
- Permission to step out without explanation during sensory peaks

## Environmental modification — travel

Travel is a sensory worst-case for many autistic adults. Pre-plan:

**Airport:**
- TSA Cares program (US) or equivalent assistance program — request in advance for help navigating security
- Sunflower Lanyard (international) — visible signal of hidden disability, recognized in many airports for support
- Noise-canceling headphones, sunglasses
- Pre-determined quiet area at the airport (some airports have sensory rooms now)
- Pre-loaded entertainment/special interest content for the flight
- Snacks that work even when sensory state is bad

**Hotel:**
- Request quiet floor, away from elevator and ice machine
- Bring a familiar pillow, blanket, or comfort object
- Identify a regulation routine that works in unfamiliar space
- Pre-plan recovery time before any first event

**Conferences and large gatherings:**
- Schedule recovery hours, not just session breaks
- Identify a quiet hideout location (hotel room, empty meeting room, your car)
- Pre-arrange a buddy who knows the plan if traveling with someone
- Choose your social load deliberately — don't try to attend every event

## Communicating sensory needs to others

Scripts the user can adapt:

For a host or organizer:
> "Quick logistic — I do better at gatherings if I can step out for a few minutes occasionally. Don't read anything into it if I disappear for a bit; I'll be back. Also, [specific need: I'm sensitive to perfume / I don't do well in really loud rooms / I'll likely leave before the late part of the night]."

For a partner:
> "When I get quiet at events, that usually means I'm starting to overload. If you check in with me with a yes/no question — 'do you want to leave' — that's easier for me to answer than open ones. Don't take silence personally."

For a colleague at a work event:
> "I'm going to do the panel and then duck out — open networking is hard for me, no offense to anyone. Catch you Monday."

## When the plan breaks

Sometimes overload happens despite the plan. The plan didn't fail — the load was higher than predicted. Iterate, don't blame:

- What signal did you miss?
- What was different about today?
- What recovery worked / didn't?
- What would you adjust for next time?

This is how the toolkit gets sharper over time.
