# School and University

For the autistic teen or adult navigating their own education. Parents driving K-12 advocacy belongs in the parent-coach skill.

## High school — student-driven advocacy

If the user is a high school student, the parent or guardian likely owns the IEP/504 process legally. But the student's voice matters and increasingly so as they near graduation.

**What students can drive:**
- Requesting to attend their own IEP meetings (legally permitted; increasingly encouraged)
- Speaking to specific accommodations they need (and ones that aren't working)
- Pushing back on accommodations that target their behavior rather than their environment
- Self-advocacy with individual teachers — emails to clarify expectations, request seating changes, ask for written instructions

**Script for an IEP meeting where the student wants to speak:**

> "I want to share what's working and what isn't from my side.
>
> Working: [specific — the quiet testing room, the extended time, the sensory break pass]
>
> Not working: [specific — the social skills group I get pulled out for, the behavior chart, the requirement to participate verbally in class]
>
> What I'd add: [specific — written rubrics for assignments, a noise-canceling headphone option in the cafeteria, permission to leave class without explanation when overwhelmed]"

This works in roughly 80% of meetings. For the other 20%, the user may need the parent or an advocate to back up their requests.

**Transition planning** — required by US IDEA from age 16. This is where the IEP plans for after-graduation life: college, work, independent living. A student-led transition plan is dramatically better than one written by adults talking about the student. Push to be the lead author.

## College and university — disability services

Significant shift from K-12: the college doesn't proactively support you. You request accommodations through the disability services office, with documentation, and the office decides what's offered.

**The general process (US; UK and other systems differ but follow similar logic):**

1. Apply to the disability services office, often before the semester starts
2. Submit documentation — your evaluation report, a doctor's letter, or in some schools an evaluator's letter specifically formatted for disability services
3. Meet with a disability services advisor
4. Receive an accommodation letter listing what's approved
5. You bring the letter to each professor, ideally first week of class
6. The professor implements the accommodations

**Common college accommodations:**
- Extended time on tests (typically 1.5x or 2x)
- Quiet/private testing room
- Permission to record lectures
- Note-taking support
- Priority registration (helps with sensory-friendly scheduling)
- Reduced course load while staying full-time
- Single dorm room
- Emotional support animal accommodation in housing
- Flexibility on attendance for sensory or burnout days
- Extensions on assignments with advance notice
- Course substitution for specific requirements that pose disability barriers

**Less common, sometimes available:**
- Recorded lectures or transcripts as accommodation
- Lower-sensory exam environments (no fluorescent lights, etc.)
- Permission to step out of class as needed
- Alternative format presentations (written instead of oral)

## Email to a professor — first week

> Subject: Accommodation letter and quick intro
>
> Dear Professor [Name],
>
> I'm in your [course] this semester. I've registered with [Disability Services] and they've sent my accommodation letter — please let me know if you didn't receive it and I can resend.
>
> A few things from my end:
>
> - I learn best when slides or readings are available before the lecture, even in draft form
> - I tend to process before responding, so I may be quiet in class — that's not disengagement
> - I'll plan to use [extended time / private testing room / etc.] for exams
>
> If you'd like to chat in office hours about anything from my accommodation letter or how I can do well in your course, I'd appreciate that.
>
> Thanks,
> [Name]

## When professors push back

Some do. Common pushbacks and responses:

- **"I don't think you really need that accommodation in my class."** → "The accommodation is approved by Disability Services. I'd appreciate if you'd implement it. If you have concerns, please raise them with [DS office name]."
- **"This isn't fair to other students."** → "Accommodations exist to make access equal, not to give an advantage. I'd like to keep this between you, me, and DS."
- **"You don't seem autistic."** → "I'd rather not have a debate about that. The accommodations are approved. Can we move forward with implementing them?"

If pushback continues, escalate to disability services. They handle this routinely.

## Self-advocacy with academic advisors

What advisors should help with:
- Course selection considering sensory and cognitive load distribution across a semester
- Pacing — graduating in 5 years instead of 4 is reasonable for many autistic students
- Major selection if the original major isn't working
- Connection to autistic student groups, if they exist on campus

What advisors are not for:
- Therapy or mental health support — those go through the counseling center, ideally to a neurodiversity-affirming clinician
- Crisis support — those go to the 24/7 crisis line on campus or local resources

## Burnout in college

Common pattern: high school worked because of structure (parents, IEP, daily schedule). College removes the scaffolding and the autistic student crashes mid-semester or mid-second-year.

Signals:
- Stopped going to classes you were enrolled in
- Inability to start assignments you know how to do
- Social withdrawal beyond your usual baseline
- Sleep wreckage
- Sensory regulation breaking down (couldn't tolerate the dining hall anymore, etc.)

What to do:
- Tell disability services. They've seen this and can help with deadline extensions, withdrawal options, medical leave
- Consider a reduced course load
- Consider taking a semester off — it doesn't have to be a failure narrative
- Get connected to mental health support, ideally an autism-informed clinician
- Tell at least one trusted person in your life

## Independent living preparation

For users transitioning to college or after-graduation independent living, common gaps in preparation:
- Cooking with sensory-tolerable foods, on a schedule, when alexithymic
- Managing executive function without parental scaffolding
- Healthcare appointments without parental management
- Money management with time-blindness
- Maintaining hygiene routines without external prompts

These aren't failure points; they're skill-building areas. Practical tools: shared calendar with reminders, automatic bill pay, recurring grocery delivery for staples, OT consultation for life-skills work, peer support from older autistic adults.
