# Burnout Recovery

Autistic burnout is a structural overload state, not a mood disorder and not a long meltdown. Read `autism-knowledge-core/references/states-meltdown-shutdown-burnout.md` for the framework. This reference is the practical recovery work.

## First — confirm it's burnout

Before producing a recovery plan, distinguish:
- **Acute meltdown / shutdown** — short-duration, recovery measured in hours
- **Depression** — persistent low mood, anhedonia, may respond to standard treatment
- **Job burnout** — workplace-specific, resolved by changing jobs
- **Autistic burnout** — sustained loss of skills, increased sensory sensitivity, extended duration, caused by chronic overload

Often more than one is in play. Don't force a single label. The recovery work is similar enough that the labels matter less than the response.

Burnout-specific signals (from the autistic adult literature, especially Raymaker et al. 2020):
- Loss of skills you previously had (driving, cooking, executive function, speech, basic self-care)
- Increased sensory sensitivity beyond your usual baseline
- Chronic exhaustion that doesn't recover with normal rest
- Withdrawal from special interests
- Inability to mask, even when masking would be safer
- Onset traces to a period of sustained overload (life transition, unaccommodated work, chronic masking, family crisis, all of these compounding)

## The recovery framework

Burnout recovery is measured in months, sometimes years. The goal is structural change, not a quick reset.

Three phases that overlap:

### Phase 1: Demand reduction

The non-negotiable first move. The system is in overload. The only way out is reducing load.

What this looks like in practice:
- **Work** — leave, reduce hours, take medical leave (FMLA in US, statutory sick leave equivalents elsewhere), restructure to lower-demand role. This is often the largest move. Do not skip it.
- **Social** — drop optional commitments. Disappoint people. Cancel ongoing arrangements that are draining. Tell people: "I need to step back for a while. I'll be in touch when I can."
- **Domestic** — minimum viable household standards. Order food. Hire help if available. Use disposables if needed. Lower the bar dramatically.
- **Self-imposed obligations** — productivity goals, exercise plans, side projects, learning goals, social media presence. All on hold.
- **Sensory** — strict environmental control. Quiet, dim, predictable, temperature-controlled, scent-free if needed.

The instinct to maintain pre-burnout output through the recovery is the trap. The system needs the load off. Months of low load, not weeks.

### Phase 2: Restoration

After load is genuinely reduced (not just reduced in theory while still being borne):

- Sleep until rested, even if that's 12 hours a day for a while
- Eat food that actually works for your sensory state
- Sensory regulation freely — stim, special interest, weighted blankets, repetitive activities
- Solo or low-demand company
- Time outside if it's regulating; not if it's not
- Slow re-engagement with special interests if they returned
- Possibly a body care reset — hydration, vitamin D check, hormone check, GI check (these often deteriorate during burnout and need attention)

This phase is about replenishment, not productivity. You're not lazy. You're recovering.

### Phase 3: Re-evaluation and structural change

When the user starts to have capacity again, the question is: what conditions caused this, and what has to be different?

Almost always involves:
- Reduced ongoing demand load
- Better sensory environment
- Less masking
- Real recovery time built into routine, not borrowed at the end
- Different boundaries with family, work, or social commitments
- Sometimes a job change, relationship restructuring, or geographic move

If you return to the same conditions, you'll get the same outcome. The user's nervous system isn't broken; the previous load was unsustainable.

## Practical scripts

### Telling work

> "I need to take medical leave. I'll be out for [time period — 4 weeks minimum, often longer]. I'll be available for one transition meeting before I leave but not for ongoing communication during leave."

If FMLA-eligible (US), file the paperwork. If not, the conversation depends on the workplace. A doctor's note saying "medical leave required for [time period] for medical reasons" usually suffices for HR; the employer doesn't need the diagnosis.

### Telling family

> "I'm going through a hard period and I need to step back from [family events / regular calls / hosting / whatever's draining]. I'll be in touch. I'm not angry at anyone. This is about my capacity. I love you."

Brief. Not an explanation. Not an apology. Many family members will not understand and will push back. That's not a reason to re-engage.

### Telling friends

> "I'm in autistic burnout right now. I'm going to be quiet for a while — could be weeks, could be months. Please don't take it personally and please don't pull back from the friendship. I'll come back when I can."

The honest version works better than the "I'm fine" version. Friends who matter respond well.

## Common traps in recovery

- **Recovering enough to barely function, then resuming full load.** This is a setup for re-burnout. Push past "barely functioning" into actual restoration before re-engaging.
- **Pursuing productivity during recovery.** Reading the burnout literature is fine; turning it into a project is not.
- **Treating the recovery as a personal failure.** It's a structural overload state, not a moral one.
- **Standard mental health treatment as the only treatment.** SSRIs and CBT alone don't fix autistic burnout. They can help with co-occurring depression. They don't substitute for demand reduction.
- **Returning to full masking after recovery.** Recovery often involves unmasking partially or fully. Returning to full mask reproduces the conditions of burnout.

## When to bring in clinicians

Most users in autistic burnout benefit from at least:
- A primary care visit to rule out medical contributors (thyroid, B12, vitamin D, sleep disorders, GI issues, hormonal issues)
- An autism-informed therapist if accessible (rare but worth the search)
- A doctor willing to write the medical leave letter
- A psychiatrist if mood symptoms are severe or persistent — but specifically one who understands that autistic burnout is not the same as depression

If the user has suicidal ideation: see crisis resources in knowledge core. Burnout in severe form can produce suicidal thoughts. This requires the same crisis response as any other suicidality, with autism-informed framing if possible.

## Re-emergence

There's no specific date when recovery is "complete." Indicators that re-engagement is becoming possible:
- Skills you'd lost are returning
- Sensory tolerance is back to baseline (or you've adjusted environments enough that load is sustainable)
- Special interests have come back
- You're considering work or commitments without dread
- You can sustain low-demand social again

When this happens, re-engage gradually and with structural changes in place. Don't time it to external pressure. The body knows when it's ready.
