# Healthcare Self-Advocacy

Healthcare environments are sensorily and procedurally hard for many autistic adults. This reference covers preparing for appointments, choosing providers, communicating symptoms despite alexithymia, and managing emergency care.

## Choosing a primary care provider

What to look for:
- Will block longer appointments if you ask (15 minutes is often inadequate)
- Communicates by patient portal so you can write symptoms in advance
- Doesn't dismiss your reports
- Open to written information you bring in
- Doesn't insist on the office's preferred sensory environment as a precondition for care

What to avoid:
- Practices that require phone-based scheduling and won't use a portal
- Providers who interrupt or don't read what you've written
- Anyone who pathologizes your communication style

If currently with a provider who isn't working: change. The fit matters.

## Preparing for any appointment

What helps for most autistic adults:

**Before:**
- Write a summary of why you're going. Use a simple structure: symptoms (what), timing (when started, when it happens), severity, what makes it worse or better, what you've tried. Bring it printed or on phone.
- List your questions. Number them.
- Note your current medications, supplements, allergies.
- Plan logistics — parking, route, how long the appointment will run, what you're doing after.
- Bring sensory tools — sunglasses for waiting rooms, headphones, fidget, something familiar.
- Bring water and a snack.
- If possible, bring a person — for support, for backup memory, for advocacy.

**During:**
- Hand over the written summary if speaking is hard. Many providers welcome this.
- Take notes. Write down what the provider says.
- Ask for clarification when something doesn't land. "I want to make sure I understood — you're saying X?"
- If you don't understand a recommendation, ask. If you're not going to do something, say so honestly so the provider can offer alternatives.
- Permission to take a moment. "I need a minute to process that" is a complete sentence.

**After:**
- Recovery time. Appointments are sensorily and emotionally costly.
- Write down anything you remember from the appointment that you didn't capture during.
- Follow up via portal in writing if you have post-appointment questions or didn't capture something correctly.

## Communicating symptoms when alexithymic

If you have difficulty identifying or describing internal states, traditional symptom questions don't work well. Workarounds:

**Body-based questions you can ask yourself:**
- Where in my body do I feel something?
- Is it sharp, dull, pressing, burning, throbbing, electric, hollow, full?
- Is it constant or does it come and go?
- What position am I in when it happens?
- What was I doing right before?
- Has anything changed in the last few weeks — sleep, food, stress, hormones, weather?

**Tracking helps.** A few weeks of brief daily notes — body sensations, sleep, food, mood as colors or numbers if words don't work, anything notable — gives the provider real data and gives you a record that survives the appointment-day amnesia common with anxiety or alexithymia.

**Use comparisons.** "Worse than X, better than Y" is sometimes more accessible than absolute scales.

## Disclosure to medical providers

Whether to disclose autism in medical contexts:

- Strong yes for: anesthesia (sensory considerations, possibly different drug responses), pain management (sensory and interoceptive variations affect reporting), psychiatry (massive treatment differences), dental (huge sensory considerations), pre-surgery, ER triage if safe to do so
- Generally yes for: primary care, specialists you'll see repeatedly
- Optional for: one-time visits, low-stakes contexts

**A general script:**

> "Before we start — I'm autistic. A few things that affect how this appointment will go: I do better with written information, I may need clarification on questions, I'm sensitive to [specific sensory things], and I sometimes have trouble describing physical sensations in standard language. I have a written summary of why I'm here today."

Most providers respond well to this. The few who don't tell you something useful about whether to keep them.

## ER and urgent care

The ER is often the worst sensory environment in modern medicine — bright fluorescent light, alarms, multiple conversations, unpredictable timing, strangers approaching, wait times that can stretch for hours.

If ER becomes necessary:

**Bring:**
- Sunglasses, noise-canceling headphones (real ones, not earbuds)
- Phone charger and entertainment
- A snack and water
- A list of medications, allergies, current diagnoses
- An ID card or wallet card stating you're autistic, what helps, what doesn't (samples available from autistic advocacy organizations)
- A trusted person if at all possible

**At triage:**
- State the medical reason clearly and briefly
- Then: "I'm autistic. The waiting room is going to be very hard for me. Is there a quieter area I can wait in? Can you let me know roughly how long?"
- This often gets a quieter waiting area, sometimes a small room

**During care:**
- Be direct about needs ("the lights are causing me significant pain, can we dim them or can I wear my sunglasses")
- If you're losing speech, write or use phone text-to-speech
- If a procedure is going to happen, ask what they'll do step by step before they do it
- You can refuse anything until you understand it. That's your right.

**Mental health crisis presentation in ER:** Autistic adults presenting in mental health crisis face documented poor outcomes. Restraint, involuntary hold, and police involvement are real risks. If at all possible:
- Bring an advocate
- Have a crisis plan document with you (preferences, what helps, providers' contact info, accommodations needed)
- Consider mobile crisis teams as alternative — many US localities have them. 988 can route.

## Mental health providers

Finding an autism-affirming therapist is hard. Look for:
- Explicit statement on website or in their bio about working with autistic adults
- Better: explicit neurodiversity-affirming framing
- Best: the therapist is themselves autistic
- Open to written communication between sessions if helpful for processing
- Doesn't insist on eye contact, conventional emotional expression, or a particular communication style

Avoid:
- Providers who pathologize stimming or special interests
- Providers who don't know that autistic burnout is distinct from depression
- ABA-trained therapists working from a behaviorist frame
- Anyone using terms like "high-functioning" or framing autism as deficit

Resources for finding affirming providers:
- Therapist Neurodiversity Collective directory (US)
- Inclusive Therapists directory (US, broader inclusion)
- Psychology Today filter for "autism" with manual review of bios
- Autistic adult Facebook groups, Reddit communities, local groups for word-of-mouth

## Dental care

Often overlooked. For many autistic adults, dental care is one of the highest-cost healthcare experiences. Strategies:
- Look for dentists who advertise sensory-friendly or special-needs-friendly practice
- First appointment as orientation only — meet the dentist, see the room, no procedures
- Discuss sedation options for harder procedures
- Bring sunglasses (the overhead light is brutal)
- Ask for breaks during long procedures
- Consider sensory-friendly weighted blankets in chair if available

## When provider relationships break down

Sometimes a provider you've invested in isn't working. Signs to consider changing:
- Repeatedly dismissive of your reports
- Pressing for treatments you've declined
- Gaslighting your symptoms
- Not reading what you've written
- Defensive when you ask questions

You can change providers without explanation. "I've decided to find a different provider" is a complete sentence. Get your records released to yourself and to the next provider.
