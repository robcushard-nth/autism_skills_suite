# Workplace Scripts and Accommodations

This reference covers requesting accommodations, disclosure decisions, manager conversations, and navigating burnout at work — all written for the autistic adult driving the conversation themselves.

## The disclosure decision

Disclosure is not all-or-nothing and not irreversible-feeling-but-actually-irreversible. Frame it as a series of choices:

- **No disclosure** — request accommodations through medical or wellness channels without naming autism
- **Functional disclosure** — describe needs without naming a diagnosis ("I work best with written communication and a low-distraction environment")
- **Selective disclosure** — tell one trusted manager, HR rep, or close colleague
- **Diagnostic disclosure** — formal disclosure with documentation, often required for legally protected accommodations
- **Open disclosure** — autism is part of how the person presents at work

Each comes with different protections, different costs, and different benefits.

### Costs to weigh
- Stigma and bias in the specific workplace, sector, and country
- Effects on promotion, project assignment, perceived competence
- Some industries (defense, certain federal roles, security clearance contexts) have nuanced effects worth researching
- The "out of the bag" problem — once disclosed, it can't be un-disclosed in the same workplace

### Benefits to weigh
- Legal protection (ADA in US, Equality Act in UK, similar in other jurisdictions) requires disclosure to apply
- Stops the energy drain of hiding
- Creates space for accommodations
- Makes it possible to seek out other autistic colleagues and find community
- Disclosure is correlated with increased job satisfaction and decreased burnout for many autistic adults — not all

### Deciding factors
- Stability of current role
- Quality of relationship with manager and HR
- Whether accommodations are needed urgently or could wait
- Whether the user is in a sector with cultural openness vs. resistance
- The user's own preference about being known

## Accommodation request — without disclosure

A request frame that names the function without the label:

> Subject: Adjustments to support my best work
>
> Hi [Manager],
>
> I've been thinking about what helps me do my best work, and I wanted to flag a few things that would make a real difference. None of these affect deliverables — they're about how I work.
>
> 1. **Written-first communication for complex topics.** When we have a multi-part decision to make, an email or doc with the question stated up front lets me respond more thoroughly than a real-time meeting.
> 2. **Agenda-first meetings.** A line or two on what we're solving sent a few hours ahead helps me come prepared.
> 3. **Camera-off as default for internal meetings.** I find I can focus better and follow more.
> 4. **A quieter desk location** if a move comes up. Open-plan adjacent to high-traffic areas is hard for sustained focus work.
>
> Happy to talk through any of this. None of it is urgent — just wanted to put it on your radar.
>
> [Name]

Note the structure: small list, framed as "how I work best," not "what I struggle with." Keep it normal-toned. Most managers respond well to this format.

## Accommodation request — with disclosure

When disclosure is the chosen path, the same content with the diagnostic context added:

> Subject: A few accommodations I'd like to discuss
>
> Hi [Manager],
>
> I want to share some context and ask for a few adjustments. I'm autistic. It hasn't changed since I started here, but a few specific accommodations would let me work more sustainably and produce better.
>
> [Same list as above]
>
> I'm not looking to change my role or output — these are work-style adjustments. I'd appreciate keeping this between us and HR for now. If you'd prefer to involve HR formally for documentation, I'm comfortable with that too.
>
> [Name]

Tradeoffs spelled out:
- "Hasn't changed since I started" lowers the threat response
- "Not looking to change my role" pre-empts the "should we move you" reaction
- Confidentiality preference stated clearly
- HR option offered, not demanded

## Common accommodations that help autistic adults

- Written instructions for complex tasks
- Advance agendas for meetings
- Camera-off option in video calls
- Reduced-distraction workspace (private office, quieter area, headphones policy)
- Flexibility in working hours (especially morning rigidity, time zone for remote)
- Reduced or restructured meeting load
- Clear written feedback on performance, not vague "vibes"
- Defined response time expectations (e.g., "I respond to non-urgent emails within 24 hours" stated openly)
- Working from home for some or all of the week
- Permission to leave the building / step out during sensory overload moments
- Stim tools at desk (fidget, putty, headphones)
- Lighting modifications (lamp instead of overhead fluorescent, dim mode on monitor)
- Sensory-friendly office event option (quiet hours, smaller groups)

## When disclosure goes badly

It happens. Common patterns:
- Manager becomes overprotective and reduces challenging work — frame back to them: *"I want to be clear: I'm not asking for less responsibility. The accommodations are about how I work, not what I work on. Please keep stretch projects coming."*
- HR escalates to a formal process the user didn't want — *"I appreciate the offer of formal accommodations, but I'd like to start informal. We can revisit if it's not working."*
- Colleagues find out and become awkward — script: *"Yeah, I'm autistic. Mostly it shows up in [whatever they're seeing]. It doesn't need to change much about how we work together."*
- Performance issues get attributed to autism — push back: *"I want to separate two things. The accommodations help me work sustainably. If there's a performance concern, let's talk about it on its own terms with specifics, not framed through my diagnosis."*

## Burnout at work

Distinguish autistic burnout from being-tired-of-this-job. Signals of true burnout:
- Loss of skills you previously had at this job
- Sensory load that used to be tolerable now isn't
- Recovery doesn't happen on weekends or short PTO
- Masking is starting to break down involuntarily
- You're considering leaving the workforce, not just the job

What helps:
- Real time off — multi-week, not a long weekend
- Demand reduction at home alongside work, not just at work
- Possibly medical leave (FMLA in US, statutory sick leave equivalents elsewhere)
- A re-evaluation of whether the job's demands are sustainable for you, not whether you can push harder

What doesn't:
- Pushing through, "just hit Q4 and then you can rest"
- Self-care lifestyle advice (yoga, gratitude journals) without environmental change
- A new productivity system

## Returning from leave

If the user is returning from medical leave or burnout time off:
- Negotiate a phased return if possible (60% time for two weeks, then 80%, then full)
- Get accommodations in writing before returning, not after
- Identify the demands that broke you and which can be permanently modified
- Set a check-in cadence with the manager that's calendar-blocked, not "let me know how it goes"
