# School Advocacy

Beyond the IEP itself — the relationship work, the implementation work, and knowing when to escalate or change schools.

## Building the teacher relationship

The single highest-leverage move in K-12 advocacy. A teacher who understands your child and is on your team makes the difference between a working year and a broken one.

**At the start of the year:**
- Send a short introduction email before the first day. Two paragraphs. What works for your child, what doesn't, what you're hoping for, what you can offer (you respond fast, you can come in for events, you make good cookies — whatever's true).
- Offer to meet briefly in the first two weeks
- Keep the tone collaborative, not transactional
- Don't lead with the IEP — lead with the kid

**Sample intro email:**

> Subject: Quick intro — [Child]'s family
>
> Hi [Teacher],
>
> I'm [Child]'s parent. I wanted to reach out before the school year really gets going.
>
> A few things that help [Child] do well: [2–3 specific things — written instructions, advance warning of changes, permission to take a sensory break, a clear weekly schedule]. A few things that are harder: [1–2 specific things — group projects without role clarity, surprise schedule changes, loud assemblies].
>
> [Child] is autistic. They have an IEP that I'm sure you've seen — happy to walk through it together if useful. They're [bright, kind, intensely interested in [interest], working hard], and most of what they need is already in the plan.
>
> I'm an easy partner. Please email me anything that comes up. I won't be defensive. I'd rather know.
>
> Thanks for what you're doing this year.
> [Parent]

**During the year:**
- Quick positive note when something goes well
- Quick neutral note when you have a question
- Less-is-more on volume; pick what to flag
- Show up for parent-teacher conferences with two questions and an offer

**When something goes wrong:**
- First move is curiosity, not accusation. "Hey, [Child] came home upset about X. Can you tell me what happened?"
- Then collaboration. "Here's what I think would help — what's possible from your end?"
- Reserve the formal-complaint mode for when collaboration fails

## When the teacher is the problem

Sometimes the teacher isn't a partner. Signs:
- Repeated failure to implement IEP accommodations
- Comments about the child that suggest the teacher doesn't believe in autism, doesn't believe in accommodations, or pathologizes autistic behavior
- Behavior reports framed as defiance with no consideration of sensory or communication factors
- Refusing to meet, refusing to communicate, or being hostile in communication
- Reports from your child of the teacher being mean, sarcastic, or punitive

What to do:
1. Document specifics in writing — dates, what happened, what was said
2. Email the teacher and copy the case manager. Stay factual. Reference the IEP.
3. If unresolved within 2 weeks, request an IEP meeting to address the issue
4. If still unresolved, escalate to principal in writing
5. Consider requesting a different teacher for the next year, or sooner if severe

The case for changing teachers mid-year: rare but sometimes appropriate when the relationship is genuinely toxic and damaging the child. Schools resist this; persist if the situation is severe.

## Working with administrators

Principals and assistant principals can either accelerate or block accommodations. Approach them as a partner who has authority you'd benefit from working with, not as an adversary.

When you need administrator involvement:
- IEP not being implemented across multiple teachers
- Schoolwide policies that conflict with IEP (uniform requirements, attendance, lunch policies)
- Bullying or peer issues
- Restraint or seclusion incidents
- Suspension or discipline issues

Default tone for admin emails: factual, calm, specific, asking a clear question. Avoid global accusations. Avoid emotional language even when warranted.

## Bullying and peer issues

Autistic kids are bullied at significantly elevated rates. The school's standard anti-bullying policies often don't fit autistic targets — the kid may not register or report bullying in standard ways, social cues that an allistic kid would catch get missed, and "just stand up to them" advice fails differently.

What helps:
- A trusted adult at school who the child can go to without being asked first
- Lunch and recess accommodations (alternative seating, alternative location, supervised group)
- Direct teaching to peers about autism and difference, age-appropriately, with the child's consent
- Documentation of incidents in writing to the principal
- For severe or persistent bullying, a 504 amendment or IEP amendment to address it formally
- For incidents that meet the legal definition, formal harassment complaint under federal law

What doesn't help:
- "Just ignore them"
- Mediation between the bully and your child as the first response
- Telling your child to be "less of a target"
- Generic anti-bullying assemblies
- Punishing the bullied child for the response (meltdowns, fighting back, school refusal)

## School refusal

When the child stops going or won't go. Almost always a signal that the environment has become intolerable for them. Common drivers:

- Bullying or social anxiety
- Sensory overload that's accumulated
- Burnout
- Specific teacher or class
- Untreated co-occurring condition (anxiety, OCD, depression)
- Recent change at school
- Transition stress (especially after vacation, illness, or a major life change)

What to do:
- Don't force-march back without addressing the cause
- Investigate the cause — talk with the child, not at them; talk with the teacher; observe at school if possible
- Address the cause concretely (move classes, modify schedule, implement accommodations, address bullying)
- Negotiate with school for a reduced-day return, modified schedule, or temporary homebound instruction if needed
- Bring in clinicians for mental health support if relevant
- Consider whether the school is the right placement long-term — sometimes the answer is changing schools

What doesn't:
- Punishment for not going
- Bribery for going
- Lectures about importance of education
- Standardized "school refusal" interventions that ignore the specific cause
- Continuing to push the child into an environment that's actively harming them

## Knowing when to leave the school

Sometimes the right answer is changing schools. Signs:
- Repeated, documented failure to implement IEP across years and teachers
- Hostile administrative response to advocacy
- Bullying that the school won't address
- Child's mental health is deteriorating in this environment
- The school's culture is fundamentally incompatible (heavy-discipline, conformity-focused, religious or competitive culture that punishes neurodivergence)

Options:
- Different school in the district (request transfer)
- Charter school (varies enormously; some are good for autistic kids, some are worse than the district)
- Private school with autism-specific or neurodivergent-friendly approach
- Specialized school (when the child has high support needs or specific profile)
- Homeschool, including secular curricula, hybrid programs, microschools, virtual options
- Unschooling for a recovery period, especially after burnout

The choice depends on resources, what's available locally, what the child wants, and what the family can sustain.

## Restraint and seclusion

Many states still permit physical restraint and seclusion (locking the child alone in a room) in schools. Both have caused serious harm and trauma to autistic students.

Things to do proactively:
- Specify in the IEP that restraint and seclusion are NOT to be used with your child
- Document any incidents immediately in writing
- Know your state's restraint/seclusion laws
- File a formal complaint with the state for any incident that violated procedure or your IEP

If your child is being restrained or secluded:
- This is an emergency-level situation
- Demand to see all incident reports
- Consider an attorney
- File a state complaint
- Consider whether continuing in this school is safe for your child

## Schoolwide change vs. one-kid change

Some parents become advocates for schoolwide change — autism awareness, sensory-friendly classrooms, restraint reform, dress code modification, mental health support increases. This is meaningful work and often beneficial for everyone, including your child.

It's also energy-intensive. The decision to do schoolwide advocacy or stay focused on your own kid is a real one. Both are valid. Don't pressure yourself into systemic work when the kid in front of you needs you.
