# Sleep and Feeding

Two of the most common, most exhausting, and most misunderstood domains in autism parenting. Both have real biological and sensory drivers; neither is fixable through willpower or behavior plans.

## Sleep

Sleep differences are extremely common in autistic children and adults. Patterns include:
- Difficulty falling asleep, often regardless of fatigue
- Frequent night waking
- Very early waking (3–5 AM is not unusual)
- Unusual sleep cycles (later sleep, irregular timing)
- Sleep that doesn't restore — long sleep but still tired
- Sleep regressions tied to growth, hormones, environmental changes

### Common drivers

- **Sensory environment.** Light, sound, temperature, texture of bedding, smells. Often the room that an allistic family member finds normal is intolerable for the autistic child.
- **Interoceptive issues.** The child may not register tiredness, may not register hunger or thirst that's preventing sleep, may not register bladder before getting in bed.
- **Anxiety and rumination.** Especially in older kids and teens.
- **Co-occurring conditions.** ADHD-related delayed sleep onset, GI issues that wake them, hormonal differences (including melatonin-pathway differences documented in autistic kids).
- **Schedule mismatches.** Some autistic kids have strong evening or morning preferences that conflict with school schedules.
- **Environmental change.** New room, new house, new sibling, schedule change, season change.

### What helps

**Environmental:**
- Blackout curtains, even in northern winter (some kids respond to small amounts of dawn light)
- White noise, pink noise, or fan if sound matters
- Specific bedding the child has chosen — texture, weight, temperature
- Weighted blankets if recommended by an OT (use age-appropriate weights; avoid for young children without guidance)
- Cool room temperature (most children sleep better cool, and many autistic kids strongly so)
- Reduced visual stimuli (limit posters, declutter, store toys in closed bins)
- Mattress comfort that matches their sensory profile

**Routine:**
- Predictable bedtime sequence — same order, same activities, same length
- Visual schedule of the bedtime sequence if useful
- Wind-down period of low-input activity before bed (no bright screens for some kids; specific calming screens for others)
- Special interest time before bed for some kids; not for others
- Body care needs met (bathroom, water, snack if needed)

**Body:**
- Adequate physical activity during the day (movement-seeking kids especially)
- Sensory regulation through the day so they're not arriving at bedtime over-loaded
- Appropriate dinner — not too late, not too sensory-difficult
- Bath if regulating; not if not (varies enormously by kid)

**Pharmacological:**
- Melatonin under medical guidance — common, often helpful, dosing is lower than typical OTC bottles suggest. Discuss with pediatrician.
- For more significant sleep issues, sleep medicine evaluation. Sleep studies if needed.
- Other medications under medical guidance only.

### What doesn't help

- "Cry it out" methods designed for neurotypical infants. Often catastrophic for autistic kids and can damage attachment.
- Behavioral sleep training that punishes leaving the bed
- Generic sleep hygiene advice that doesn't account for the specific sensory profile
- Exercising the child to exhaustion as the primary strategy
- Removing all special interest access in the evening as a control mechanism

### When to bring in clinicians

- Sleep that's affecting the child's daytime functioning significantly
- Sleep that's affecting the parent's mental health or functioning
- Sudden new sleep issues (rule out medical: ear infection, GI, hormonal, sleep-disordered breathing)
- Suspected sleep apnea (snoring, gasping, witnessed pauses) — autistic kids have elevated rates of sleep-disordered breathing
- Failure of environmental and routine strategies after a real attempt

## Feeding

Eating differences are nearly universal in autistic kids. The patterns range from mild preference to ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder).

### Common patterns

- **Strong texture preferences.** Crunchy, smooth, dry, wet — often very specific
- **Brand specificity.** The same yogurt; that exact brand; a different package looks different even if the food is identical
- **Temperature specificity.** Only cold; only room temperature; nothing too hot
- **Color or shape specificity.** No green; no mixed foods; foods can't touch
- **Smell sensitivity.** Foods rejected before tasting based on smell
- **Pickiness around novelty.** New foods are presumed unsafe until proven otherwise; introduction is slow
- **Loss of previously-eaten foods.** Especially during sensory peaks or stress
- **Strong attachment to safe foods.** A small set of foods consumed reliably
- **Eating only at specific places, with specific people, in specific contexts**

### What's actually happening

Several things, often layered:

- **Sensory processing.** Texture, temperature, smell, taste, and visual properties of food are processed at higher intensity. Foods that allistic eaters experience as neutral can be intensely unpleasant.
- **Interoceptive differences.** The child may not reliably register hunger, fullness, or specific food cravings. Eating happens by external cue (mealtime) rather than internal signal.
- **Routine preference.** Same foods feel safe; novel foods carry risk.
- **GI issues.** Many autistic kids have actual GI conditions (constipation, reflux, IBS, eosinophilic disorders) that make eating uncomfortable in ways they can't always articulate.
- **Oral motor issues.** Some kids have actual difficulty chewing certain textures.
- **Anxiety and trauma around food.** Past forced-eating experiences, gagging, choking can produce lasting aversion.

### What helps

**Sensory and emotional safety:**
- Don't force foods. Forcing creates lasting aversion and trauma.
- Don't hide foods in other foods. Erodes trust and safety.
- Don't bargain ("two bites of broccoli and you can have dessert"). Frames eating as a battle.
- Stock and serve safe foods reliably. Hunger does not reliably override sensory aversion in many autistic kids; chronic underfeeding follows.
- Permission to eat the same food repeatedly. Variety is not nutritionally required; nutritional adequacy is.
- Permission to leave the table.
- Permission to eat alone, away from family, or at off-times if those are sensory-easier.

**Slow expansion:**
- New foods presented without pressure to eat. On the plate, near other food, available.
- Food chaining — moving from accepted foods to similar foods in tiny variations (same brand, slight flavor change; same texture, slight color change)
- Cooking together if the child enjoys it; not if they don't
- Shopping with the child for food choices
- The child as authority on what they will eat

**Body care:**
- Pediatrician check-ups with attention to growth curves and nutrition
- Multivitamin if intake is restricted
- Iron, vitamin D, B12 testing if indicated
- GI evaluation if symptoms suggest it (constipation, reflux, weight loss)
- Dental care given that texture preferences sometimes correlate with reduced oral hygiene

**Feeding therapy decisions:**
- An OT or SLP feeding evaluation can be useful for genuine eating challenges
- Avoid programs based on extinction (not eating preferred foods until non-preferred are eaten), forced exposure, or reward charts
- Look for SOS (Sequential Oral Sensory) approach, responsive feeding, or division-of-responsibility approaches
- Avoid feeding programs that make the child cry, gag, or refuse to attend

### What doesn't help

- "Just keep offering" without any environmental change
- Reward charts for eating
- Removing safe foods to "force" expansion
- Pediatricians who treat picky eating as a behavior problem to extinguish
- Diets framed as autism cures — GFCF as treatment, ketogenic for autism, specific carbohydrate diet, etc. Some autistic kids have actual food sensitivities (celiac, EoE) that warrant elimination diets under medical supervision; this is not the same.
- Stress around mealtimes that contaminates eating across the long term

### When restriction is severe

ARFID-pattern eating where the child eats fewer than 10 foods, has lost weight, or is showing nutritional deficiency requires medical attention. Approach:
- Pediatrician first to rule out medical causes
- Registered dietitian familiar with ARFID and neurodivergence
- Feeding therapy with a responsive (not extinction-based) approach
- Sometimes psychological support for food-related anxiety or trauma
- Sometimes nutritional supplementation while the longer work continues

Most importantly: don't assume the kid is being difficult. ARFID is real and a child with ARFID needs care, not discipline.

## School lunch

A specific challenge. School cafeterias are sensory hellscapes for many autistic kids — loud, bright, crowded, smell-laden, time-pressured. Even kids who eat well at home often eat poorly or not at all at school.

What helps:
- Pack what they will actually eat. Nutritional balance over months, not single meals.
- Permission to eat in a quieter location (resource room, library, classroom). Add to IEP.
- Permission to bring their own food rather than school food
- Lunch box that protects sensory preferences (insulated, non-leaking, contents kept separate)
- Don't worry about social eating during lunch — for many kids, lunch is decompression, not social

What doesn't:
- Forcing them to eat in the cafeteria
- Forcing them to eat school food
- Punishing not eating at lunch
- Worrying about the variety of school lunch over the variety of home lunch

## When you can't make it work

Some periods, despite all of this, sleep or feeding will be objectively bad for a while. The kid is in a regression, an illness, a transition, a hormonal shift, a stress period.

Hold the framework. Lower expectations to match reality. Don't blame yourself or the kid. Bring in clinicians if needed. The hard period passes more often than parents fear.
