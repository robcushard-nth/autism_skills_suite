# AAC and Communication

AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication) is communication. It is not a therapy goal, a stepping stone to speech, or a consolation prize. Nonspeaking and minimally speaking autistic people communicate — through behavior, through gesture, through facial expression, through AAC systems, through typing. Your job as a parent is to support communication in whatever form it takes, not to extract speech.

## Validated AAC vs. contested methods

### Validated AAC

**Picture-based systems — PECS and beyond.** PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System) is a common entry point. It teaches structured requesting: the child hands over a picture card to get something. Strength: it builds an initiating communication habit with a clear physical exchange. Limitation: PECS is structured around requests, not communication broadly. A child using PECS to request snacks is communicating, but they also need vocabulary for comments, protests, questions, feelings, and stories. If PECS is the whole system rather than a starting point, the child's communication is artificially capped at "I want."

**Speech-generating devices (SGDs).** Dedicated devices and tablet apps that produce spoken output when the user selects symbols or types. Major systems include Proloquo2Go (symbol-based, widely used on iPad), TouchChat with Word Power (robust vocabulary, motor-planning-friendly layouts), and LAMP Words for Life (Language Acquisition through Motor Planning — consistent motor patterns so words are always in the same place). Strength: rich vocabulary, expandable, spoken output that communication partners hear. Limitation: requires access to the device at all times — a device in a backpack or locked in a desk is not a communication system.

**Low-tech core boards.** Laminated boards with core vocabulary (go, stop, want, more, done, help, like, don't like, I, you, it) that the child points to. No batteries, no charging, no breakage. Strength: always available, waterproof, cheap to produce, good backup for tech failures. Limitation: smaller vocabulary, no spoken output, requires a communication partner who's paying attention.

**Partner-assisted scanning.** For individuals with significant motor differences who can't reliably point or select, a communication partner points to options and the AAC user signals (eye gaze, vocalization, gesture) when the partner hits the target. Strength: works with minimal motor demand. Limitation: slow, heavily dependent on a skilled partner, and the partner's assumptions can shape the output if they're not careful.

### Facilitated Communication (FC)

Refused. FC involves a "facilitator" supporting the nonspeaking person's hand or arm while they type or point to letters. Controlled studies — where the facilitator and the autistic person are shown different information — consistently show the communication originates from the facilitator, not the autistic person. There are documented cases of FC producing false abuse allegations and overriding the autistic person's actual communication. This is not the same as validated AAC. FC is on the pseudoscience refusal list for this suite (see `../../../autism-knowledge-core/references/pseudoscience-refusal-list.md`).

### RPM and S2C

Rapid Prompting Method (RPM) and Spelling to Communicate (S2C) occupy contested ground. The autism research community has not validated these methods through controlled studies that demonstrate the communication originates from the speller rather than the prompter. Some autistic adults who use these methods report they are authentic communication and have used them to produce substantial written work. The methodological problems exist on both sides — studies are small, poorly controlled, or absent.

If you are considering RPM or S2C, approach with eyes open. Prioritize methods where independent typing — without a communication partner holding or stabilizing the letterboard — is verifiable and the goal. Ask practitioners directly: "What does your plan look like for fading support until my child is typing independently?" If the answer is vague or defensive, that's information.

## Aided language input — the central concept

Aided language input means modeling on the AAC device while you speak. You tap symbols on the device at the same time you talk. This is how AAC users learn their communication system — the same way hearing children learn spoken language: by being immersed in it.

The operational rule of thumb: you should produce models on the device at a rate roughly similar to the rate of spoken language you produce. If you spend 30 minutes talking to your child, you should spend those same 30 minutes also modeling on the device. Not every word — model the key words, especially core vocabulary (go, want, stop, more, like, help, feel, look).

**What this looks like during a typical activity:** You're making a snack together. You say "Let's get the crackers" while tapping GET on the device. "Do you WANT cheese or peanut butter?" — you tap WANT, then CHEESE, then PEANUT BUTTER. Your child reaches for the cheese. You model: "You WANT CHEESE. GOOD choice. I LIKE cheese too" — tapping WANT, CHEESE, GOOD, LIKE, CHEESE.

You are not quizzing them. You are not asking them to repeat. You are showing them what the words look like on their device while using those words naturally. Over time, they start using those same motor patterns to communicate on their own. This works. It is the single highest-leverage thing you can do to support an AAC user's communication growth.

## Vocabulary organization principles

AAC vocabulary splits into **core** and **fringe**. Core vocabulary is the small set of words that make up the majority of everything anyone says — I, you, want, go, stop, more, like, help, not, make, get, put, look, do, feel, that, here, there. Fringe vocabulary is the topic-specific stuff — dinosaur names, school subjects, family member names.

A robust AAC system has core vocabulary on the front page, always accessible, always in the same location. Motor planning matters: if words move around every time the vocabulary is reorganized, the user has to re-learn where everything is. That's like rearranging someone's keyboard every week.

Two vocabulary design failures that create plateaus:

1. **Request-only vocabulary.** If the device only has "I want [food/toy/activity]," the child's communication is capped at requesting. They can't comment, protest, ask questions, tell jokes, describe feelings, narrate stories, or say no. Request-only systems make the child look less competent than they are.

2. **Buried verbs and descriptors.** If getting to "feel" or "think" or "funny" or "scared" requires navigating three folder levels, the child won't use those words in real-time conversation. Communication is time-sensitive. Core words belong on the main page.

## Diagnosing a plateau

If your child's AAC use has stalled, work through this checklist before assuming they've hit a ceiling:

- Are adults around your child modeling on the device daily, multiple times per hour during interaction?
- Is the vocabulary structured for communication beyond requests — can they comment, protest, ask, describe, narrate?
- Is the motor pattern stable — are words in the same place every time, or has the layout been reorganized recently?
- Are communication partners waiting long enough for your child to compose? (Count to 15 silently before jumping in. Then wait longer.)
- Are they being asked to demonstrate skills they already have ("show me the apple") vs. being given genuine reasons to communicate ("what do you want to tell me about your day")?

If the answer to any of these is no, the plateau is in the environment, not in the child. Fix the environment first.

## Presume competence

Operationally, presuming competence means:

- Assume your child understands more than they can currently express. Speak to them age-appropriately — not in simplified baby talk, not in third person, not past them to another adult.
- Offer real choices, not performative ones. "Do you want the red cup or the blue cup" is a real choice. "Do you want to do your homework" when the answer can only be yes is not.
- Do not talk about your child in front of them as though they aren't there. This includes IEP meetings, doctor's offices, and family conversations. If they're in the room, they're in the conversation.
- When they communicate something unexpected or inconvenient, take it seriously. A child who uses their AAC device to say "NO" is communicating successfully. That's the goal working.

## Evaluating SLPs

Not all speech-language pathologists are trained in AAC, and not all who claim AAC expertise practice in ways that support autistic communication. Questions to ask:

- **"Do you model on the device yourself? How often?"** A good answer describes regular aided language input during sessions. A red flag is an SLP who only prompts the child to use the device but doesn't model on it themselves.
- **"What's your stance on prompting and forcing use?"** You want an SLP who teaches through immersion and modeling, not one who physically prompts hand-over-hand or withholds desired items until the child "uses their words."
- **"How do you set communication goals — what does a good month look like?"** Good goals are about expanded communication functions (commenting, protesting, narrating), not just compliance or correct symbol selection.
- **"Are you trained in aided language input?"** Direct question. The answer should be yes with specifics.
- **"How do you handle plateaus — what do you reach for first?"** A good SLP reaches for environmental changes — more modeling, vocabulary restructuring, partner training. A red flag is reaching for behavioral compliance strategies.

Red flags in answers: emphasis on compliance, prompting hierarchies, withholding until the child performs, goals focused on speech production rather than communication, unfamiliarity with the child's specific AAC system, or treating AAC as a bridge to speech rather than as communication in its own right.

## How to push back without losing the relationship

If an SLP recommends compliance-driven approaches, forced sentence use, or prompting hierarchies that override your child's agency:

**Requesting a change in approach:**
> "I'd like us to shift toward more modeling-based approaches. Can we set a goal for this month where you increase aided language input during sessions and we reduce hand-over-hand prompting? I want [child] to see the device used naturally, not just be directed to use it."

**When the approach isn't shifting:**
> "I appreciate the work you've done with [child]. I'm looking for an SLP whose primary approach is aided language input and communication-function expansion rather than prompted use. Can you recommend someone, or should I look through the ASHA directory for AAC specialists in the area?"

If you're navigating this in a school setting, the IEP is your leverage. AAC goals and SLP service delivery are written into the IEP — you have standing to request changes in approach, not just changes in hours. See `references/iep-504-toolkit.md` for the meeting strategy and escalation path.

## AAC and behavior

When an AAC user has "behaviors" — meltdowns, aggression, self-injury, refusal — the first question is whether the AAC system is meeting their actual communication needs. A child who can only request food and toys but can't say "I'm scared," "that's too loud," "I need to stop," or "I don't understand" will communicate those things through behavior, because behavior is the communication channel still available to them.

Before building a behavior plan, audit the communication system. See `references/behavior-as-communication.md` for the full framework — and run every behavioral concern through the chain there with the additional question: "Does this child have the communication tools to express what they're trying to communicate through behavior?"

If the answer is no, the intervention is communication support, not behavior management.
