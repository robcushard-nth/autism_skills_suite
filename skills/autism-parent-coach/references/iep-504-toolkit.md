# IEP and 504 Toolkit

US-focused (the IEP and 504 framework). For other countries, the principles transfer but the legal framework differs — note this when working with non-US parents.

## The two frameworks

**IEP (Individualized Education Program)** — under IDEA. For students whose disability adversely affects educational performance and who require specialized instruction. Provides specially designed instruction plus accommodations and services. Most autistic students who qualify for special education services have IEPs.

**504 Plan** — under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. For students with disabilities who need accommodations to access the general education environment but don't require specialized instruction. Generally easier to obtain, less detailed, less protective.

A child may qualify for either. IEP provides more services and stronger legal protection. 504 is faster to obtain and simpler to maintain. Many families start with a 504 and move to an IEP if needed; some maintain a 504 if the child's needs are well-met by accommodations alone.

## Eligibility process

The school district has 60 days (in most states) from receiving a written eligibility request to complete the evaluation. The clock starts when you submit the request **in writing** — verbal requests don't trigger the timeline.

**Sample request letter:**

> [Date]
>
> [Principal Name]
> [School Name]
>
> Dear [Principal],
>
> I am requesting a comprehensive evaluation under IDEA for my child, [child's name], grade [X], to determine eligibility for special education and related services.
>
> [Child's name] has been showing [specific concerns — sensory overload at school, difficulty with transitions, communication differences, social difficulty, executive function challenges, recent regression, etc.]. I have attached [outside diagnostic reports, doctor's letters, prior evaluations] for reference.
>
> Please confirm receipt of this request and provide the evaluation timeline. I understand that I will be invited to participate in the evaluation planning meeting and to receive a copy of the evaluation results before the eligibility meeting.
>
> Thank you,
> [Parent name]
> [Contact info]

**What to attach:**
- Outside diagnostic evaluation if you have one
- Doctor's letter recommending evaluation
- Examples of work products or behavioral notes from school
- Prior school records showing concerns

## The IEP meeting

What's in an IEP, in the order it's typically discussed:

1. **Present Levels of Performance** — current academic and functional performance, with data
2. **Annual Goals** — measurable goals with progress monitoring frequency
3. **Special Education Services** — what specialized instruction the child receives
4. **Related Services** — speech, OT, PT, counseling, AAC support
5. **Accommodations and Modifications** — how the general environment is adjusted
6. **Assistive Technology** — devices and software
7. **Placement** — where services are delivered (general ed with push-in, resource room, self-contained, special school)
8. **Transition Plan** — required from age 16, addresses post-secondary life

## Common accommodations to request

This list is for parents to consider, not a checklist to copy wholesale. Match to the actual child.

**Sensory:**
- Sensory breaks on demand (not requiring permission each time)
- Access to a sensory regulation room or quiet corner
- Sunglasses or hat in cafeteria/gym/assemblies
- Headphones permitted (noise-canceling, sound-reducing, music if regulating)
- Seating away from high-traffic areas, doors, windows, fluorescents
- Modified lighting (lamp instead of overhead, dimmable area)
- Permission to wear preferred clothing (no uniform requirement when there's sensory difficulty)
- Alternatives to sensory-difficult environments (skip pep rally, alternative gym activity, alternative cafeteria seating)

**Communication:**
- Written instructions for assignments
- Visual schedules
- AAC device access throughout the day
- Permission to use written communication when speech is hard
- Email access to teachers for clarification
- Pre-warning before being called on (or no cold-calling)

**Executive function:**
- Extended time on assignments
- Chunked assignments (broken into smaller pieces)
- Written rubrics with explicit criteria
- Check-ins on long-term assignments
- Planner or schedule support
- Permission to record lectures

**Testing:**
- Extended time (typically 1.5x or 2x)
- Quiet room for testing
- Reading instructions aloud
- Reduced number of items per page
- Frequent breaks

**Social and behavioral:**
- Skip group projects or modify the role
- Permission to leave classroom for regulation
- Identified safe adult/space at school
- Communication system between home and school (daily note, weekly check-in, app)
- No requirement for verbal class participation
- Lunch in alternative location

**Transitions:**
- Advance warnings for changes in routine
- Schedule of teacher absences when known
- Transition support during class changes
- Alternative arrival/dismissal times to avoid crowds

## What to push back on

Common things schools propose that parents should question:

- **Behavior plans focused on extinguishing autistic behaviors.** Stimming, eye contact, hand flapping, rocking, scripting, repetitive interests — these are not problems to solve.
- **Social skills groups that teach masking.** Look at what's actually being taught. "Eye contact and not interrupting" is masking instruction.
- **Time-outs as a response to sensory or communication-driven behaviors.** Punishment for autistic communication causes harm.
- **Goals like "decreases stimming" or "engages in more eye contact" or "indistinguishable from peers."** These are masking goals.
- **Restraint and seclusion clauses** — read these carefully. Seclusion is locking a child in a room alone. Some states allow it; most autistic adults who experienced it report severe trauma. Ask whether less restrictive alternatives have been tried; document any incidents.
- **Reduction in services because the child is "doing well."** Often the child is doing well because the services are working. Reduction is often premature.
- **Home as the place the child should "make up for it."** Homework loads that consume entire evenings, behavior charts that punish home for school behavior, weekend assignments that erase decompression time.

## Specific language for asks

### Asking for a quiet testing space
> "Add to the IEP: [Child] will take all assessments in a quiet, low-distraction setting separate from the general classroom, with the option of noise-canceling headphones."

### Asking for sensory breaks
> "Add to the IEP: [Child] will have access to sensory breaks of up to [10] minutes, [up to X times per day], without requiring teacher permission for each instance. The break may be taken in [identified location]."

### Asking for AAC access
> "Add to the IEP: [Child]'s communication device [specify] will be available throughout the school day, including unstructured times (lunch, recess, transitions). All staff working with [Child] will be trained in supporting AAC use. AAC access will be considered equivalent to verbal communication for participation, assessment, and behavioral interpretation."

### Asking for extended time
> "Add to the IEP: [Child] will receive [1.5x / 2x] extended time on all classroom assignments, tests, and standardized assessments where extended time is permissible. Extended time will be scheduled in a separate quiet location when possible."

### Asking for reduced or modified homework
> "Add to the IEP: Homework will be reduced to [specific cap, e.g., 30 minutes per night maximum] to account for [Child]'s school-day cognitive load and need for after-school decompression. Assignments will be modified to align with this time, not extended into evening."

### Asking for after-school decompression accommodation
> "Add to the IEP: Family communication is recognized as occurring after school decompression time. Teachers will not expect parent response to communications received after school hours until the next school day, and [Child]'s after-school decompression time is acknowledged as part of the educational accommodation plan."

## Meeting strategy

**Before:**
- Get the draft IEP in advance — request it 5 business days before the meeting
- Read it carefully; mark what to add, remove, or modify
- Bring outside reports, work samples, communication logs
- Bring an advocate if available, especially for first IEPs or contested ones
- Decide which 3–4 items are non-negotiable for this meeting; let smaller items go to a follow-up

**During:**
- Take notes
- If something isn't clear, ask
- If you disagree, say so and request the change
- If you don't have an answer right now, say "I'd like to take this back and respond by [date]"
- You don't have to sign at the meeting — you can take it home, review, and sign later

**After:**
- Get a copy of the signed IEP
- Calendar the next review date
- Begin tracking implementation — accommodation in the IEP doesn't mean accommodation in practice
- Document any failures to implement (in writing, dated, specific)

## When implementation fails

The accommodations are written; the teacher isn't using them. Common pattern.

Step 1: **Email the teacher.** Specific, dated, factual. *"On [date], [child] was [denied a sensory break / made to read aloud / not given the visual schedule]. The IEP requires [specific accommodation]. Please confirm how this will be addressed going forward."*

Step 2: If unresolved, **email the case manager and copy the principal.** Same factual tone. Reference the IEP page and accommodation language.

Step 3: If still unresolved, **request an IEP team meeting** to address implementation failures.

Step 4: If still unresolved, **file a state complaint or due process.** This is the legal escalation. Most parents don't need to get here; some do. An advocate or attorney is strongly recommended at this stage.

Document everything in writing. Verbal conversations don't survive a dispute.

## Outside evaluations

Sometimes the school's evaluation is inadequate or biased. Parents have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense if they disagree with the district evaluation. The district must either pay for the IEE or file due process to defend their evaluation.

Outside evaluations can also support specific service requests — speech-language for AAC, OT for sensory profile, neuropsychological for academic supports.

## When to bring in advocates and attorneys

Special education advocates (non-attorney): worth considering for any IEP, especially for first IEPs, transitions to new schools, contested evaluations, or implementation disputes. Often paid hourly; some work pro bono.

Special education attorneys: needed for due process, mediation, or formal disputes. Some states require attorneys to be paid by parents win-or-lose; others have fee-shifting provisions if the parent prevails.

How to find them:
- Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates (COPAA) directory
- State protection and advocacy organization (P&A) — every state has one, free for low-income families
- Local autism parent groups, especially around special education
- Word of mouth from other parents

Resist the impulse to hire an attorney as the first move. Most situations resolve at lower escalation levels. But know when to escalate.
