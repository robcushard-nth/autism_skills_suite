# Parent Self-Care

The parent's own wellbeing is not optional and not a side note. Parents of autistic kids have measurably elevated rates of burnout, depression, anxiety, marital strain, and social isolation. The reasons are structural, not personal failure. This reference is about the parent's own life.

## What you're carrying

Even before naming any specific stressor, parents of autistic kids often carry:
- A higher cognitive load than allistic-parenting peers (anticipating, accommodating, advocating)
- More medical, educational, and service-coordination work
- More social interpretation and translation
- More public-encounter management
- More disrupted sleep, often for years
- More financial stress if services are out-of-pocket
- More relationship friction if partners disagree
- More extended family friction
- More isolation from peer parents who don't share the experience
- Sometimes, secondary trauma from advocating against systems that hurt the child

This is real. Naming it is the first step in addressing it.

## Parent burnout

A measurable, well-documented phenomenon. Distinguished from being-tired-of-parenting:

- Loss of capacity for tasks you previously handled
- Exhaustion that doesn't recover with normal rest
- Emotional flatness or numbness toward your own kids
- Increased irritability or rage
- Sleep disturbances
- Loss of interest in things that previously mattered
- Inability to enjoy time with your kids
- Withdrawal from your own friendships and activities
- Sometimes, intrusive thoughts about leaving or harm

Parent burnout is not a character defect. It's a structural state caused by sustained excess load.

What it requires:
- Real reduction in demands, not "self-care" advice
- Outside support — partner, family, paid help, community, therapy
- Sometimes, medical evaluation (thyroid, B12, vitamin D, hormones, sleep)
- Mental health support if depression is significant
- Permission to be a less-good parent for a period while you recover

What doesn't help:
- Advice to "make sure you take time for yourself" without addressing what's eating the time
- Lifestyle interventions that add to the workload (a new exercise routine, a meditation app, a journaling practice) on top of unaddressed structural overload
- Productivity advice
- Comparison with parents who appear to be coping better
- Being told you're being a martyr or a saint

## Marriage strain

Common. The diagnosis adds load to a relationship that was already running on the typical load of partnership. Compounding factors:

- Different processing speeds for the diagnosis (one partner ahead of the other)
- Different views on services, schools, ABA, etc.
- Different distribution of who does the autism-related work
- Different attitudes toward the autistic child's behavior
- Differences in how each partner connects with the child
- Sleep deprivation from years of disrupted sleep
- Reduced couple time
- Reduced sex
- Resentment patterns building over time

What helps:
- Couples therapy, ideally with a therapist familiar with disability and special needs parenting
- Direct conversations about distribution of work, with concrete renegotiation
- Each partner getting some time without the other partner that's actually theirs
- Real time together as a couple, even short and frequent rather than long and rare
- Vulnerability — telling your partner what's actually hard, not just managing the logistics
- Sometimes, a separation or divorce conversation that ends up clarifying the relationship rather than ending it

What doesn't:
- Pretending things are fine
- Letting one partner do the autism work alone
- Taking your stress out on each other indirectly
- Making the autistic child the problem in your marriage

## Single parents

If you're parenting solo (by choice, separation, death, or other circumstances), the load is real and the buffering is reduced. What helps:

- A small but reliable support network — even one or two people you can call
- Outsourcing what you can — meal services, cleaning, school logistics, anything that's available
- Lower expectations of yourself than partnered parents have
- Connection to other single parents of autistic kids (online communities help)
- Therapy if accessible
- Real childcare backup for emergencies, illness, mental health days

What doesn't:
- Pretending you can match a two-parent household
- Refusing help out of pride
- Building everything around the kid while abandoning your own life

## Isolation

Parents of autistic kids often become isolated. Why:
- Hard to attend social events with the kid (sensory, schedule, behavior)
- Hard to attend social events without the kid (childcare difficulty, exhaustion)
- Friends with allistic kids stop relating to your life
- Family events become hard
- Public outings get reduced because they're costly
- Special interest hobbies fall away from time pressure
- Online community is real but doesn't replace embodied presence

What helps:
- Other parents of autistic kids — local groups, online communities. The relief of being understood.
- Maintaining one or two friendships pre-autism that you defend
- Finding ways to socialize that work — coffee instead of dinner, walks, parallel activities
- Hobbies that fit the available capacity, even if smaller than before
- Therapy as a real social-relational space
- Faith communities or other communities that meet you where you are
- Sometimes, intentionally making new friends in the post-diagnosis life

What doesn't:
- Waiting until things calm down to invest in social life
- Trying to maintain the social calendar of a non-autism-parent
- Refusing to be honest with friends about your life
- Performing "we're doing great" in every interaction

## When the parent is also autistic

Increasingly common, increasingly recognized. Many parents identify their own autism through their child's diagnosis. Implications:

- The parent is experiencing autistic life with the additional load of parenting an autistic child
- Sensory environments that strained you before are now harder
- Social load is higher (school events, medical appointments, advocacy meetings)
- Burnout risk is elevated
- The parent's own past — masking, late identification — may surface during the child's diagnosis
- Marriage dynamics may shift

What helps for autistic parents specifically:
- Address your own accommodations and sensory environment, not just the kid's
- Therapy with an autism-affirming therapist
- Connection to autistic adult community, not only autism parent community
- Honest conversation with partners about your needs alongside the kid's
- Permission to mask less in your own home
- Sometimes, formal evaluation for yourself if you want it
- Letting your own diagnosis be its own thing, not subordinate to the child's

## Mental health

Rates of depression and anxiety in parents of autistic kids are well above population baseline. Drivers include the structural factors above plus:
- Fear about the child's future
- Grief that doesn't resolve once and stay resolved
- Trauma from medical and educational system encounters
- Isolation
- Pre-existing vulnerabilities now under more load

When to bring in a clinician:
- Persistent low mood for weeks
- Anxiety that's interfering with daily function
- Sleep wreckage that doesn't resolve with environmental change
- Suicidal thoughts (immediately)
- Heavy substance use as coping
- Inability to feel anything for your kids
- Persistent sense that you can't go on

What helps:
- Therapy with a clinician familiar with disability parenting and grief
- Medication if indicated, evaluated and prescribed appropriately
- Treatment for any specific co-occurring issues (PTSD, OCD, eating issues)
- Real social and structural support, not just clinical treatment

If suicidality arises: see crisis resources in `autism-knowledge-core/references/crisis-resources.md`. Parental suicide is a real risk factor and not to be quietly carried.

## What you don't owe

A few things parents of autistic kids are told they should provide that they don't owe:

- Constant cheerful resilience for public consumption
- Inspirational posts, narratives, or framing
- Education of every relative, friend, or stranger
- Defense of the diagnosis to skeptics
- Compliance with every recommended intervention
- Apology for accommodations
- Performance of "autism mom"/"autism dad" identity if it doesn't fit you
- Putting your child's life on display for sympathy or community
- Sacrificing your own life entirely

You owe your child your love, your honest effort, your accommodation, your respect, your protection, and a parent who is alive and present. You owe yourself a life that includes more than caregiving.

## What sustains the long run

The parents who sustain across years and decades tend to have:
- Realistic expectations of the load
- A small reliable network of support
- Real ongoing connection to autistic adults and to other parents
- Mental health support when needed
- A life outside the caregiving — work, hobbies, friendships, marriage, or whatever they choose
- Acceptance of the kid as they are, not as a project
- Acceptance of themselves as imperfect parents doing real work
- Time. The intensity does change, in either direction at different ages, and the cumulative experience builds wisdom and capacity over time.

You're doing harder work than people who haven't done it understand. Take seriously that the work is hard. Take seriously that you matter too.
