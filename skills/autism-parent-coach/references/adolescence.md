# Adolescence (13–18)

The autistic adolescent is doing developmental work — autonomy, identity, sexuality, future-planning — on top of the usual autistic life of sensory regulation, social translation, and executive function. The parent's role shifts from primary support to co-pilot, then to consultant.

## What changes in adolescence

- Autonomy needs increase. Treatment of the teen as a capable agent matters more than ever.
- Identity work — autistic identity, gender, sexuality, political and religious identity, vocational identity. Often intense, often more vulnerable in autistic teens than in allistic peers.
- Mental health risk increases. Depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, eating disorders, self-harm. Many autistic teens experience their first mental health crisis in this window.
- Social complexity peaks. The high school social environment is one of the hardest social environments most people will ever experience.
- Romantic and sexual development. Often delayed, often less mediated by peer culture, often more vulnerable to predation or to misreading consent dynamics.
- Future stakes become real. College decisions, work, driving, independent living plans.

The parental temptation is to tighten control to manage the increased risks. The actually-effective move is to invest in the relationship so the teen comes to you with real information, while quietly building the structural supports that catch them when needed.

## Communication retrofit

If the relationship in early childhood was based on parent leading, that pattern needs to update. With an autistic teen:

- Direct communication often works well. Drop the indirection.
- Written communication (text, shared docs, notes) often works better than verbal for hard topics. Use it.
- Don't require eye contact. Many of the best conversations with autistic teens happen in cars, on walks, while doing a parallel activity.
- Don't push for emotional disclosure they can't access. Body-based check-ins often work better ("how is your sleep, how is your eating, anything hurt").
- Respect the special interest. A teen who's still talking with you about their interests is staying connected.
- Don't fish. They'll tell you what they're ready to tell you.

## Mental health watch

Suicide risk in autistic teens is significantly elevated above population baseline. Multiple studies have found 7–10x elevated rates of suicidal ideation, suicide attempts, and suicide deaths among autistic adolescents and young adults.

Risk factors that compound:
- Bullying history
- Sustained masking
- Burnout
- Co-occurring depression or anxiety
- Gender dysphoria, especially in unsupported environments
- Family rejection of autistic identity, sexuality, or gender
- Disconnection from autistic peers and resources
- Recent transitions (school change, family change)

What to watch for:
- Withdrawal beyond their usual baseline
- Loss of interest in special interests
- Sleep wreckage in either direction
- Giving things away
- References to "burdening people," "everyone would be better off," "I won't be a problem much longer"
- Sudden calm after a depressive period (sometimes a warning sign of decision)
- Increased risk-taking behaviors

What to do:
- If you're concerned, ask directly. "Are you thinking about hurting yourself or ending your life?" Asking does not increase risk; not asking misses opportunities.
- If yes: see crisis resources in `autism-knowledge-core/references/crisis-resources.md`. Take seriously. Connect with mental health support immediately.
- Mental health support should be autism-affirming if possible. Generic therapy often misses the autistic-specific drivers.
- Reduce load while assessing — school accommodations, activity reduction, social pressure decrease.
- If you're considering hospitalization: ER environments are very hard for autistic teens, and psychiatric hospitalization can itself be traumatic. If safety is immediate, do what's needed; if not immediate, mobile crisis teams or outpatient intensification may be safer.

## Sexuality and relationships

Autistic teens often experience delayed or atypical sexual development. They also experience disproportionate vulnerability to sexual exploitation, sexual assault, and unwanted pregnancy, in part because:
- Social cues that signal danger are often missed
- The "fawn" response under pressure is common
- Compliance training (from ABA, from school discipline systems, from family dynamics) can override the no-saying instinct
- Sex education has not historically addressed neurodivergent learners well

What helps:
- Comprehensive, explicit, written sex education from a neurodivergent-affirming source
- Direct teaching about consent, including "you can stop or leave any time," "you don't owe anyone anything," and concrete scripts for refusing
- Open conversation about your teen's actual orientation, identity, and interests — not your assumptions
- Affirmation of LGBTQ+ identities, which co-occur in autistic populations at well-above-population rates
- Connection to broader queer and autistic communities where applicable

What doesn't help:
- Abstinence-only education
- Assuming your teen is straight, cisgender, or uninterested in relationships
- Surveillance instead of education
- Denying that your teen is a sexual being

## Driving

Highly variable for autistic teens. Some drive well; some never drive comfortably; some drive only in specific conditions. The decision to learn to drive should be the teen's, not a checkbox parents impose.

Considerations:
- Sensory load (mirrors, sound, motion, decision pressure) is high
- Executive function load (multitasking, prioritization, prediction) is high
- Co-occurring ADHD significantly affects driving safety
- Anxiety can be a real factor
- Some teens benefit from extended learning timelines, more structured practice, or specialized driving instruction for neurodivergent learners

If your teen wants to drive:
- Allow longer than the standard timeline
- Practice in low-stakes environments (empty lots, quiet roads) extensively before traffic
- Consider a driving instructor with neurodivergent experience
- Build in recovery time after lessons
- Don't push — both for safety and for the teen's relationship with driving long-term

If your teen doesn't want to drive:
- That's a valid choice
- Plan for transit access, ride-hailing, family logistics, possibly relocation later to transit-accessible cities

## Post-secondary planning

Required as part of the IEP from age 16 (US, per IDEA). The transition plan should be substantive and student-led.

Possible paths:
- 4-year college, residential or commuter
- 2-year community college, often as transfer pathway or terminal
- Vocational program or trade school
- Apprenticeship
- Specialized post-secondary programs for autistic students (some excellent, vary by region)
- Direct to work
- Gap year(s) to recover, mature, and plan
- Supported living programs with employment integration

The decision factors:
- The teen's actual interests and capacity
- Sensory and social capacity for residential vs. living-at-home college
- Executive function capacity for self-managed academics
- Mental health stability
- The family's resources

Don't let the question be "where will you go to college" until you've established the question is "what comes next that fits you."

## Driving the IEP toward transition

The transition section of the IEP should include:
- Course of study (what classes get them where they want to go)
- Post-secondary education or training goals
- Employment goals
- Independent living goals
- Specific transition services (including community-based instruction, work experiences, self-advocacy training)
- Connection to adult services (vocational rehabilitation, supported employment, etc.)
- A summary of performance document at graduation

The teen should be the lead author of this. The skill of self-advocacy in IEP meetings is itself a transition skill — practice it.

## Identity work

Autistic teens are doing the same identity work as all teens, intensified. The parent's role:

- Hold space without forcing disclosure
- Affirm whatever they share — identity, orientation, religion, politics, future plans
- Don't take changes personally
- Connect them to autistic community where possible
- Provide resources without lectures
- Trust they're working through it on their timeline

Common identity territories in autistic adolescence:
- Autistic identity itself — coming into the language, finding autistic creators online, sometimes intense identification followed by integration
- Gender — autistic and gender-diverse co-occurrence is well above population rate
- Sexual orientation — same
- Political and ethical identity, sometimes intense
- Religious identity — often involves deep examination, sometimes leaving family religion, sometimes a strong spiritual path of their own
- Vocational identity — what kind of work, what kind of life

## When the relationship gets hard

It often does in adolescence. Not always — but commonly. Patterns:

- The teen who's closed up
- The teen who's angry
- The teen who's depressed and unreachable
- The teen who's making decisions you disagree with
- The teen who's struggling with substance use, eating, or self-harm
- The teen who's gone silent on autism while figuring it out

Strategies:
- Don't surveil. Their autonomy is real even if you're scared.
- Maintain low-stakes contact — meals together, drives, parallel activities — without forcing depth.
- Make their room and their door respected territory.
- Apologize when you mess up, specifically and without elaboration.
- Get your own support so you're not bringing all your fear to the relationship.
- Trust them, in the specific sense of believing they have wisdom you don't yet see.

## Letting them lead

The single biggest shift in this period is from the parent leading the autistic life to the teen leading it. Your role becomes:
- Logistics and resources
- Emergency response
- Trusted consultant when asked
- Steady relationship

Done well, the parent of an autistic teen is moving toward the parent of an autistic adult — someone the adult child can rely on without being managed by. That's the goal of this stage.
