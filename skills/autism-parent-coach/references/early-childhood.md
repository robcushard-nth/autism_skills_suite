# Early Childhood (0-5)

The years from infancy through preschool. Often when concerns first surface, when evaluations happen, and when the choices made set the developmental trajectory and the family's relationship with the autism world.

## Common first concerns

What parents often notice in this age range, in rough order of frequency:
- Speech delay or atypical speech development (gestalt language, scripting, late onset, sudden loss)
- Sensory differences — extreme reactions to sound, light, texture, food, clothing
- Eating differences — extreme food selectivity, ARFID-pattern restriction
- Sleep differences — difficulty settling, frequent waking, very early rising, irregular cycles
- Social differences — limited or unusual eye contact, reduced response to name, strong preference for solitary or parallel play, atypical joint attention
- Strong specific interests or attachments
- Movement differences — toe-walking, hand-flapping, spinning, lining up, repetitive movements
- Difficulty with transitions and changes in routine
- Regression — losing skills (especially language) typically between 18 and 24 months
- Family history of autism, ADHD, or related profiles

Any one of these alone is not diagnostic. Patterns matter. Parents who notice patterns are usually right to investigate.

## When the pediatrician says "wait and see"

Common and often wrong. If you have concerns, you have the right to evaluation regardless of pediatrician comfort.

What to do:
- Request a referral to a developmental pediatrician, child psychologist, or autism evaluation center directly
- Self-refer to early intervention (Birth-3 in US; equivalents elsewhere) — most states allow self-referral
- Get on waitlists immediately even while continuing to investigate; waits are often 6–18 months
- Document specific concerns in writing
- Switch pediatricians if the current one is dismissive

What you don't have to do:
- Wait for the pediatrician to refer
- Justify your concerns past a basic level
- Argue with anyone about whether your child "seems autistic enough"
- Accept "girls don't really get autism" or "they make eye contact, so probably not" or other myths

## Early intervention (US: Birth–3)

A federally funded program with state-level implementation. Free to families who qualify. Provides evaluation, services, and a plan (IFSP — Individualized Family Service Plan).

Common Birth-3 services:
- Speech-language therapy
- Occupational therapy
- Developmental therapy
- Family training and support
- Sometimes physical therapy
- AAC support if needed

What to look for in providers:
- Neurodiversity-affirming framing
- Child-led, play-based approach
- Comfortable with stimming, special interests, and atypical communication
- Respectful of the child's autonomy and pace
- Open communication with parents

What to avoid:
- Compliance-based behavior approaches
- "Indistinguishable from peers" as a goal
- Suppression of stimming
- Forced eye contact

The transition from Birth-3 to school-based services happens at age 3. The school district takes over, and the planning shifts to an IEP if the child qualifies. This transition is administrative and worth preparing for — request a transition meeting at least 90 days before the third birthday.

## Preschool and pre-K decisions

Several paths:
- **Public Pre-K with IEP** — services delivered through the school system; quality varies by district
- **Private preschool with outside services** — preschool plus separately arranged speech, OT, etc.
- **Specialized program** — autism-specific or special-education preschool, public or private
- **Home or hybrid** — staying home with services, sometimes with co-op or part-time enrichment
- **Inclusive preschool with explicit autism support** — increasingly available, often the strongest match

Decision factors:
- The child's current capacity for group settings
- Sensory tolerance for the environment
- Quality of the specific program (visit, observe, watch how staff interact with kids)
- Family logistics
- Cost and availability

Whatever you choose, watch for signs the choice isn't working: meltdowns increase, regression, sleep wreckage, a child who used to enjoy the place now resists going, signs of distress at drop-off that don't resolve in a few weeks.

## ABA decision in early childhood

The most common intervention recommended in early childhood, often the only one insurance covers. Many parents arrive in this skill having been told ABA is the gold standard.

Read `autism-knowledge-core/references/pseudoscience-refusal-list.md` for the framing. The short version for parents:

- ABA's evidence base is weaker than its reputation suggests
- Many autistic adults who experienced early-childhood ABA report harm: trauma symptoms, learned compliance that disabled their later self-protection, suppressed stimming, masking, eating issues, identity damage
- Modern "play-based" or "naturalistic" ABA branding doesn't necessarily fix the core issue; some practices are better than others, and the variation across providers is large

If a family has been told they need ABA:
- Ask about alternatives: occupational therapy, speech-language therapy with neurodiversity-affirming clinicians, DIR/Floortime, SCERTS, Hanen, AAC support, sensory integration, child-led play approaches
- If insurance only covers ABA, sometimes finding a clinically affirming OT/SLP combination meets the same need; sometimes the family decides limited ABA from a specific provider with explicit constraints (no compliance training, no stim suppression, no extinction)
- Ask any provider directly: how do you handle stimming? How do you handle a child saying no? What are your goals — and do they include "indistinguishable from peers"? Look for affirming answers.
- Watch the child. If a child is regressing, becoming more anxious, or losing skills during ABA, the intervention is not working regardless of what providers report.

## Communication development

Speech is one channel, not the goal. The goal is communication.

If a child is delayed in spoken language:
- AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication) is appropriate and beneficial. Picture systems, speech-generating devices (Proloquo2Go, TouchChat, LAMP), early letter boards. AAC does not delay or prevent speech — research consistently shows it supports language development.
- Speech therapy with a neurodiversity-affirming clinician helps. Avoid clinicians whose primary framework is compliance or whose goal is suppressing scripting.
- Don't withhold AAC waiting for speech to "develop on its own." A child who can't communicate becomes frustrated, falls behind socially, and may develop behaviors that get pathologized. Communication first.

Gestalt language processing — many autistic children acquire language in chunks (whole phrases) rather than word-by-word. This is normal autistic language development. Reframe scripting as communication, not as deficit. Look up the work of Marge Blanc and natural language acquisition for this framework.

## Sensory and feeding

Sensory differences in early childhood are intense and often the source of most "behavior" reports. See `references/sleep-and-feeding.md` for the deep dive on those topics.

Quick orientation:
- Sensory needs are real, not fussy. Adapt environments, clothing, food, lighting, schedules to match the child.
- ARFID-pattern eating in early childhood is common. Don't force foods. Don't make food a battle. Get an OT or SLP feeding evaluation rather than a behavioral feeding program.
- Sleep issues are also common. Address sensory factors first, melatonin under medical guidance, environmental modification (blackout curtains, weighted blankets if recommended, predictable bedtime ritual).
- Toilet training is often delayed. Don't push. Work on interoception, comfort with the bathroom environment, and AAC for body signals.

## Social development

Watch for what your child enjoys, not what they should be doing.

- Solitary or parallel play is valid play. So is repetitive play, lining up, sorting, and special interest immersion.
- Joint attention may look different — sharing through handing objects, looking at the parent after engaging with something, parallel observation. It's there; it may not match neurotypical patterns.
- Friendships happen, sometimes with a single specific child, sometimes around a shared interest, sometimes in unusual structures (parallel play with intermittent contact). All of these are real friendships.
- Don't push neurotypical social benchmarks. Social skills training that drills "sharing," "turn-taking," and "eye contact" is teaching masking; the cost in childhood is depletion and identity damage.

What helps:
- Smaller groups, predictable settings, shared interests as the basis for connection
- Other autistic kids when accessible — autistic-to-autistic friendship often comes more easily
- Parental support without parental management — let them have the friendship they have, not the one you imagined
- Modeling that solo time is valid and that quietness isn't loneliness

## What to put down for now

For a parent in their first year or two of an early-childhood diagnosis or pre-diagnosis investigation:

- Long-term worry about adulthood. Future you will worry about future them. Today's child needs today's parent.
- The full library of reading. Pick two or three books and start.
- Optimization of every minute. Down time and play are developmental, not wasted.
- Comparison with allistic kids the same age. Not the same race; different track.
- Other people's opinions about your parenting. The relatives, the strangers in restaurants, the parents at the playground. Their input is not your responsibility.

## What to keep doing

- Saying yes to your child's interests
- Adapting environments to fit them
- Trusting your observations
- Building a small team of providers who actually help
- Remembering this is a child, not a project

The early childhood years are intense and they pass. The relationship you build now is what supports the rest.
