# Transition to Adulthood

The legal cliff at 18, the practical cliff that often arrives between 18 and 25, and the slow renegotiation of the parent-child relationship into the parent-adult-child relationship.

## What changes legally at 18

In the US (similar in many countries with variation in the specifics):

- The young adult is the legal decision-maker for medical, educational, financial, and personal matters
- Parents lose automatic access to medical records, school records (FERPA), and most financial information
- IEP rights transfer to the student (with some state variation)
- The young adult can sign contracts, vote, register for selective service (males), and is criminally prosecutable as an adult
- Healthcare proxies, financial powers of attorney, and other supports require explicit legal documents

This shift is real even when the young adult continues to live at home, depend on parental insurance, or want significant support. The legal default is independent adult.

## Guardianship — the question

Many parents are advised to seek guardianship of an autistic young adult, especially one with high support needs. This is a significant legal action with real consequences.

**What guardianship does:**
- Removes legal decision-making capacity from the young adult
- Transfers it to the guardian (often parent)
- Generally requires court process to obtain and to terminate
- Affects voting rights, contract rights, marriage rights, and ability to sign for medical care or housing in many jurisdictions

**The autistic adult community's strong position:** guardianship should be a last resort, not a first response. Many autistic adults who experienced guardianship report significant negative effects on autonomy, confidence, and life trajectory. Less restrictive alternatives often work as well or better.

**Less restrictive alternatives to consider first:**
- **Supported decision-making.** A formal arrangement where the young adult retains legal capacity but designates trusted people to help them make decisions. Now legally recognized in many US states. The autistic adult chooses their support network and the decisions they want help with.
- **Healthcare proxy / medical power of attorney.** The young adult signs a document designating someone to make medical decisions if they're unable to. Voluntary, revocable, doesn't require court action.
- **Financial power of attorney.** Similar, for financial matters. Can be limited to specific transactions or broad. Voluntary, revocable.
- **HIPAA release.** Allows a designated person access to medical information without removing the patient's own decision-making.
- **Representative payee for Social Security benefits.** Allows another person to manage benefits without affecting other rights.
- **Trustees for inherited or settlement money** without affecting personal autonomy.

**When guardianship may be appropriate:**
- The young adult is unable to communicate decisions in any consistent way and no AAC has unlocked communication
- There is significant risk of exploitation that less restrictive alternatives can't address
- The young adult themselves wants the guardianship
- Specific medical or legal situations require the legal authority

**What to do before pursuing guardianship:**
- Consult with attorneys experienced in disability rights law, not just elder law guardianship
- Explore supported decision-making and other less restrictive alternatives
- Talk with autistic adults about their experiences
- Consider time-limited guardianship if available, or domain-specific guardianship rather than full

If you proceed with guardianship, do it knowing what you're doing and why. Many parents who pursued full guardianship without considering alternatives later report regret.

## College

For autistic young adults pursuing college, the transition from K-12 supports to college supports is significant. See `autism-self-coach/references/school-and-university.md` for the student-driven version. Parents' role:

- Help the young adult research disability services at colleges they're considering
- Support them in registering with disability services and obtaining accommodations
- Help with logistics (orientation, dorm setup, healthcare transfer)
- Step back from academic and social management
- Be available without hovering
- Know when to step in (mental health crisis, safety, disability services failure) and when to let the young adult navigate

Common pitfalls:
- Continuing to do work the young adult should be learning to do (managing their schedule, contacting professors, communicating with disability services)
- Underestimating sensory and executive function load of the college environment
- Overestimating it — many autistic young adults do well in college with appropriate supports
- Pushing for college as the default when other paths fit better

## Work

Autistic adults work in every field. The path varies. Common patterns:

**Direct hire after college or training:** Standard pathway, often works well for autistic adults whose profile fits the field. The work itself may be easier than the college environment was.

**Vocational rehabilitation services (US):** Adult disability services available in every state. Helps with job search, training, accommodation. Quality varies widely. Worth applying to.

**Supported employment programs:** For young adults who need ongoing job coaching, accommodation negotiation, or social support at work. Different from sheltered workshops (which are increasingly recognized as exploitative).

**Self-employment:** Many autistic adults thrive as freelancers, consultants, artists, or small business owners. Allows control over environment, schedule, social load. Has its own demands (executive function, billing, taxes).

**Family business or supported integration:** Some families integrate the autistic young adult into family work or businesses with built-in accommodation.

**Project to project / part-time / nontraditional schedules:** Work that fits sensory and energy capacity rather than 40-hour standard.

The path that matters is the one that fits the specific person. Don't impose a vision.

## Independent living

Wide spectrum:

- Lives independently (apartment, house) with no daily support
- Lives independently with periodic check-ins (family, support staff)
- Lives in supported housing — small group home, supported apartment, intentional community
- Lives with family long-term, an arrangement that works for some autistic adults and is increasingly recognized as valid rather than failure
- Lives with family with explicit transition plan to greater independence
- Lives in specialized communities for autistic or otherwise neurodivergent adults

The decision factors:
- Capacity for daily-life executive function (cooking, cleaning, money, scheduling)
- Sensory needs and the environments that match them
- Social capacity and community connection
- Safety considerations
- Family preferences — both the young adult's and the parents'
- Resources (financial, available housing, support services)
- The young adult's own wishes

What helps:
- Trial periods rather than permanent commitments
- Skill-building during adolescence with real-world practice
- Connection to other autistic adults living independently
- Clear support agreements when family or paid support is involved
- Iteration — what works at 19 may not be what works at 25 and vice versa

## Healthcare transition

A major and often-overlooked challenge. From 18 onward:
- Pediatrician → adult primary care
- Pediatric specialists → adult specialists
- Insurance changes (parental insurance until 26 in US under ACA, then own coverage)
- Mental health provider transitions
- Medication management and prescription handling

What helps:
- Transition to adult providers gradually, ideally over a year or two
- Ensure the young adult has medical records, medication list, and provider contact info
- Healthcare proxy in place
- HIPAA release for parental access if mutually wanted
- The young adult learning to schedule, attend, and manage their own appointments

What doesn't:
- Sudden transition with no preparation
- Continuing to manage all healthcare without teaching
- Pediatric providers who refuse to graduate the patient

## Finances

Financial life is often a significant challenge for autistic adults — not because of intelligence or capability, but because of executive function, time-blindness, and sensory aversion to financial tasks.

What helps:
- Direct deposit and automatic bill pay
- Financial software with clear visualization
- A trusted person available for budget consultation, not management
- Specific support with taxes if needed
- ABLE accounts (US) for autistic adults receiving SSI/SSDI to save without losing benefits
- Special needs trusts where appropriate
- Representative payee for Social Security if useful

What doesn't:
- Parental control of all finances indefinitely
- Surprise expectations of full self-management at 18
- Generic financial literacy that doesn't account for executive function

## Benefits and services

For autistic adults who qualify, these become relevant at 18 or shortly after:

- **SSI (Supplemental Security Income)** — needs-based, for adults with disabilities and low income. Application is bureaucratic; approval rates vary.
- **SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance)** — work-history-based, for adults who became disabled after working long enough to qualify, or as adult-disabled-child of a parent on Social Security
- **Medicaid** — health insurance for low-income disabled adults
- **Vocational Rehabilitation** — state services for disabled adults seeking employment
- **State developmental disabilities services** — varies enormously by state, sometimes substantial supports, sometimes minimal
- **Section 8 housing vouchers** — long waitlists in most areas

Application processes are long and require documentation. Start early when possible.

## The relationship shift

The biggest work of this period for parents: shifting from the parent of a child to the parent of an adult.

What changes:
- Decisions are theirs unless they ask for input
- Their life choices are theirs to make, including ones you'd choose differently
- Your wisdom is available when requested, not imposed
- Their privacy is real and respected
- Disagreements are between adults, not between parent and child
- You stop being responsible for outcomes you can't control

What stays:
- Love
- Practical support when needed and welcome
- Backup when something goes wrong
- The relationship, in whatever form it takes between you

This shift is hard and often takes years. Many parents struggle to let go of management, especially when they've been heavily involved in their child's life because of the autism. Therapy for the parent helps. Connection with other parents who've been through it helps. Trust in the young adult helps most.

## When the young adult is struggling

Some autistic young adults hit a difficult patch in their early 20s — burnout from the cumulative cost of education, mental health crisis, employment difficulty, isolation, identity work that's slowed down their life trajectory.

What helps:
- Permission to be in a slow phase. Linear timelines aren't required.
- Reduced demands and judgment from family
- Mental health support if accessible
- Connection to autistic peers
- Practical support without taking over
- Time

What doesn't:
- Pressure to "launch"
- Comparison to peers
- Threat to withdraw support unless they perform
- Treating the difficult period as evidence the autism is "getting worse"

## What the relationship can become

At its best, the parent-autistic adult relationship is a real adult relationship. The parent has unique knowledge from the years of close work; the adult child has unique self-knowledge they've earned. They consult each other. They respect each other's lives. The autism is part of the picture without being the central organizing fact.

That's the goal of the years of advocacy, accommodation, and respect that came before. It's also the work of this period, intentionally chosen.
