# Post-Diagnosis Processing

The first weeks and months after a child's diagnosis. A common time for parents to be in the most intense emotional state and the most pressure to make decisions. This reference is about pacing — what to do now, what to put down, and how to think about the year ahead.

## The first 48 hours

Almost no important decisions need to be made in the first 48 hours. Most things you'll be told are urgent are not. Resist the pressure.

What's actually useful in the first 48 hours:
- Sit with the news. Don't perform anything.
- Tell whoever you need to tell to feel less alone — usually a partner, a close friend, possibly a sibling. Don't broadcast yet.
- Don't make decisions about therapy, school, or services yet. Those can wait.
- Permission to feel whatever you feel. Relief, grief, anger, confusion, vindication, fear, all of them, none of them, all in the same hour.
- Don't lecture yourself about how you "should" feel.
- Look at your child the way you did yesterday. Same kid. Now you have a frame for things you were already noticing.

## The first week

Things you can do in the first week without committing to anything:

**Read one thing — but only one.** A book by an autistic adult (Devon Price's *Unmasking Autism*, Barry Prizant's *Uniquely Human*) or a single article from a trusted source. Don't binge.

**Don't tell everyone yet.** The disclosure decisions about extended family, school, and the child themselves deserve thought. Default to private until you've thought about it.

**Don't sign anything new.** If a provider is pushing you toward immediate enrollment in a therapy program, that pressure is a signal to slow down, not to comply.

**Notice your child.** Same kid. Watch them with the new frame and see what becomes more legible.

**Sleep, eat, breathe.** The processing is real work and your nervous system is doing it.

## The first month

Decisions that may make sense in the first month:

- Telling the child, age-appropriately. See section below.
- Deciding which 1–2 trusted adults to tell first (a close friend, a family member, a therapist if you have one).
- Beginning to research providers, but not yet committing
- Joining a support community — autistic-led or autism-affirming, not generic. Online communities, local groups if available.
- Starting to think about school accommodations, but not necessarily activating them yet
- Reading 2–3 more things, deliberately chosen

Decisions that should usually wait beyond the first month:
- Major school placement changes
- Starting intensive therapy programs
- Family announcements
- Public posts
- Career changes or work modifications based on the diagnosis
- Decisions about siblings' lives based on the diagnosis

## Telling the child

Whether and how depends on age and the child.

**For young children (under 8):** Often a low-key, ongoing conversation rather than a single sit-down. "You know how some people see colors really intensely and other people don't notice as much? Brains are like that too. Yours notices a lot. That's called being autistic. Lots of people in our family probably are." Match to their interest level. Don't make it a big production unless they make it one.

**For elementary-age (8–12):** Often a more direct conversation when they're ready. Affirm clearly that being autistic is part of who they are, not something wrong, not something to fix. Connect to specific positives ("this is why you can focus so well on dinosaurs, this is why music feels so big to you") and specific accommodations ("this is why we're going to make some changes about how loud school feels to you"). Recommend an age-appropriate book or video by an autistic creator.

**For teens:** They likely have suspected for a while. The conversation is more like a discussion of the diagnostic confirmation. Hand off agency — they can decide who to tell, what to do with the information, how to relate to their autistic identity. Affirm that their self-knowledge has been correct.

**For older teens and young adults:** They're driving. Your role is to support what they want to do with the information.

What works across ages:
- Your tone matters more than the words. If you're treating it as a tragedy, they'll learn to. If you're treating it as part of who they are, they'll know that too.
- Connect them to autistic creators, characters, role models, communities. They should know they're not alone.
- Don't promise it'll be easy. Promise you'll be there.

What doesn't work:
- Hiding the diagnosis "until they're old enough." Most autistic adults raised with this approach later report it harmed them.
- Framing it as a battle, a fight, or something to overcome
- Performing positivity that doesn't match your real feelings
- Making the conversation about you

## Telling other people

Different people, different decisions:

**Co-parent or partner:** Should be in the loop from the start, ideally as a co-receiver of the diagnosis, not as a downstream notification.

**Other parent who didn't attend the evaluation:** Tell them as soon as practical. Not in front of the child. Allow space for their reaction.

**Siblings:** Tell when you've had time to think about how to frame it. See `references/family-dynamics.md`. Generally honest, age-appropriate, with attention to what the sibling needs to know about their own experience and not just about the diagnosed child.

**Extended family — grandparents, aunts, uncles:** Variable. Tell the supportive ones earlier. The unsupportive ones can wait, and may not need to be told at all if your relationship doesn't require it. You can disclose differently to different people.

**School:** Depends on whether you want services. If yes, formal disclosure with documentation through the IEP/504 process. If you want to wait on services, you can also wait on school disclosure.

**Friends:** As feels right. Often easier to tell one or two close friends and let it spread organically than to make announcements.

**Public — social media, broader community:** This is the child's information, not yours. Default to keeping it private. The child may want to be public later; let that be their choice.

**Healthcare providers:** Yes, going forward. Pediatrician, dentist, any specialist. They need to know to provide appropriate care.

## Processing your own feelings

A few things to expect, none of them required:

**Relief.** Often the first wave. The patterns you'd been noticing finally have a name. The advice that hadn't been working makes sense now.

**Grief.** For the imagined version of your child's life that's been rewritten. This is real and not a sign you don't love who they are. Many parents move through it; it doesn't have to be performed away.

**Guilt.** For the things you didn't know, the strategies that didn't help, the moments you didn't understand. Most of this guilt is misdirected — you didn't have the information; now you do; what matters is what comes next.

**Anger.** At providers who missed it, at family members who dismissed your concerns, at systems that punished your child for being autistic, at the larger culture that pathologizes neurodivergence. Often productive when channeled into advocacy.

**Fear.** About the future, services, school, money, your child's wellbeing as an adult, the marriage if your partner doesn't agree, the family if relatives are hostile. Real and worth attending to over time.

**Acceptance.** Eventually. Not absence of fear or grief, but integration of the diagnosis into the picture of your child without it being the central organizing fact.

What helps the processing:
- Talking with other parents of autistic kids — but specifically, parents who are at peace with the diagnosis. Communities focused on grief and fight can deepen your own.
- Reading by autistic adults
- Therapy if accessible
- Time
- Watching your child be themselves

What doesn't:
- Pushing yourself to skip stages
- Pretending you don't have the feelings you have
- Apologizing to the child for your processing
- Making the processing the child's problem
- Reading constantly without integration

## A common mistake

Many newly-diagnosed parents go into "fix it" mode. Sign up for everything, research constantly, optimize the schedule, deploy interventions, become the expert, manage the trajectory.

This is understandable and rarely helpful. The autistic child does not need an optimization project. They need:
- A parent who knows them
- An accommodating environment
- Time and space to grow at their own pace
- The relationship you already had, with the new understanding layered in

The biggest gift of the diagnosis is the framework that lets you parent more effectively, not the to-do list of services and protocols.

## What to keep doing

The things you were doing right before the diagnosis that you might be tempted to question:
- Following your instincts about your child
- Adapting to what works for them, even when it doesn't match generic advice
- Protecting them from environments that hurt them
- Saying yes to their interests
- Letting them be quiet, weird, intense, themselves

You probably weren't doing this perfectly. You were probably doing it more than you realize. Keep doing it.

## What to put down

Things you can let go of, at least for now:
- The plan you had for their life that the diagnosis disrupts
- Feeling like you should have known sooner
- The need to make a public statement
- The instinct to optimize
- Comparison with other kids
- The fight with relatives who don't understand yet

These can wait or may not be needed at all. The first months are about settling, not performing.

## A year out

If you're a year past the diagnosis, the texture is usually different. The acute feelings have softened. The accommodations are part of life. The kid is more themselves than ever, or you're seeing them more clearly. The scary projections have given way to actual lived experience.

Most parents at the year mark say something like "I didn't know how it would feel; turns out it feels like our regular life with better information." That's the goal.
