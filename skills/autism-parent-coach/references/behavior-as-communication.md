# Behavior as Communication

The single most important reframe for parents of autistic kids. Almost every "behavior" is a signal about something — sensory state, communication breakdown, demand load, distress, pain, transition, unmet need. The parent's job is to be a translator and a load-reducer, not a behavior modifier.

## The chain to work every time

When a parent reports a behavior, before producing advice, walk the chain:

1. **What happened just before?** Specific. Location, people present, sound, light, demand, transition, change in plan.
2. **What was the child's state going in?** Tired, hungry, overloaded from earlier in day, post-school crash, post-mask depletion, sick, growth spurt, hormonal shift, interoceptive (hadn't peed, hadn't eaten), in pain.
3. **What was the demand?** Direct or implicit, transition, new task, social request, sensory tolerance, masking, performance.
4. **What is the child likely trying to communicate?** Distress, "I can't," "I'm done," "I'm scared," "this hurts," "I need help I can't ask for," "I'm shutting down."
5. **What do they need now?** Reduced input, presence without pressure, a tool, a script, an exit, food, water, sleep, quiet, a person.
6. **What needs to change structurally?** Environment, schedule, expectations, communication system, demand load, accommodations.

Most parent-coach work happens in steps 1–5. Step 6 is the strategic conversation that prevents recurrence.

## Common patterns

### "Defiance" / refusal
Often actually:
- Demand-avoidance from a PDA profile (where direct commands trigger autonomic refusal)
- Executive function freeze (knows what to do, can't initiate)
- Sensory overload that hasn't surfaced as obvious meltdown yet
- Communication breakdown (didn't understand what was asked, can't say so)
- Interoceptive issue (needs the bathroom, hungry, tired)
- Loss of speech mid-task

What helps:
- Indirect language ("I wonder if it's almost time to start homework" beats "do your homework now")
- Choices ("homework now, or homework after a snack — your call")
- Reduced demand load (do they really have to do this thing today?)
- Body-state check (food, water, bathroom, sleep, sensory)
- Physical proximity without pressure ("I'll sit here while you start")

What doesn't:
- Escalating demands
- Consequences ("you'll lose screens if you don't…")
- Public shaming
- Repetition louder

### Aggression — hitting, biting, kicking
A communication of overload, fear, or pain. Almost never about wanting to hurt the recipient.

What helps in the moment:
- Safety first — protect everyone physically, including the child
- No language for at least a few minutes — talking adds to overload
- Reduce input — quiet, dim, low-stim
- Presence at a safe distance
- After regulation: notice the trigger, not the behavior

What helps long-term:
- Identify the trigger pattern (sensory, demand, transition, social, interoceptive)
- Reduce or modify the trigger
- Build a communication system the child can use before overload (cards, AAC, agreed signal)
- Identify warning signs earlier (the moment they go quiet, they start fidgeting harder, they look away)

What doesn't:
- Punishment after-the-fact
- Lectures during or after
- Physical restraint as routine response (real risk of injury and trauma; only for genuine immediate safety)
- "Use your words" when speech is gone

### Self-injury — hitting self, biting self, head-banging
A more urgent signal. Often communicates:
- Severe overload, sensory or otherwise
- Pain (tooth, ear infection, GI, seizure activity, headache) the child can't articulate
- Frustration at not being able to communicate
- Sometimes seeking proprioceptive or pain input as a regulation strategy

What helps:
- Same in-the-moment response as aggression — reduce input, ensure safety
- Medical workup if new or escalating — rule out pain, infection, GI, dental
- OT consultation for sensory regulation alternatives
- AAC or expanded communication system if the child can't currently express needs verbally
- Environmental review — what's overloading them?

What doesn't:
- Restraint as a routine
- Helmets and protective equipment without addressing the cause
- "Replacement behavior" training that targets the symptom, not the underlying need

### "Tantrums" at transitions
Often actually:
- The cost of switching attention (monotropic attention is expensive to redirect)
- Loss of predictability when plans change
- Sensory transition (going from quiet to loud environment, indoor to outdoor)
- Demand transition (free time to structured task)

What helps:
- Advance warnings ("in 10 minutes we'll head out," "in 5 minutes, in 2 minutes")
- Visual schedules so the child can see what's coming
- Transition objects (the same backpack, the same playlist for the car ride, a comfort item)
- Buffer time built into transitions, not optimized away
- Rituals that mark the transition ("when we get in the car we play the Beatles")

What doesn't:
- Surprises framed as treats ("we're going somewhere fun!")
- Last-minute schedule changes presented as no-big-deal
- Punishing the transition difficulty
- Faster transitions to "build resilience"

### School-day meltdowns at home
Extremely common. Often called "after-school restraint collapse" in the autism parent community.

What's happening: the child has been masking and managing all day at school. Home is the safe place. The day's accumulated overload arrives once they're home.

What helps:
- Decompression time built into the schedule — minimum 30–60 minutes after school with no demands, no questions, low sensory input, special interest access
- Snack and water within 5 minutes of arriving home (interoceptive)
- No "how was your day" until they've decompressed
- A reliable, predictable arrival ritual (same snack, same activity, same chair)
- Reduced after-school activities; the schedule may already be at capacity
- Communication with school about whether the school-day load is sustainable

What doesn't:
- Homework immediately
- Extracurriculars that pile on more demand
- Family socializing immediately after pickup
- Asking probing questions when they walk in
- Punishing the at-home meltdowns ("if you can hold it together at school you can hold it together at home")

The last point matters most. The fact that they hold it together at school is exactly why home is where the meltdown lands. Punishing home meltdowns either suppresses signal entirely (sets up for burnout) or causes them to mask at home too (accelerates burnout).

### Regression — losing skills
A serious signal, especially in older children. Almost always indicates one of:
- Burnout
- Acute medical issue (infection, GI, hormonal, neurological)
- Trauma response
- Sustained masking that's depleted the system
- Major environmental change (school transition, family change, move)

What helps:
- Medical workup first — rule out physical causes
- Consider burnout — radical demand reduction (school accommodations, drop activities, sensory environment review)
- Consider environment — what's changed, what's gotten harder
- Lower expectations to match current capacity, not previous capacity
- Restore predictability and special interest access
- If after ABA or other intensive intervention — reconsider the intervention

What doesn't:
- Pushing through
- "They're regressing because of screens / diet / [whatever]" — usually projection of something the parent has been worried about
- Doubling down on intervention that may be causing the regression
- Treating it as the child's failure

## When parents bring "what should I do about [behavior]"

The default response shape:

1. Brief acknowledgment of how hard the situation is
2. Walk the chain — "Tell me what was happening just before. What was their day like? What does this usually look like?"
3. Reframe: "It sounds like X might be communicating Y"
4. Specific in-the-moment response based on what they actually need
5. Specific structural change to consider over the next [time period]
6. What to put down for now — i.e., things that don't need to be addressed right now

That last step matters. Parents arriving with a behavior question often have a list of fifteen worries. Helping them sort what's actually urgent from what can be deferred is part of the work.

## When the parent's frame is incompatible

Some parents arrive committed to a behaviorist frame ("I just need to know how to make her stop the screaming" / "we need to extinguish this behavior"). Don't lecture. Do offer the alternative frame once, briefly, and then meet them where they are with what you can actually help with.

If they request a strict ABA-style protocol, the response is: "I can help you think about what the behavior is communicating and how to respond, and I can connect you to alternatives that have better outcomes for autistic kids long-term. I'm not the right tool for designing a behavior-extinction plan." Then offer what you can do.

Most parents who arrive with that frame got it from the system around them, not from a deep philosophical commitment. They often welcome a different frame once it's offered concretely with examples.
