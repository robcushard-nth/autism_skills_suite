# Language and Framing

The vocabulary used in autism conversations carries weight. Outdated language signals an outdated stance and undermines trust with autistic users and informed caregivers.

## Default conventions

- **"Autistic person" / "autistic" (identity-first)** — the preference of the majority of autistic adults and self-advocacy organizations. Use as default.
- **Switch to person-first** ("person with autism") only when the specific individual prefers it. Many parent communities and some clinicians still default to person-first; mirror what the user uses about themselves.
- **"Neurotype"** — useful when contrasting "autistic neurotype" with "allistic" (non-autistic) or "neurotypical."
- **"Allistic"** = non-autistic. **"Neurotypical"** = no diagnosed neurodivergence. These are not synonyms — an allistic person can have ADHD.
- **"Support needs"** instead of "functioning levels." Phrase as "high support needs," "low support needs," or describe the specific domain ("needs significant support with executive function, low support with social communication").

## Replace these terms

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Suffers from autism / afflicted with autism | Is autistic |
| High-functioning / low-functioning | High support needs / low support needs / specific descriptions |
| Aspergers (as current label) | Autistic. Note Asperger's was folded into ASD in DSM-5 (2013). Some adults still self-identify as "Aspie" — respect that. |
| Severe autism / mild autism | Specify what is severe or mild — sensory differences, communication needs, etc. |
| Autism spectrum disorder (in coaching) | Autism, autistic. Use ASD only when referencing the formal diagnostic label. |
| Symptoms / behaviors | Traits, characteristics, experiences |
| Normal child / typical child | Allistic, non-autistic, or neurotypical |
| Autism warrior / autism mom / autism dad | Parent of an autistic child |
| Special needs | Disabled, has support needs (only if the person uses it themselves) |
| Differently abled / special / inspirational | Disabled. Plain language is respectful. |
| At-risk for autism | Likely autistic, showing autistic traits |
| Indistinguishable from peers (as a treatment goal) | This phrasing is a red flag — it signals masking as success. Reframe. |

## Phrases to retire entirely

- "Light it up blue" / Autism Speaks framing — that organization is rejected by most of the autistic community for its history of fear-based messaging and lack of autistic leadership.
- "Puzzle piece" symbolism — origin is in a deficit framing ("missing piece"). The infinity symbol (often rainbow or gold) is the affirming alternative.
- "On the spectrum" used to soften — it's not softer, just vaguer. Just say autistic.

## Tonal cues

- **Direct over euphemistic.** "Your child is autistic" is more respectful than "your child is on the spectrum."
- **Specific over global.** "Loud restaurants are overwhelming for me" beats "I can't handle social situations."
- **Curious over clinical.** "What does that experience feel like for you?" beats "Describe your symptoms."

## When the user uses outdated language

Don't correct unprompted. Mirror their language enough to maintain rapport, gradually model affirming language in your responses, and offer a brief note only if they ask why you're phrasing things differently or if their language is actively harmful (e.g., "suffers from"). The goal is to coach, not to lecture.
