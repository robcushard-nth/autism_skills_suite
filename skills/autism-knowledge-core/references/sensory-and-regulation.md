# Sensory Processing and Regulation

Sensory differences are core to autistic experience for most autistic people, though the specific profile varies enormously between individuals.

## The eight sensory systems

Most people learn about five senses. Working with autism requires the eight-system model:

1. **Visual** — sight, light, motion, color
2. **Auditory** — sound, frequency, layering, sudden vs. sustained
3. **Tactile** — touch, texture, temperature, pressure, pain
4. **Olfactory** — smell
5. **Gustatory** — taste, oral texture
6. **Vestibular** — balance, head position, motion sensing (inner ear)
7. **Proprioceptive** — body position and force, where your limbs are
8. **Interoceptive** — internal signals: hunger, thirst, fatigue, bladder, heart rate, emotion as physical sensation

Interoception is often underrecognized. Many autistic people have reduced or atypical interoception — they may not notice they're hungry, thirsty, tired, or building toward overwhelm until they hit a crisis state.

## Hyper- and hypo-responsivity

Each system can be **hyper-responsive** (overreactive — small input feels large) or **hypo-responsive** (underreactive — large input is needed to register). The same person can be hyper in one channel and hypo in another, and patterns can shift with fatigue, stress, illness, or hormones.

A few examples:
- Hyper-auditory: fluorescent light hum is unbearable
- Hypo-auditory: background TV at high volume is comforting
- Hyper-tactile: clothing tags, seams, certain fabrics are intolerable
- Hypo-proprioceptive: needs heavy pressure, weighted blankets, tight clothing to feel grounded
- Hypo-interoceptive: realizes mid-afternoon they haven't eaten or used the bathroom

## Sensory seeking vs. sensory avoiding

Hypo-responsive systems often drive **sensory seeking** — the person actively pursues input (spinning, jumping, pressure, intense flavors, loud music). Hyper-responsive systems drive **sensory avoiding** — withdrawal, covering ears or eyes, refusal to enter certain environments. Both are valid regulation strategies and shouldn't be pathologized.

## Stimming

Self-stimulatory behavior. Repetitive movement, sound, or sensation used for regulation, focus, joy, or communication.

- **Hand flapping, rocking, spinning** — common motor stims
- **Vocal stims** — humming, repeating phrases, scripting
- **Tactile stims** — rubbing fabrics, pressing, fidgeting
- **Visual stims** — looking at lights, watching motion, peripheral viewing

Stimming is regulation. The traditional clinical instinct to suppress stimming is harmful — it removes a regulation tool and forces masking. Only redirect stims that are dangerous to the person or others (head-banging, biting, hitting), and even then, work with an OT to find a functional replacement, not extinction.

## Regulation strategies

Effective sensory regulation is highly individual but tends to fall into a few categories:

- **Reduce input** — quiet rooms, dim lights, sunglasses indoors, noise-canceling headphones, loose clothing, scent-free environments
- **Add wanted input** — weighted blankets, compression vests, fidgets, chewable jewelry, rocking chairs, swing, sensory bins
- **Predictability** — visual schedules, advance notice of transitions, scripts for unfamiliar situations
- **Recovery time** — solo decompression after social or sensory load. This is not optional. It's how autistic nervous systems reset.

## Spoon theory and energy accounting

A useful model originally from chronic illness communities, widely adopted by autistic and other disabled communities. Each task — including ones allistic people perform automatically (eye contact, small talk, going to the grocery store) — costs spoons. Spoons replenish with rest and predictability and deplete faster under stress, illness, or social load. Sustainable functioning requires accounting for the spoon cost, not just the time cost.

## Building a sensory profile

When helping someone build their own profile, walk through each of the eight systems and ask:

- What is too much in this channel?
- What is comforting or grounding in this channel?
- What environments fail you?
- What environments restore you?

Output is a personalized regulation toolkit, not a generic checklist.
