# Executive Function in Autistic Experience

Most autistic people have executive function differences. They overlap heavily with ADHD profiles — co-occurrence is common, and AuDHD (autism + ADHD together) is a recognized profile with its own dynamics.

## Domains of executive function

- **Task initiation** — starting a task even when you know what to do and want to do it
- **Working memory** — holding information in mind while using it
- **Planning and prioritization** — sequencing steps, choosing what to do first
- **Time perception ("time blindness")** — estimating duration, sensing passage of time, anticipating future
- **Cognitive flexibility / set-shifting** — pivoting between tasks, contexts, or rules
- **Emotional regulation** — modulating emotional intensity, recovering from upset
- **Self-monitoring** — noticing what you're doing while you're doing it, catching errors

A person can have intact ability in some domains and significant impairment in others. "Smart and capable" coexists with "couldn't get out of bed and start a familiar task" — that's not contradiction, it's executive function.

## Monotropism

Many autistic people experience **monotropic attention** — attention strongly drawn to a narrow set of interests at high intensity, with high cost to switch. This is different from the "scattered attention" of ADHD, though they can co-occur.

Implications:
- Special interests are not a problem to be managed. They are how monotropic attention works at its best — deep, sustained, generative engagement.
- Transitions are expensive, even between two desirable activities. Build in transition warnings, buffer time, and acknowledgment that the switch itself is the hard part.
- Interruptions are not just annoying — they can collapse the entire attention state and require significant recovery to rebuild.

## Demand avoidance and PDA

A subset of autistic people have a **Pathological Demand Avoidance** profile (also called Pervasive Drive for Autonomy in affirming framings). Direct demands trigger an autonomic anxiety response that makes compliance feel impossible — even for tasks the person genuinely wants to do. Standard parenting and management techniques (clear instructions, consequences, rewards) often backfire badly.

PDA-affirming approaches:
- Reduce direct commands
- Use indirect language ("I wonder if..." "It would help me if...")
- Offer real choice
- Collaborate rather than instruct
- Accept that strong autonomy needs are not defiance

## Inertia

A common autistic experience: difficulty starting *and* difficulty stopping. Once engaged in a task, switching out is hard; once disengaged, starting is hard. Both directions cost executive function.

Coaching implication: stopping rituals matter as much as starting rituals. Build wind-down routines, not just wake-up routines.

## Scaffolding strategies

What actually helps (per autistic adults' reports and the practitioner literature):

- **External structure** — visual schedules, written instructions, alarms, calendars, project boards. The brain doesn't need to hold it.
- **Body doubling** — working alongside another person (in person or virtually) makes initiation easier for many people.
- **Decision reduction** — fewer choices, pre-decided defaults (same lunch each day, same outfit pattern, fixed weekly routine).
- **Chunking** — breaking tasks into very small steps, smaller than seems necessary.
- **Time anchors** — "after coffee," "before the meeting," "during the show" beats "at 2 PM" for time-blind brains.
- **Environmental cues** — leaving the running shoes by the door, the medication on the counter, the laptop open to the file.
- **Capacity-based scheduling** — high-demand tasks during high-capacity windows; protect recovery time after.

## What doesn't help

- Lectures about willpower or discipline
- Productivity systems designed for allistic brains (rigid time-blocking with no flex)
- "Just push through" advice — this leads to burnout, not growth
- Pathologizing rest or low-output periods
