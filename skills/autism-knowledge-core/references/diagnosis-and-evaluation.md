# Diagnosis and Evaluation

The diagnostic landscape is uneven. What works in one country, region, age group, or insurance situation often doesn't work in another. Coaching here is mostly about helping users navigate, not about giving a single recommendation.

## Self-diagnosis

Treat as legitimate in coaching contexts. Most autistic adults who pursue formal evaluation already strongly suspect they're autistic — they're seeking confirmation, accommodations, or services, not insight.

Reasons people choose self-diagnosis without formal evaluation:
- Cost ($1,500–$5,000 USD typical for adult evaluation, often not covered)
- Lack of evaluators trained in adult, female-typical, or otherwise non-stereotypical presentations
- Risk of formal diagnosis affecting custody, immigration, military service, security clearance, insurance, employment in some sectors, or adoption eligibility
- Bad experiences with the medical system
- No concrete benefit if accommodations aren't needed

Self-diagnosis becomes complicated when:
- Insurance, school, or workplace accommodations require formal documentation
- The person wants access to specific services (occupational therapy, speech-language therapy, AAC, ABA — though see knowledge core stance on that, social skills programs)
- A child is involved (formal diagnosis usually opens doors to school services in most countries)

## What evaluation looks like for adults

A thorough adult evaluation typically includes:
- Clinical interview, often multi-session
- ADOS-2 (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule) — though it was designed primarily for children and has known limitations for adults, women, and people who mask
- ADI-R (Autism Diagnostic Interview-Revised) — historical interview, often involves a parent or longtime family member
- Cognitive testing (WAIS or similar)
- Adaptive functioning measures
- Screening for co-occurring conditions

Quality varies hugely. A bad evaluator can produce a confidently wrong "no autism" outcome by relying on stereotype matching (eye contact, surface social skills) instead of probing the actual diagnostic criteria. Users seeking evaluation should look for evaluators with explicit experience in adult, female, masked, or 2e (twice-exceptional) presentations.

## What evaluation looks like for children

Process varies by country and system. In the US, two main paths:

1. **Medical/clinical diagnosis** — through a developmental pediatrician, child psychiatrist, or licensed psychologist. Required for medical insurance to cover therapies. Often a long waitlist (6–18 months in many regions).
2. **Educational evaluation** — through the public school system. Required for IEP services. Uses educational eligibility criteria, which differ from medical diagnostic criteria. A child can have one and not the other.

Many families pursue both because they cover different supports.

## Late diagnosis in adults

A growing population. Common drivers:
- A child's evaluation reveals familial patterns
- Burnout in midlife when masking strategies stop working
- Reading or community discovery (autistic content online, autistic friends, books like *Unmasking Autism*)
- A partner's observation
- ADHD diagnosis that reveals adjacent autism

Late diagnosis often produces a complicated emotional arc: relief, grief for the misunderstood years, anger at past systems, identity reconstruction, sometimes a phase of strong autistic identification followed by a more integrated stance. All of this is normal. Coaching should not rush the user through any phase.

## Masked / female-typical / underrepresented presentations

The diagnostic literature historically captured a narrow stereotype: white boys, externalized behaviors, classic interests (trains, dinosaurs), obvious social atypicality. This produced massive underdiagnosis in:

- Girls and women — internalized presentation, social mimicry, masking, special interests in socially acceptable areas (horses, books, specific people)
- Adults who learned to mask in childhood
- Black and brown autistic people, who face both diagnostic bias and disproportionate misdiagnosis as conduct disorder, ODD, or other behavioral diagnoses
- 2e (twice-exceptional) — autistic + intellectually gifted, where verbal ability masks support needs
- Autistic people with PDA profiles
- Gender-diverse autistic people (autistic and trans/nonbinary co-occurrence is well above population rate)

When working with users from these populations, take seriously that prior "no autism" verdicts may have been wrong, and that current self-recognition deserves respect.

## Coaching implication

- Don't gatekeep autistic identity based on whether the user has a formal diagnosis.
- Don't push users toward formal evaluation if they've considered the cost/benefit and chose otherwise.
- Do help them weigh the decision when they're actively considering it.
- Do flag when formal evaluation is functionally required for what they want to access.
