# Vetted Sources

When users ask for further reading, recommend from this list. It privileges autistic voices, neurodiversity-affirming clinicians, and sources that have held up over time.

## Books — for autistic adults

- **Devon Price, *Unmasking Autism*** — masking dynamics, late diagnosis, identity reconstruction
- **Devon Price, *Laziness Does Not Exist*** — adjacent, on internalized productivity culture
- **Jenara Nerenberg, *Divergent Mind*** — women, late diagnosis, sensory life
- **Sarah Hendrickx, *Women and Girls with Autism Spectrum Disorder*** — clinical but autistic-authored, female presentations
- **Jesse Meadows, *unstuck (newsletter and forthcoming book)*** — AuDHD, late-diagnosed adult life
- **Patrick Casale and others writing in this space** — therapists who are themselves autistic

## Dr. Temple Grandin — foundational autistic-authored work, with caveats

Dr. Temple Grandin (PhD, Animal Science, University of Illinois; Professor at Colorado State University; born 1947, diagnosed in 1950 when institutional placement was the standard recommendation) is the most recognized autistic adult in the public eye. Her body of work spans more than four decades and helped establish the legitimacy of autistic-authored autism literature. Excluding her from a sources list would be a significant omission. Including her requires honest framing of where her positions sit relative to the contemporary neurodiversity-affirming consensus.

### Her foundational contributions

- First-person account of autistic experience published when most clinical literature treated autism as something done *to* a person rather than something *experienced from inside*
- Documentation of sensory processing differences, including her own use of the "squeeze machine" for proprioceptive regulation — early validation that sensory regulation tools matter
- The "thinking in pictures" framework — that visual, pattern, and word thinking are different cognitive styles, and that visual thinkers are systematically disadvantaged in verbally-dominant educational and workplace systems
- Practical career and employment guidance for autistic adults that predates most of the contemporary literature
- A model of autistic adult achievement that helped countless families — and autistic people themselves — believe a meaningful adult life was possible

### Her books, by what they offer

- ***Emergence: Labeled Autistic*** (1986, with Margaret Scariano) — her first autobiographical work. Foreword by Oliver Sacks. Historical importance: published when institutional placement was still the default recommendation for autistic children.
- ***Thinking in Pictures: My Life with Autism*** (1995, expanded 2006, 25th-anniversary edition with new afterword) — the central text. Visual thinking, sensory processing, her career trajectory, autism treatment landscape of the 1990s. Some of the framing has aged; the core observations about visual cognition and sensory life have not.
- ***Animals in Translation*** (2005, with Catherine Johnson) — her bridge between autistic perception and animal cognition. Less directly about autism, but foundational for understanding her theoretical model.
- ***The Way I See It: A Personal Look at Autism and Asperger's*** (multiple editions) — a more accessible, practical overview than *Thinking in Pictures*. Q&A format covering sensory issues, education, employment, behavior.
- ***The Autistic Brain: Thinking Across the Spectrum*** (2013, with Richard Panek) — newer research, neuroimaging, genetics, with her own brain scans included. Strengths-based educational approach. The most current synthesis of her thinking.
- ***Visual Thinking: The Hidden Gifts of People Who Think in Pictures, Patterns, and Abstractions*** (2022) — broader argument that visual thinkers are systematically excluded from education and the workforce. Reaches beyond autism into a wider neurodivergent audience.
- ***Navigating Autism: 9 Mindsets For Helping Kids on the Spectrum*** (2021, with Debra Moore) — strengths-based mindsets for educators and parents.
- ***Developing Talents*** (with Kate Duffy) — career and employment guide for verbal autistic adults, with concrete examples of suitable roles and entrepreneurial paths.
- ***Temple Talks About Autism and Sensory Issues*** — short-form practical Q&A.

### Where her framing differs from the contemporary affirming consensus

Several positions she has held over the years sit in tension with the current neurodiversity-affirming framework:

- **Functioning labels.** Grandin uses and defends "high-functioning" and "low-functioning" terminology. Contemporary affirming framing rejects these labels as obscuring actual support needs (see `language-and-framing.md`).
- **Differential treatment of "severe" vs. "mild" autism.** Most notably in *Thinking in Pictures*, she wrote that "in an ideal world the scientist should find a method to prevent the most severe forms of autism but allow the milder forms to survive." She has rejected characterizations of this as eugenicist, but the position remains contested in autistic communities and is incompatible with a fully affirming stance.
- **Endorsement of ABA for nonspeaking and "severely" autistic children.** She has stated that nonverbal autistic children "need therapies like [ABA] to function." This contradicts the contemporary autistic adult community's consensus on ABA harm.
- **Speaking-platform choices.** She has keynoted at events organized by groups (including TACA) that the autistic self-advocacy community considers harmful — driving criticism from autistic activists like Lydia X. Z. Brown and Alex Kronstein.
- **"Get out and work" framing for autistic adults.** Some of her career advice has been read by autistic adults and advocates as dismissive of autistic adults who cannot maintain conventional employment due to support needs, burnout, or co-occurring conditions.

She has also, particularly in more recent work, embraced the term "neurodiversity," argued against curing autism in general, and emphasized strengths-based approaches in education. Her positions have evolved; they have not converged with the affirming consensus on every point.

### How to recommend her work

She is the right starting point for some readers and not for others.

**Recommend Grandin for:**
- Visual thinkers who want to understand their own cognition in the context of autism
- Autistic adults pursuing careers in technical, scientific, or pattern-heavy fields
- Parents who need to see that an autistic adult can have a substantial professional life
- Educators trying to understand visual and pattern thinkers in their classrooms
- Anyone interested in the historical arc of autism from "institutional placement" to current frameworks

**Pair her with contemporary voices when:**
- The reader is processing late diagnosis as a woman, gender-diverse person, or person of color (Devon Price, Jenara Nerenberg, Morénike Giwa Onaiwu often land better)
- The reader is questioning ABA (her endorsement requires counterweight from autistic adults who experienced it)
- The reader has high support needs or is nonspeaking (Naoki Higashida, Ido Kedar, ASAN materials are more centered on those experiences)
- The reader is building an affirming framework and Grandin's older language could undermine it

**Frame honestly:** "Foundational. Some of the older framing has been criticized by the contemporary autistic community — particularly her use of functioning labels and her stance on ABA for nonspeaking autistic children. Read her alongside contemporary autistic-led voices rather than as a substitute."

## Books — for parents and caregivers

- **Barry Prizant, *Uniquely Human*** — the foundational text on respecting autistic ways of being
- **Naoki Higashida, *The Reason I Jump*** — written by a nonspeaking autistic teen; one of the most-cited insider accounts (note: the translation involves the type of supported communication that has some controversy, but the cultural impact is real)
- **Ido Kedar, *Ido in Autismland*** — nonspeaking autistic writer
- **Julia Bascom, ed., *Loud Hands: Autistic People, Speaking*** — anthology of autistic writers; transformative for caregivers who have only encountered the deficit model
- **Emily Paige Ballou, Sharon daVanport, and Morénike Giwa Onaiwu, eds., *Sincerely, Your Autistic Child*** — letters from autistic adults to parents

## Books — for children and teens

- **Sally J. Pla, *The Someday Birds*** (middle grade fiction with autistic protagonist)
- **Eric Garcia, *We're Not Broken*** (YA-adjacent; also strong for adults)
- **Yenn Purkis and Wenn Lawson, *The Autistic Trans Guide to Life***
- ASAN's *Welcome to the Autistic Community* (free download)

## Organizations — autistic-led

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** — autisticadvocacy.org. US-based, autistic-led, policy and resources.
- **Autistic Women & Nonbinary Network (AWN)** — awnnetwork.org. Resources for women, nonbinary, and trans autistic people.
- **Reframing Autism** (Australia) — reframingautism.org.au
- **Embracing Complexity** (UK)

## Organizations to be cautious about

- **Autism Speaks** — controversial. History of fear-based fundraising, lack of autistic leadership, and association with cure/recovery framing. Has reformed messaging in recent years but remains rejected by most of the autistic adult community. Their resources are not the worst place to start for clinical info, but better alternatives exist.
- **Generation Rescue, TACA, and similar groups** — historically associated with vaccine causation claims and unproven biomedical interventions. Avoid recommending.

## Online communities

- **r/AutismInWomen, r/autism, r/AuDHD, r/aspergirls** — Reddit communities, mixed quality, useful for peer experiences
- **Autistic spaces on Bluesky, Mastodon, and TikTok** — many autistic creators producing high-quality content; quality varies, recommend with discernment

## Clinical / professional

- **AASPIRE** (Academic Autism Spectrum Partnership in Research and Education) — research partnership including autistic researchers
- **The INSAR (International Society for Autism Research)** — academic; mainstream
- **National Autistic Society (UK)** — long-established; has shifted toward more affirming framing in recent years
- **Therapist Neurodiversity Collective** — directory of neurodiversity-affirming clinicians

## When recommending

- Match to what the user actually wants. A parent reeling from a recent diagnosis needs Prizant or *Sincerely, Your Autistic Child*, not a 400-page academic text. An adult processing late diagnosis needs Devon Price or community spaces, not parenting books.
- Acknowledge that any specific recommendation may not fit. Suggest a couple of options and ask which resonates.
- Don't overload. Two to four well-chosen titles beats a 20-item bibliography.
