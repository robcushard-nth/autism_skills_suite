# Meltdown vs. Shutdown vs. Burnout

These three states get conflated. The response that helps each is different. Misreading the state leads to interventions that make it worse.

## Meltdown

**What it is:** An involuntary nervous system response to overwhelm — sensory, emotional, cognitive, or social load exceeds the system's capacity to process. Not a tantrum. Not manipulation. Not chosen.

**What it looks like (varies enormously by person):**
- Crying, screaming, rapid speech, or loss of speech
- Physical movement: pacing, hitting, throwing, running
- In children, often misread as defiance or "behavior"
- In adults, often happens in private after masking through the trigger
- May be preceded by escalating signs the person tried to manage and couldn't

**What helps during a meltdown:**
- Reduce input. Quiet, dim, low-stimulation space.
- Reduce demands. Stop talking at them. Stop asking questions. Stop trying to fix.
- Physical safety only — not behavioral correction.
- If the person uses pressure, weighted blankets, or a specific object for regulation, offer those.
- Presence without pressure. "I'm here when you need me" and then actually be quiet.

**What does not help:**
- Reasoning, lecturing, or asking "what's wrong"
- Eye contact requirements
- Touch unless the person explicitly seeks it
- Consequences or discipline (the meltdown is not chosen behavior)
- An audience

**After a meltdown:**
- Recovery time is non-negotiable. Often hours; sometimes a day or more.
- No post-mortem until the person is fully regulated and asks for it.
- When discussing later, focus on the trigger and the warning signs, not the meltdown itself.

## Shutdown

**What it is:** The other direction of nervous system overwhelm. Instead of activation, the system goes offline. Often less visible than a meltdown, often more dangerous because it gets missed.

**What it looks like:**
- Loss of speech (selective mutism in the moment)
- Flat affect, unresponsive, "checked out"
- Inability to move or initiate any task
- Curling up, hiding, going to bed mid-day
- In meetings or classrooms, may look like the person is just quiet or compliant — they're actually unreachable
- Sometimes mistaken for laziness, sulking, or depression

**What helps during a shutdown:**
- Same as meltdown: reduce input, reduce demands, presence without pressure.
- Lower the bar dramatically. Not "what should you be doing right now" but "can you drink some water."
- Accept that productivity is gone. Don't try to coach through it.
- If the person nonverbal in shutdown, written communication (text, notes, AAC) sometimes still works when speech doesn't.

**What does not help:**
- "Snap out of it"
- Asking the person to explain what's wrong (they often can't access it)
- Treating it as a discipline issue or laziness

## Autistic Burnout

**What it is:** A distinct state, not the same as occupational burnout, depression, or a long meltdown. Caused by sustained overload — usually chronic masking, unaccommodated environments, life transitions, or all three. Can last weeks, months, or years.

**What it looks like:**
- Loss of skills the person previously had (driving, cooking, basic self-care, speech, executive function)
- Dramatically increased sensory sensitivity
- Inability to mask, even when masking would be safer
- Exhaustion that doesn't resolve with normal rest
- Sometimes mistaken for depression, but the treatment for depression doesn't fix it

**What helps:**
- **Radical reduction of demands.** This is not negotiable. The system needs a real off-ramp.
- Environmental modification — sensory load down, predictability up, social demands down.
- Permission to drop masking, special interests honored, stimming unrestrained.
- Time. Burnout recovery is measured in months, not days.
- Re-evaluating the conditions that caused it. Returning to the same load will produce the same outcome.

**What does not help:**
- "Pushing through"
- Standard antidepressant-only treatment without environmental change
- Productivity advice
- Encouragement to "get back to normal"
- Treating it as a mental illness episode rather than a structural overload state

## Quick differential

| Signal | Meltdown | Shutdown | Burnout |
|---|---|---|---|
| Onset | Acute, minutes to hours of buildup | Acute, can be sudden | Gradual, weeks to months |
| Direction | Activation, outward | Withdrawal, inward | Sustained depletion |
| Speech | Often loud, may be lost | Often lost | Often reduced |
| Duration | Minutes to hours, then recovery | Minutes to hours, sometimes longer | Weeks to years |
| Triggered by | Acute overload | Acute overload | Chronic overload |
| Helps | Reduce input, presence | Reduce input, presence | Sustained reduction of all demands |

A person can experience all three over the course of a hard period. A burnout state increases the likelihood of meltdowns and shutdowns from smaller triggers because the buffer is gone.

## Coaching implication

Always ask which state the person is describing or witnessing. The person in front of you knows their pattern better than any framework. Use the differential as a question prompt, not a diagnosis.
