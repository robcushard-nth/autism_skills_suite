# Co-occurring Conditions

Autism rarely travels alone. The conditions that frequently co-occur shape what coaching needs to address — and missing them produces incomplete or wrong advice.

## ADHD

**Co-occurrence rate:** Studies estimate 30–80% of autistic people meet criteria for ADHD, depending on the population studied. The DSM only allowed dual diagnosis starting in 2013.

**The combined profile (often called AuDHD):**
- Internal conflict between novelty-seeking (ADHD) and routine-seeking (autism)
- Monotropic deep dives + rapid topic switching
- Time blindness compounded by autistic time perception differences
- Hyperfocus that locks out basic needs (food, water, bathroom, sleep)
- Strong sensory sensitivity + sensory seeking simultaneously

**Coaching implication:** Don't apply pure-autism strategies (rigid routines) or pure-ADHD strategies (constant novelty) — the combined profile needs both, with switches between them.

## Anxiety disorders

**Co-occurrence rate:** Approximately 40–50%, possibly higher.

**Distinctions worth making:**
- **Generalized anxiety** — pervasive worry, often about social and sensory unpredictability
- **Social anxiety** — overlaps with autism but is not the same. Social anxiety responds to exposure; autistic social difficulty doesn't, because the underlying processing difference is structural.
- **OCD** — distinct from autistic routines. OCD rituals are ego-dystonic (the person doesn't want to do them, feels driven by intrusive thoughts). Autistic routines are typically ego-syntonic (the person wants the routine; it provides stability and joy). Treating autistic routines as OCD is a common misdiagnosis.

## Depression

Common, especially in late-diagnosed adults and in autistic people with chronic masking burden. Risk factors include:
- Sustained masking
- Social isolation
- Bullying history
- Lack of accommodations
- Internalized stigma
- Burnout

Treatment differs from neurotypical depression in important ways. Standard CBT can require modification — abstract reframing exercises don't always land the same way. Autistic-affirming therapists are scarce but worth searching for.

## Alexithymia

Difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions. Roughly 50% of autistic people have meaningful alexithymia. Important for coaching:

- "How do you feel?" is often the wrong question — the user may genuinely not know
- Body-based questions work better: "Where in your body do you feel that?" "Is your chest tight, or your throat?"
- Emotion vocabulary lists, mood meters, and feelings wheels are scaffolding tools, not deficits
- Don't push for emotional disclosure the person can't access

## Interoceptive differences

Often underrecognized. Many autistic people have reduced awareness of internal signals:
- May not register hunger, thirst, fatigue, or need to urinate until extreme
- May not recognize emotional states until they manifest physically (panic attack, sudden tears, meltdown)
- May not notice illness onset

Coaching implication: external scaffolding for body care (timed water, scheduled meals, hydration tracking, "if it's been 3 hours, pee") often matters more than willpower or awareness training.

## GI issues

Gastrointestinal differences are well-documented in autistic populations — chronic constipation, IBS, food sensitivities, reflux. Sometimes mistakenly attributed to "autism behavior" when the actual issue is physical pain. Always rule out medical causes for sudden behavior changes, especially in nonspeaking individuals.

## Eating differences

ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) is significantly more common in autistic populations. Driven by sensory sensitivity (texture, temperature, smell), interoceptive differences (not registering hunger), and routine preference (same foods feel safe). This is not picky eating to be powered through. Restrictive interventions risk worsening it.

## Hypermobility / EDS / hEDS

Joint hypermobility and Ehlers-Danlos syndromes co-occur with autism at significantly elevated rates. Worth screening for if the user mentions chronic joint pain, fatigue, dislocations, or skin changes. Routes to: rheumatology, geneticist familiar with EDS, physical therapy.

## PMDD and hormonal sensitivity

Many autistic women, trans men, and AFAB nonbinary individuals report severe premenstrual dysphoric disorder. Sensory sensitivity often spikes premenstrually. Cyclical patterns matter for self-knowledge and accommodation planning.

## Trauma and PTSD

Autistic people experience disproportionate rates of trauma — bullying, abuse, restraint, ABA experiences, medical trauma, sexual violence (especially for autistic women and gender-diverse autistic people). Always hold open the possibility that what looks like an autism trait is actually a trauma response, and that both can be true.

## Coaching implication overall

When something doesn't fit cleanly into "autism," consider whether a co-occurring condition is doing the work. Don't insist on autism-only framing if a different lens (ADHD, anxiety, trauma, PMDD, hypermobility) actually fits the user's experience better.
